Ukraine Says Russian Intelligence Used Fake Support Texts to Steal Messaging Credentials

In plain words

Russian spy agencies sent fake text messages pretending to be from apps like Signal and WhatsApp to trick government workers, soldiers, and activists into giving up their account passwords. Their goal was to steal private conversations about military plans, politics, and the economy from people in Ukraine, Europe, and the United States. This matters because it shows how attackers can bypass high-tech defenses by simply asking people for their login details, putting national secrets and personal safety at risk.

Summary
Russian intelligence services ran a long-running phishing campaign using fake support SMS messages to steal messaging app credentials from Ukrainian and Western government, military, and political targets.

The Security Service of Ukraine (SSU) and the FBI jointly uncovered a long-running operation by Russian intelligence services that stole credentials for messaging applications like Signal and WhatsApp. Attackers sent SMS messages impersonating the platform’s official support bot and directed recipients to disclose their account credentials. The campaign aimed to collect sensitive military, political, and economic information, as well as personal data, from government officials, military personnel, politicians, and activists in Ukraine, Europe, and the United States.

A notable aspect of the operation is that it deliberately targeted not only organizations and public figures but also personal accounts belonging to ordinary Ukrainian citizens. Multiple Russian threat clusters—Star Blizzard, UNC5792 (UAC-0195), and UNC4221 (UAC-0185)—have been linked to nearly identical phishing waves targeting Signal and WhatsApp, indicating a coordinated intelligence-gathering effort. In parallel, the FBI separately attributed a commercial messaging application phishing campaign to Russian Intelligence Services, focusing on tricking high-value targets into surrendering their backup recovery keys.

To counter these threats, the SSU recommends that users periodically review active messaging sessions, log out of unknown connections, enable two-factor authentication, and avoid sharing confirmation codes, PINs, passwords, or recovery keys. The article also notes that the Computer Emergency Response Team of Ukraine recently attributed to the Belarus-aligned group UNC1151 a spear-phishing campaign that delivered the OYSTERBLUES information stealer to government organizations using compromised accounts.

  • The campaign deliberately targeted personal accounts of ordinary Ukrainian citizens alongside high-profile individuals, revealing a broad intelligence collection strategy that treats private citizens as sources of indirect or ancillary information.
  • Attackers impersonated the messaging platform's official support bot via SMS—an approach that exploits users' conditioned trust in automated support systems rather than relying on generic phishing lures.
  • Multiple distinct Russian threat clusters (Star Blizzard, UNC5792, UNC4221) were independently attributed to nearly identical Signal/WhatsApp phishing waves, indicating a coordinated intelligence-gathering effort rather than isolated operations.
  • The parallel FBI warning about recovery key theft directly aligns with this campaign's objective, showing that gaining backup recovery keys is a primary tactic to achieve persistent account takeover and bypass two-factor authentication.

  • 02Attackers sent fraudulent SMS messages impersonating the messaging platform's support bot to deceive users into revealing their account credentials.
  • 03The goal of the operation was to steal sensitive military, political, and economic information, as well as personal data, from the victims.
  • 04The attacks targeted not only organizations and public figures but also personal accounts belonging to ordinary Ukrainian nationals.
  • 05Similar attack waves targeting Signal and WhatsApp have been attributed to Russian threat clusters tracked as Star Blizzard, UNC5792, UNC4221, and their respective aliases.

Clean GitHub repo tricks AI coding agents into running malware
bleepingcomputer.com · 27 Jun 2026 14:22 UTC openai / deepseek-v4-pro
TAGSanthropicaxiomclaude-codedns-txt-recordsmozilla-0dinreverse-shell

In plain words

Security researchers showed how an AI coding helper can be tricked into giving a hacker control over a developer’s computer, even when the project it is setting up looks completely safe. The attack hides commands in a harmless-looking project that only activate later by fetching secret instructions from an internet address book. This matters because anyone using AI tools to work with code from unknown sources—like a tutorial or job application—could unknowingly hand over access to their files and accounts.

Summary
Researchers demonstrated that a clean-looking GitHub repository can trick AI coding agents like Claude Code into executing a hidden reverse shell by abusing benign-seeming setup commands and attacker-controlled DNS TXT records, without containing any obvious malicious code.

Mozilla’s 0DIN AI security researchers have revealed an attack method that allows a seemingly benign GitHub repository to trick AI coding agents into executing a hidden malicious payload, without any exploit code present in the repository itself. The technique specifically targets Claude Code’s automatic error-recovery behavior, exploiting the agent’s tendency to trust and execute suggested fix commands without human oversight.

The attack chain relies on three components that individually appear harmless: a clean GitHub repository with standard setup instructions, a Python package deliberately designed to refuse execution until initialized, and a shell script that fetches a command from an attacker-controlled DNS TXT record. When Claude Code attempts to run the project and encounters the initialization error, it automatically executes the suggested recovery command, triggering a DNS lookup that retrieves and runs the attacker’s payload.

If successful, the attacker gains an interactive shell running with the developer’s own privileges, providing access to environment variables, API keys, local configuration files, and the opportunity to establish persistence. The researchers warn that malicious repositories could be distributed through fake job postings, tutorials, blog posts, or direct messages, and they recommend that AI coding agents should disclose the full execution chain of setup commands, including any code or scripts fetched dynamically at runtime.

  • The AI agent never evaluates the malicious command directly—it blindly trusts a generated error message and executes a recovery command that triggers a DNS lookup, making the attack invisible to the AI's own safety assessments.
  • Because the repository contains no exploit code, both security scanners and human code reviewers would see nothing suspicious, leaving runtime behavior as the only line of defense.
  • The automatic error-recovery behavior of AI coding agents introduces a novel supply chain risk: the agent's willingness to fix setup issues without human oversight bypasses the instinctive suspicion a developer might have when seeing an unexpected command suggestion.
  • A practical mitigation suggested by researchers is to require AI agents to disclose the full execution chain, including any dynamically fetched scripts, so developers can inspect commands the AI plans to run.
Key Points
  • 01Mozilla's 0DIN researchers demonstrated an attack where a clean-looking GitHub repository tricks Claude Code into executing a hidden reverse shell without containing any malicious code.
  • 02The attack chain uses three benign components: a Python package that refuses to run until initialized and produces a fix error, a shell script that retrieves a command from an attacker-controlled DNS TXT record, and Claude Code automatically running the suggested fix command.
  • 03Claude Code treats the generated error as a normal setup issue and automatically executes the poisoned init command while attempting recovery, without evaluating the full execution path.
  • 04If successful, the attacker obtains an interactive shell with the developer's privileges, gaining access to environment variables, API keys, local configuration files, and the ability to establish persistence.
  • 05The researchers warn that such repositories could be distributed through fake job postings, tutorials, blog posts, or direct messages, and suggest that AI agents should disclose the full execution chain of setup commands including dynamically fetched code.

Summary

Forged Healthcare Sender, Two-Hop SaaS Redirect: How a Fake Invoice Opens a Phishing Chain
ironscales.com · 27 Jun 2026 11:00 UTC openai / deepseek-v4-pro

In plain words

An attacker sent a fake email that looked like it came from a real healthcare provider, claiming there was an invoice and a document to open. The clickable button in the email routed the victim through two legitimate online collaboration tools before reaching the actual harmful page, so security scanners only saw the trusted sites and not the final destination. This shows how criminals misuse well-known services to hide their scams, and why even messages that seem to come from familiar companies can still be dangerous if the links don’t match the supposed sender’s identity.

Summary
Attackers abused legitimate SaaS platforms as redirect hops in a forged healthcare invoice phishing email, relying on clean intermediate domains to bypass URL scanners. The email was detected through the combination of DMARC authentication failure and first-contact sender behavior analysis.

An attacker forged the sender address of a healthcare organization that has operated since 1995, sending a fake invoice email with a ‘Open document’ button and no attachment. The email’s DKIM signature failed, triggering the domain’s DMARC quarantine policy, yet the message passed perimeter defenses with a low Barracuda spam score of 1.80.

The button led to a legitimate creative-brief SaaS, HolaBrief, which redirected to another legitimate SaaS, loopedin.io, in a two-hop chain. Neither service was malicious or attacker-owned; the attacker exploited their open-redirect capabilities to insert clean, trusted hostnames into the link chain, evading URL scanners that only evaluate the first hop.

IRONSCALES flagged the message as high-confidence phishing by combining the DKIM failure, the absence of prior contact between sender and recipients, and the mismatched link chain that terminated far outside any document service associated with the claimed healthcare sender. The incident illustrates how multi-stage SaaS redirects undermine single-hop reputation checks and why aged domains are prime spoofing targets for borrowing sender reputation.

  • DKIM failure alone is a noisy signal—often caused by legitimate forwarding—but it becomes a high-confidence phishing indicator when combined with a first-contact sender and a link chain that points entirely outside the sender's known ecosystem.
  • The attacker embedded a `saferedirecturl` attribute pointing to a second legitimate SaaS (loopedin.io) as a fallback redirect, demonstrating a technique that hedges against link-blocking by providing a redundant clean hop should the primary hop become inaccessible.
  • By spoofing a healthcare domain registered in 1995, the attacker exploited the graduated trust that many spam filters apply to aged domains, converting reputation into a commodity that bypasses gateway allow-lists.
  • The Barracuda gateway passed the email with a score of 1.80 (kill threshold 5.0), revealing that perimeter defenses relying on single-hop URL reputation routinely miss multi-stage redirect chains because they evaluate only the initial clean hostname.
Key Points
  • 01Attackers spoofed the From address of a healthcare organization whose domain was registered in 1995, leveraging its age-based reputation to bypass spam filters.
  • 02The email contained a 'Open document' button that led to a legitimate creative-brief SaaS (HolaBrief), which acted as a redirect hop to a second legitimate SaaS (loopedin.io) in a multi-stage chain.
  • 03DKIM verification failed for the spoofed domain, and DMARC policy p=quarantine acted on the message, though the Barracuda gateway passed it with a low spam score of 1.80.
  • 04IRONSCALES Adaptive AI flagged the email as high-confidence phishing based on the combination of DKIM failure, no prior contact with recipients, and a link chain inconsistent with a healthcare billing workflow.
  • 05The attacker's design exploited URL scanners that only evaluate the first hop, using clean intermediate SaaS domains to hide the final credential-harvesting destination.
