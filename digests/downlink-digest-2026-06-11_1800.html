Overview

The article reports that ShinyHunters exploited an unpatched Oracle PeopleSoft vulnerability, CVE-2026-35273, to breach enterprise systems, steal data, and demand payment to keep the information private. Google Mandiant attributes the activity to UNC6240 and dates it between May 27 and June 9. Oracle published its advisory on June 10, meaning the flaw was a zero-day for the entire period described by Mandiant.

The campaign hit universities hardest. Mandiant notified more than 100 organizations whose IP addresses matched vulnerable endpoints, and 68% were in higher education, mostly in the United States. Some organizations blocked the activity, while others were compromised and had data posted to the ShinyHunters leak site.

Vulnerability and affected product

CVE-2026-35273 is a remote code execution vulnerability in Oracle PeopleSoft Enterprise PeopleTools. The article states that it is rated 9.8 out of 10 and requires no login and no user interaction. Exploitation requires network access over HTTP and can result in takeover of the server.

The vulnerability is located in the Updates Environment Management component, which sits behind the Environment Management Hub, also known as PSEMHUB. Oracle lists PeopleTools 8.61 and 8.62 as affected and says earlier unsupported versions are probably vulnerable too. Oracle credits researchers from TrendAI Zero Day Initiative and TrendAI Research for reporting the issue.

The exposure described in the article applies to PeopleSoft deployments where the Environment Management Hub is reachable from outside. The article says the immediate action for such deployments is to lock those endpoints down.

Attribution and timing

Google Mandiant attributes the campaign to the group it tracks as UNC6240, identified in the article as ShinyHunters. Mandiant CTO Charles Carmakal confirmed that the bug is being exploited in the wild. The article notes that Oracle has not said whether it has observed exploitation.

Mandiant dates the activity from May 27 to June 9. Oracle’s advisory appeared on June 10, after the observed activity window. Because of that timing, the article describes the vulnerability as a zero-day during the campaign.

Attacker infrastructure and tooling

The article says operational details became public because the attackers left their own infrastructure exposed. Researcher @nahamike01 publicly flagged open directories, after which Mandiant triaged five sequential IP addresses running Python’s SimpleHTTP server on port 8888.

Those exposed servers contained staging files, including:

  • A shared .bash_history file.
  • Custom MeshCentral remote-management agents disguised as Microsoft Azure binaries.
  • A lateral-movement script.

The MeshCentral agents called home to a command-and-control server at azurenetfiles.net. The article says the domain was selected to look like Azure NetApp Files.

Lateral movement and data handling

The lateral-movement script was named [victim]_fanout.sh. According to the article, it spreads over SSH by spraying a hardcoded list of usernames and passwords against internal hosts pulled from /etc/hosts. It then drops a marker file named README-IF-YOU-SEE-THIS-YOUVE-BEEN-HACKED.TXT into PeopleSoft directories.

The command history showed data being compressed with zstd. It also showed an outbound SSH connection to the server hosting the public mirror of the ShinyHunters leak site.

Victims and impact

Mandiant notified more than 100 organizations whose IP addresses matched vulnerable endpoints. The article states that 68% were in higher education, most of them in the United States. Some organizations blocked the activity, while others were compromised and had data posted to the leak site.

The University of Nottingham is identified as one of the first confirmed victims. Have I Been Pwned counted about 455,000 unique email addresses in the leaked set. The data covered current students and alumni and included names, addresses, phone numbers, passport numbers, and details on ethnicity and disabilities.

The University of Nottingham confirmed the breach. The article also states that ShinyHunters says victim outreach has only just started and that it has not posted most of the organizations it claims, so more names are likely.

Mitigation guidance

Oracle’s guidance is to disable the Environment Management Hub service on multi-server setups. For single-server setups, Oracle advises removing the PSEMHUB application outright. If neither option is possible, organizations should block external access to /PSEMHUB/*, especially /PSEMHUB/hub, and /PSIGW/HttpListeningConnector at the perimeter.

Mandiant warns that WAF body-inspection rules alone are not sufficient because they can be bypassed. The article also notes that restricting these endpoints does not break normal user sessions. Organizations are advised to apply Oracle’s update for the relevant PeopleTools version once they confirm it is available in My Oracle Support.

Hunting guidance and indicators

The article lists several signs organizations should look for when checking for compromise:

  • WebLogic access logs showing external POST requests to /PSEMHUB/hub or /PSIGW/HttpListeningConnector.
  • Unexpected .jsp files under the PSEMHUB.war web application directory.
  • Odd folders named logs, persistantstorage, or scratchpad under PSEMHUB paths.
  • Recently changed .xml files under the web document root’s envmetadata/data/environment directory.
  • Outbound SMB traffic on port 445 from PeopleSoft hosts to external destinations.

The article explains that the XML files can be abused for XMLDecoder persistence that triggers on the next restart. It also says the exploit chain may use outbound SMB traffic to capture machine-account NetNTLM hashes.

Significance

The article frames the campaign as notable because ShinyHunters has recently focused on vishing, stolen tokens, and weak access controls against SaaS and education platforms. Examples cited include Salesforce customers and Canvas. In contrast, a server-side zero-day in on-premises ERP software represents a different method aimed at the same data-rich targets.

The article closes by saying the open question is whether this was a one-off borrowed zero-day or the start of ShinyHunters moving into ERP exploitation.

01 Oracle PeopleSoft CVE-2026-35273 Exploitation by ShinyHunters

Google Mandiant attributed a PeopleSoft exploitation campaign to ShinyHunters, tracked as UNC6240, with observed activity dated between May 27 and June 9. The exploited vulnerability, CVE-2026-35273, affects Oracle PeopleSoft Enterprise PeopleTools and carries a CVSS score of 9.8 due to unauthenticated remote code execution over HTTP without user interaction.

The campaign appears heavily concentrated in higher education. Mandiant notified more than 100 organizations with vulnerable endpoints, and 68% were in higher education, mostly in the United States. The University of Nottingham confirmed a breach, and Have I Been Pwned counted approximately 455,000 unique email addresses in a leaked dataset associated with the incident.

Oracle confirmed that CVE-2026-35273 affects PeopleSoft Enterprise PeopleTools versions 8.61 and 8.62 and released emergency mitigations while indicating that a patch is forthcoming. BleepingComputer reported that ShinyHunters claimed to have used a “gadget chain” combining older flaws and zero-day vulnerabilities, allegedly stealing data from 300 PeopleSoft instances across more than 100 organizations. Researcher Michael R also identified exposed online directories containing attack-related tooling and shared IP addresses relevant to the campaign.
