Summary
A phishing email impersonating a document-sharing notification used multiple trusted link-protection wrappers and Google Docs to hide a credential-harvesting redirect chain. Despite failing SPF, DKIM, and DMARC, it reached the inbox, showing how abused security infrastructure and relay trust can mask malicious emails.

IRONSCALES detected a credential-harvesting campaign in May 2026 targeting a mid-size equipment finance company. The email impersonated a document-sharing notification from “Jennifer Johnson” and referenced a company-specific aging report. The actual sender was davidbr@bellnet[.]ca, a Canadian consumer ISP account with no connection to the target organization or a document-sharing platform.

The payload link was routed through three layers before reaching a Google Docs presentation: EdgePilot, Barracuda LinkProtect, and the attacker-controlled domain smstkart[.]com/bee. The article says the use of legitimate link protection services and Google Docs gave the campaign apparent legitimacy and helped disguise the redirect chain. AppRiver’s SecureTide gateway logged link wrapping and returned a clean verdict.

At the recipient’s Microsoft 365 tenant, the message failed SPF, DKIM, and DMARC, while composite authentication returned none with reason 405. Despite those failures, Microsoft delivered the email to the inbox with SCL=-1, which the article says is typically reserved for trusted or allow-listed messages. Themis flagged the message at 84% confidence and auto-resolved it as phishing based on behavioral signals such as a first-time sender, consumer ISP address, display-name mismatch, and inconsistent communication pattern.

  • Legitimate link-protection services can become camouflage when attackers nest malicious redirect chains inside trusted security wrappers, causing visible URLs and reputation checks to appear safe.
  • A clean verdict from an upstream email gateway can create false trust downstream when the gateway’s forwarding breaks SPF, DKIM, and DMARC validation but the recipient environment still treats the relayed message as trusted.
  • Authentication failures alone did not prevent inbox delivery because Microsoft assigned SCL=-1 despite SPF, DKIM, DMARC, and composite authentication failures at the recipient tenant.
  • Behavioral context was decisive in detection because Themis flagged anomalies that URL reputation and authentication checks did not resolve, including first-time sender status, consumer ISP origin, display-name mismatch, and unusual vendor-relationship patterns.
Key Points
  • IRONSCALES detected a credential-harvesting campaign in May 2026 targeting a mid-size equipment finance company with an email impersonating a document-sharing notification.
  • The email claimed to come from “Jennifer Johnson,” but the actual sender address was davidbr@bellnet[.]ca, a Canadian consumer ISP account with no connection to the target organization or a document-sharing platform.

  • Themis flagged the message at 84% confidence and auto-resolved it as phishing based on behavioral signals including a first-time sender, a consumer ISP address, a display-name mismatch, and an inconsistent communication pattern.
Why it matters

Employees at a mid-size equipment finance company were targeted with an email that looked like a normal shared financial report but could have led them to give up their login credentials. The broader risk is that attackers can hide behind familiar services like link-protection tools and Google Docs, making dangerous emails look safe even when basic checks fail.
