Vietnamese threat actors are running a large-scale phishing campaign that abuses Google AppSheet to compromise and resell approximately 30,000 Facebook accounts. This operation highlights how attackers are repurposing trusted platforms to bypass security controls and monetize stolen credentials.

A Vietnamese-linked cyber operation codenamed AccountDumpling has compromised approximately 30,000 Facebook accounts by abusing Google AppSheet as a phishing relay. Attackers send spoofed Meta Support emails from noreply@appsheet.com to bypass spam filters and create a false sense of urgency. These messages target Facebook Business owners with threats of permanent account deletion unless they submit an appeal.

Victims are directed to fake landing pages hosted on legitimate platforms like Netlify, Vercel, and Google Drive. The schemes harvest login credentials, two-factor authentication codes, government IDs, and browser screenshots. All stolen data is exfiltrated to attacker-controlled Telegram channels. The compromised accounts are subsequently sold back through an illicit underground storefront. This operation demonstrates how threat actors continuously repurpose trusted cloud services to facilitate delivery, hosting, and monetization.

Guardio security researcher Shaked Chen has documented a large-scale phishing campaign dubbed AccountDumpling that targets Facebook Business account owners. The operation relies on Google AppSheet to distribute emails from noreply@appsheet.com, effectively masking the malicious traffic behind a trusted Google service. These messages falsely claim to be from Meta Support and warn recipients that their accounts will be permanently deleted without immediate action.

The campaign employs four primary lure clusters designed to induce panic and drive users to credential-harvesting pages. The first cluster uses Netlify-hosted pages to collect dates of birth, phone numbers, and government-issued ID photos. The second cluster features blue badge evaluation lures on Vercel that require a bogus CAPTCHA before redirecting users to a phishing page. This second method also forces a retry to capture credentials and two-factor authentication codes.

The third cluster distributes Google Drive-hosted PDFs generated through a free Canva account, which use html2canvas to screenshot browser activity and steal passwords. The fourth cluster impersonates major companies like WhatsApp and Adobe to build rapport and redirect victims to attacker-controlled sites. All harvested data from the first three clusters is forwarded to Telegram channels holding roughly 30,000 victim records. Most victims are located in the United States, Italy, Canada, the Philippines, India, Spain, Australia, the United Kingdom, Brazil, and Mexico.

Open-source intelligence links this individual to a digital marketing website based in Vietnam. The operation functions as a continuous criminal-commercial loop where stolen accounts are sold back through an illicit storefront. Lure Clusters and Data Harvesting The operation utilizes four distinct lure clusters designed to trigger Meta-related panic and direct victims to malicious landing pages.

  • Netlify-hosted Facebook help center pages facilitate account takeover attacks while collecting dates of birth, phone numbers, and government-issued ID photos.
  • Vercel-hosted pages mimic a Blue badge evaluation or Meta Privacy Center. These pages gate access behind a bogus CAPTCHA and force a credential retry to harvest passwords and two-factor authentication codes.
  • Google Drive-hosted PDFs masquerade as account verification instructions. Generated via a free Canva account, these documents use html2canvas to capture browser screenshots and steal passwords alongside two-factor codes.
  • Fake job offers impersonate companies such as WhatsApp, Meta, Adobe, Pinterest, Apple, and Coca-Cola. These lures build rapport with recipients and redirect them to attacker-controlled sites or call sessions.

Exfiltration and Victim Demographics All stolen data from the first three clusters is forwarded to attacker-controlled Telegram channels. These channels collectively store approximately 30,000 victim records. The majority of compromised accounts belong to users in the United States, Italy, Canada, the Philippines, India, Spain, Australia, the United Kingdom, Brazil, and Mexico. Most victims have been locked out of their own accounts due to the credential theft.

EXEC Executive Overview

This digest covers intelligence reported within the 2026-05-01 coverage window, detailing a phishing campaign that has compromised approximately 30,000 Facebook accounts through the abuse of Google AppSheet infrastructure. The operation illustrates a trend of threat actors exploiting low-code application platforms to distribute malicious content, leveraging the reputation of Google’s ecosystem to bypass email security controls.

The campaign specifically targets owners of Facebook Business accounts. Threat actors deploy social engineering lures that mimic official Meta Support communications, warning recipients of permanent account deletion to induce urgency. These messages direct victims to phishing pages designed to harvest both login credentials and two-factor authentication codes, indicating a methodical approach to account takeover that neutralizes multi-factor authentication defenses.

Technical analysis confirms the use of Google AppSheet to generate and route phishing emails. This technique allows actors to send messages from a trusted Google domain, potentially circumventing filters that scrutinize external sender domains. The phishing infrastructure captures sensitive data, which is then exfiltrated to Telegram channels controlled by the attackers. These channels function as a staging area for stolen credentials, which are subsequently sold via an illicit underground storefront, establishing a monetization pipeline for compromised accounts.

Open-source intelligence and document metadata link the operation to a Vietnamese threat actor identified as “PHẠM TÀI TÂN.” This individual operates a digital marketing website, suggesting a potential overlap between legitimate business activities and cybercrime infrastructure. The reliance on AppSheet for distribution and Telegram for exfiltration reflects an operational strategy that balances tool accessibility with obfuscation.

The compromise of 30,000 accounts highlights the risks of platform abuse in large-scale credential harvesting operations. The targeting of Facebook Business accounts and the impersonation of Meta Support represent sophisticated vectors for compromising social media infrastructure. This intelligence confirms that actors are actively misusing enterprise tools to scale phishing efforts, resulting in significant account losses and the continued circulation of stolen credentials in underground markets.
