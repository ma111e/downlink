In plain words

A stolen Microsoft 365 school email account in Argentina was used to send a fake message to a K-12 school district in the eastern United States, pretending to be someone the recipient already knew. The email looked trustworthy because it came from a real school account and included a casual link that led to a brand-new site meant to steal login details. This matters because ordinary checks that prove an email came from a real account can still be fooled when the account itself has been taken over, putting schools and their communities at risk.

Summary
A compromised Microsoft 365 account at an Argentine school sent a highly targeted phishing email to a U.S. K-12 district, impersonating a known contact and passing SPF, DKIM, and DMARC. The attack used a same-day registered credential-harvesting domain, showing why authentication alone cannot stop account-compromise phishing.

In May 2026, IRONSCALES detected a spear-phishing campaign targeting a K-12 school district in the eastern United States. The email came from a compromised Microsoft 365 account belonging to an Argentine educational institution, escueladelaciudad[.]com[.]ar. It used the exact display name of a known community contact and a casual subject line, “re: plans for party,” to make the message appear familiar and low-risk.

The message passed SPF, DKIM, and DMARC because it was sent through the legitimate Microsoft 365 tenant of the compromised account. The email contained a single link to bgiur[.]ethrecn[.]com/ulusouaienkueortkeci, using ethrecn[.]com, a domain registered the same day as the attack. IRONSCALES said the same-day domain, randomized URL path, unfamiliar sender address, and display-name mismatch were key signs of a credential-harvesting attempt.

The article emphasizes that email authentication checks can succeed even when an attacker is using a hijacked account, because those checks verify authorized sending infrastructure rather than user intent. IRONSCALES community intelligence flagged the incident based on patterns from similar campaigns, and the message was quarantined before the recipient engaged. The case is mapped to MITRE ATT&CK techniques T1566.002, T1036.005, and T1586.002.

  • SPF, DKIM, and DMARC all passed because the attacker used a compromised account inside a legitimate Microsoft 365 tenant, showing that email authentication cannot distinguish between the real user and someone using stolen credentials.
  • The display name matched a known contact while the sender address did not, making the mismatch between familiar names and unfamiliar addresses a strong detection signal even when authentication checks succeed.
  • The same-day registration of ethrecn.com helped the phishing link avoid reputation-based detection because the domain had no prior history, abuse reports, or blocklist entries.
  • The casual subject line “re: plans for party” reduced suspicion by imitating an ongoing personal conversation rather than using a typical financial or IT-themed lure.
  • Community intelligence provided the decisive detection signal by matching the message against similar campaign patterns, while automated analysis alone assigned only a baseline confidence score.
