Vulnerability Overview

CVE-2026-44338 represents a critical authentication bypass flaw within the PraisonAI open-source multi-agent orchestration framework. The vulnerability carries a CVSS score of 7.3 and fundamentally stems from a legacy Flask API server that ships with security controls disabled by default. Specifically, the src/praisonai/api_server.py file hardcodes AUTH_ENABLED = False and AUTH_TOKEN = None. This configuration effectively removes authentication requirements for the legacy server, exposing protected functionality to any network-accessible caller.

Technical Impact and Exploitation

The unconditional authentication bypass allows unauthenticated actors to interact with critical API endpoints. Attackers can enumerate configured agent files through the /agents endpoint and trigger locally configured workflows via the /chat endpoint.   • Unauthenticated enumeration of agent configurations
  • Unauthorized workflow execution
  • Repeated consumption of model API quotas
  • Exposure of execution results to external parties The ultimate impact severity is contingent upon the permissions granted within the operator’s agents.yaml configuration file.

The vulnerability impacts all PraisonAI Python package versions from 2.5.6 through 4.6.33. The maintainers have released patch version 4.6.34 to resolve the issue. Overview of AI Hallucinations in Cybersecurity

AI hallucinations refer to confidently presented, plausible-sounding outputs that are factually inaccurate. Base language models do not retrieve verified information; instead, they construct responses by predicting words and phrases based on learned patterns in their training data. Because these outputs are statistically likely rather than factually true, they can closely resemble accurate information while containing fabricated data, nonexistent citations, or incorrect research references. A 2025 evaluation using Artificial Analysis’s AA-Omniscience benchmark highlighted the scale of this issue, revealing that 36 out of 40 tested AI models were more likely to provide a confident but incorrect answer on difficult questions than a correct one. When AI takes on a larger role in cybersecurity operations, these hallucinations introduce serious security risks by exploiting human trust and feeding incorrect data directly into automated systems.

Root Causes of AI Hallucinations

Several factors contribute to the formation of hallucinated outputs:

  • Flawed training data: Models learn from their training sets, meaning outdated information or outright errors become embedded in their outputs without being flagged.
  • Bias in input data: Overrepresentation of certain patterns causes models to treat those scenarios as universally applicable, even when the context differs.
  • Lack of response validation: Base language models optimize for coherent, plausible outputs rather than factual accuracy, leaving them vulnerable to hallucinations unless retrieval or grounding layers are added.
  • Prompt ambiguity: Vague inputs increase the likelihood that models will fill informational gaps with assumptions, raising the risk of incorrect outputs.

Impact on Cybersecurity Operations

Incorrect or fabricated AI information leaves organizations vulnerable to three primary cyber threats:

  • Missed threats: AI threat detection relies on historical patterns and learned behavior. When attacks align with known behaviors, the AI performs well, but it often fails to flag zero-day attacks or underrepresented techniques that lack sufficient context in the training data.
  • Fabricated threats: Models may misclassify normal network traffic as suspicious, triggering false positives and unnecessary incident response actions. Repeated false alarms lead to alert fatigue, desensitizing security teams and increasing the risk of overlooking legitimate threats.
  • Incorrect remediation: This is the most dangerous manifestation, occurring after trust has been established. AI systems may confidently recommend deleting sensitive files, modifying system configurations, or disabling firewall rules. If executed through privileged accounts, these hallucinated actions can expose organizations to identity-based attacks, lateral movement, or irreversible data loss.

Mitigation Strategies and Governance

While AI hallucinations cannot be fully eliminated, their impact can be significantly reduced through specific controls and governance measures:

  • Require human review before action: AI-generated outputs should never trigger sensitive or privileged actions without human verification first. This applies to infrastructure changes, access updates, and incident response workflows.
  • Treat training data as a security asset: Regularly auditing training data by eliminating outdated records, biased datasets, and inaccurate information reduces the likelihood of flawed outputs. Continuous data governance is also necessary to prevent model collapse, where future models are trained on fabricated information produced by earlier AI systems.
  • Enforce least-privilege access for AI systems: AI-driven systems should only receive the permissions needed to perform their tasks. Restricting access ensures that even if an AI generates incorrect guidance, it cannot execute actions beyond its allowed scope.
  • Invest in prompt engineering training: Organizations must train employees, especially those interacting directly with AI systems, on how to write specific prompts that drive verifiable outputs. Understanding that AI outputs require validation prevents employees from treating AI systems as authoritative by default.

Identity Security as the Central Control

AI hallucinations become real security risks when they lead to action, which is fundamentally an access problem rather than just a model problem. Security incidents arise when AI systems have sufficient access to act on incorrect guidance or when humans trust outputs without verification. Placing identity security at the center of AI governance involves enforcing least-privilege access, monitoring privileged activity, and securing both human and Non-Human Identities (NHIs). By implementing these controls, organizations can prevent AI-driven decisions from evolving into damaging security incidents.

Attack Methodology and Execution

The attack initiates through external Microsoft Teams chats where threat actors impersonate internal IT or help-desk staff. Once engaged, victims are tricked into pasting a malicious PowerShell command directly into their systems. This command triggers a download from a Dropbox link containing a ZIP archive. ModeloRAT Capabilities and Architecture

The deployed ModeloRAT variant has evolved considerably compared to previous operations. The malware maintains multiple independent access channels, including a primary RAT, a reverse shell, and a TCP backdoor. These channels operate on separate infrastructure to ensure that access is preserved even if one communication path is disrupted. Functionally, the malware collects system and user information, captures screenshots, and exfiltrates files from the host filesystem.

Persistence and Resilience Mechanisms

KongTuke has implemented expanded persistence mechanisms to ensure long-term network access. The implant utilizes the following techniques:

  • Startup shortcuts
  • VBScript launchers

A critical feature of this persistence strategy is that the scheduled task is intentionally excluded from the implant’s self-destruct routine. This routine wipes other persistence artifacts but leaves the scheduled task intact, allowing it to survive standard cleanup procedures and system reboots.
