    5. Adds a preinstall hook.
    6. Increases the package versions.
    7. Republishes them using the compromised maintainer’s identity.

EXEC Executive Overview

This digest covers a surge in software supply chain attacks targeting developer ecosystems, the emergence of a sophisticated OAuth consent phishing platform that bypasses MFA, disruptions of criminal service platforms, and several high-impact vulnerability disclosures. Multiple campaigns exploited compromised accounts and CI/CD pipelines to steal credentials, while threat actors continued to abuse legitimate identity and code-signing mechanisms for malware distribution.

01 Supply Chain Attacks on Developer Ecosystems

A coordinated wave of supply chain attacks struck the npm ecosystem, with the compromised account ‘atool’ (i@hust.cc) leading to the publication of 639 malicious versions across 323 unique packages, including popular libraries timeago.js and echarts-for-react. The payload, dubbed Shai-Hulud and Mini Shai-Hulud, harvests credentials from CI/CD environments by reading Runner.Worker process memory, targeting over 20 credential types across AWS, GCP, Azure, GitHub, SSH, and databases. Exfiltration uses the Session P2P network, HTTPS to an attacker-controlled domain (t.m-kosche[.]com), and a fallback mechanism that creates public GitHub repositories containing stolen data. The campaign also drops persistent backdoors into VS Code and Claude Code configurations, and can generate valid Sigstore provenance attestations by abusing OIDC tokens from compromised CI pipelines.

Separately, a compromised version of the Nx Console VS Code extension (version 18.95.0) silently fetched an obfuscated payload from a dangling orphan commit in the official nrwl/nx GitHub repository, affecting over 2.2 million installations. The malicious code harvested developer secrets and installed a Python backdoor on macOS systems. In a related GitHub Actions supply chain attack, threat actors redirected tags on the actions-cool/issues-helper action to an imposter commit that exfiltrated CI/CD credentials via the same t.m-kosche[.]com domain. These incidents highlight a concerted effort by threat actors to target developer toolchains and build pipelines.

02 Criminal Service Platforms: Malware Signing, BadIIS, and Ad Fraud

Microsoft disrupted Fox Tempest, a malware-signing-as-a-service platform that abused the company’s Artifact Signing service to produce fraudulent code-signing certificates for ransomware affiliates from Rhysida, INC, Qilin, and Akira. Microsoft revoked over 1,000 certificates, seized the platform’s website, and took down hundreds of virtual machines. The service, operating since May 2025, signed malware families such as Oyster, Lumma Stealer, and Vidar, and was paid millions of dollars in cryptocurrency by ransomware affiliates.

Cisco Talos uncovered a commodity BadIIS variant used in a malware-as-a-service ecosystem operated by Chinese-speaking threat actors. The malware, developed under the alias ‘lwxat’ since at least September 2021, includes a builder tool that configures payloads for traffic redirection, content hijacking, and SEO fraud. The same developer produced auxiliary tools for automated deployment and persistence.

In the ad fraud space, the Trapdoor scheme involved 455 malicious Android apps that generated 659 million bid requests per day at their peak. The apps used install attribution to activate malicious behavior only for users acquired via threat actor-run ad campaigns, tricking advertisers into paying for fake traffic. Google removed all apps from the Play Store after responsible disclosure.

03 Vulnerability Disclosures and Exploits

Cisco Talos disclosed eight vulnerabilities in the TP-Link Archer AX53 router, including a stack-based buffer overflow and multiple OS command injection flaws. Additionally, privilege escalation vulnerabilities were reported in Adobe Photoshop and Norton VPN installation processes via the Microsoft Store, and a reachable assertion vulnerability in OpenVPN 2.6.x and 2.8_git can cause denial of service.

A proof-of-concept exploit for CVE-2026-31635, dubbed DirtyDecrypt, was released for a Linux kernel local privilege escalation vulnerability affecting distributions with CONFIG_RXGK enabled (Fedora, Arch Linux, openSUSE Tumbleweed). The exploit is a variant of earlier attacks like Copy Fail and Dirty Frag. Drupal announced an urgent core security release scheduled for May 20, 2026, affecting all supported branches; administrators are advised to patch quickly as exploits may emerge within hours or days.

04 Microsoft Ecosystem: Vulnerability Trends, Patching Issues, and Bug Fixes

Microsoft disclosed 1,273 vulnerabilities in 2025, a decline from the prior year, but critical vulnerabilities doubled from 78 to 157. Elevation of Privilege flaws accounted for 40% of all CVEs, while Information Disclosure flaws rose 73%. Critical vulnerabilities in Azure and Dynamics 365 spiked from 4 to 37, and Office vulnerabilities surged 234% year-over-year, indicating increased attacker focus on stealth and reconnaissance. Microsoft also confirmed that restricted network environments may experience Windows Update failures (error 0x80010002) after the January 2026 preview updates, and is working on a resolution via a Known Issue Rollback group policy.

A macOS security update is causing undismissible location prompts in Microsoft Teams, as the OS no longer stores users’ location permission selections. Microsoft is investigating a fix and advises users to manually toggle location access in System Settings.

05 OAuth Consent Phishing Bypasses MFA

The EvilTokens phishing-as-a-service platform compromised over 340 Microsoft 365 organizations within five weeks by tricking users into entering a code at microsoft.com/devicelogin and completing their normal MFA challenge. The attacker then receives a valid refresh token scoped to mailbox, drive, calendar, and contacts, bypassing MFA because the user authenticates on the legitimate identity provider. The normalization of clicking through consent screens by knowledge workers, combined with toxic permission combinations across multiple applications, greatly expands the risk surface. Closing this gap requires treating OAuth grants with the same monitoring and revocation discipline as authentication.

06 Data Breaches, Law Enforcement, and Regulatory Actions

7-Eleven confirmed a data breach on April 8, 2026, where an unauthorized third party accessed systems storing franchisee documents. The ShinyHunters extortion group claimed responsibility, leaking a 9.4GB archive of documents after the company refused to pay a ransom. ShinyHunters continues to target Salesforce environments.

INTERPOL’s Operation Ramz led to the arrest of over 200 individuals across the Middle East and North Africa, seizing 53 servers used for phishing and malware. Authorities dismantled an investment scam operation in Jordan and shut down a phishing-as-a-service platform in Algeria.

In the UK, regulator Ofcom is updating its codes of practice to require tech companies to detect and remove non-consensual intimate images and deepfakes, with new legislation mandating removal within two days and banning nudification tools. The move follows the UK Prime Minister describing the spread of such images as a ‘national emergency.’
