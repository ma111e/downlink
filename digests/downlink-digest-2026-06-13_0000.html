01 Critical Enterprise Exposure: Splunk Enterprise CVE-2026-20253

Splunk released fixes for CVE-2026-20253, a critical Splunk Enterprise vulnerability rated CVSS 9.8. The issue affects Splunk Enterprise 10.0.0 through 10.0.6 and 10.2.0 through 10.2.3, with fixes available in 10.0.7 and 10.2.4; Splunk Cloud is reported as unaffected because it does not use the relevant PostgreSQL sidecars.

Splunk described the flaw as allowing an unauthenticated user to create or truncate arbitrary files through an unauthenticated PostgreSQL sidecar service endpoint. watchTowr Labs reported that the /v1/postgres/recovery/backup and /v1/postgres/recovery/restore endpoints can be abused for pre-authenticated remote code execution, making the bug especially relevant for organizations running exposed or reachable Splunk Enterprise management components.

02 Chinese-Linked Espionage and Abuse of Trusted Access Paths

Sygnia reported that the Chinese-linked Velvet Ant group maintained access to an isolated critical infrastructure network for 10 years in a campaign tracked as Operation Highland. The attackers initially compromised internet-facing systems, deployed a modified GS-Netcat reverse shell, and used a custom SOCKS5 proxy before modifying Nginx and FastCGI configurations to create an HTTP-based remote execution path into the segregated environment.

The campaign’s persistence centered on trusted authentication components. Velvet Ant allegedly replaced legitimate Linux PAM modules and OpenSSH components with backdoored versions that accepted hardcoded passwords, harvested credentials, captured commands, and preserved access; Sygnia noted that remediation was operationally complex because replacing trojanized authentication components risked outages and administrator lockout.

A separate insider-threat case underscored similar trust-boundary risks. Former Saydel Community School District IT employee Ezekiel Dean Potter was sentenced to 21 months in prison after prosecutors said he retained credentials and disrupted the district over 21 months, including deleting accounts and pages, interfering with Apple School Manager, Schoology, and Gmail access, and causing tens of thousands of dollars in remediation costs.

03 Supply Chain and Authenticated Phishing Infrastructure

StepSecurity reported a June 2026 supply-chain attack against the Arch User Repository, dubbed “Atomic Arch,” in which attackers hijacked more than 400 AUR packages. The attackers adopted orphaned packages with existing users and modified PKGBUILDs or install hooks to execute malicious npm dependencies such as atomic-lockfile and js-digest.

The malicious npm package reportedly contained a Rust credential-stealing payload targeting browser cookies, SSH keys, developer tokens, chat sessions, Docker or Podman credentials, and cloud access keys. The reporting specifically called out Arch and Arch-based environments, including self-hosted CI runners and WSL2 Arch systems, as potentially relevant where AUR packages were installed during the affected period.

IRONSCALES described a credential-harvesting campaign that abused Salesforce sending infrastructure and passed SPF, DKIM, and DMARC for the outcomes.com domain. The Medicare Transaction Facilitator attestation-themed message used a self-send pattern through Salesforce and linked to nexaroco.com/providerrelations/, which served an Office 365 credential harvesting page.

04 Anthropic Fable 5 and Mythos 5 Export-Control Suspension

Multiple reports covered Anthropic’s suspension of Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 access after receiving a U.S. government export-control directive requiring the company to block access by foreign nationals. Anthropic said the directive applied to foreign nationals inside and outside the United States, including its own foreign-national employees, leading the company to disable the models globally rather than selectively enforce access restrictions.

Anthropic stated that the government concern appeared tied to a reported jailbreak involving asking Fable 5 to read a specific codebase and fix software flaws, but the company disputed that this represented a universal jailbreak or uniquely dangerous capability. Reporting from Socket noted the directive arrived on June 12 at 5:21 p.m. ET and that some users lost access mid-project or shortly after upgrading paid plans.

Aikido’s analysis added context around the release, noting that Fable 5 was publicly released while Mythos Preview remained limited to Glasswing initiative participants and a second cohort of about 150 organizations.
