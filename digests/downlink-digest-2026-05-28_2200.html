Cybersecurity researchers at WithSecure have exposed a persistent cyberespionage campaign by a threat group named GreyVibe. The campaign has been active since at least August 2025 and appears to serve Russian state interests, though the operators exhibit behaviors atypical of a mature nation-state actor. Their primary targets are Ukrainian or Ukraine-related organizations spanning military, government, civilian, and business sectors. The group’s use of large language models (LLMs) for lure generation and malware development sets it apart from many traditional espionage actors.

GreyVibe employs five documented attack chains. PhantomMail uses spear-phishing emails with links to ZIP/RAR archives on Google Drive and 4sync, delivering decoy PDFs that impersonate Ukrainian government, emergency, telecom, or energy entities. PhantomClick relies on fake CAPTCHA or Cloudflare verification pages designed to trick victims into executing self-infecting commands, while PrincessClub and DroneLink use fraudulent adult/dating and military charity websites respectively to spread malware. A fifth chain, Nebo, presents a fake Russian military communications login page, likely attempting to fool Ukrainian personnel into believing they are accessing a Russian terminal.

The malware arsenal includes two PowerShell-based remote access trojans: LegionRelay, which steals files, screenshots, browser credentials, Telegram and WhatsApp data, and sets up RDP access; and PhantomRelay, which performs fingerprinting and executes arbitrary commands. On mobile, the FallSpy Android spyware harvests contacts, call logs, device information, location, media, and SIM data. Four custom obfuscators (LOOKVALPS, LOOKVALJS, DAYLIGHT, TEASOUP) were likely developed with LLM assistance, and AI tools like ChatGPT, Ideogram AI, and Google Gemini were used to create convincing images and content for lure pages.

Despite the focus on Ukrainian targets and the use of Russian-language malware panels and code comments, WithSecure hesitates to label GreyVibe a definitive nation-state group. The actor’s lack of operational discipline is evident: development and test samples were uploaded to a public scanning platform, and on some victim machines a cryptocurrency miner was deployed—an activity more common among cybercriminals. Moreover, early PhantomRelay samples shared a unique ISO builder linked to former TrickBot members (UAC-0098) who previously targeted Ukraine, suggesting a connection or overlap with criminal circles.

The researchers speculate that GreyVibe could be a hybrid entity—either former cybercriminals absorbed into state-backed operations, independent criminals receiving state-directed tasking, or a mixed team of both. This blurry attribution reinforces the need for defenders to focus on the provided indicators of compromise and behavior patterns rather than solely on threat actor labels. WithSecure has published a full report and IOCs, enabling organizations to strengthen detection against these multifaceted intrusions.

GreyVibe Campaign Overview

A likely Russian-aligned threat group, designated GreyVibe by WithSecure, has been executing a cyberespionage campaign against Ukrainian targets since at least August 2025. The operation leverages artificial intelligence tools to craft convincing lures and develop custom malware, and it demonstrates a blend of state-interest targeting with criminal tradecraft. Researchers identified multiple attack vectors, a diverse malware toolkit, and behaviors that challenge easy classification into purely nation-state or cybercriminal categories.

Targets and Objectives

The campaign concentrates exclusively on Ukrainian or Ukraine-related entities:

  • Military organizations
  • Government bodies
  • Civilian infrastructure
  • Business sector entities

This target profile aligns with Russian state interests as of the article’s timeline (published mid-2026, covering events from 2025 onward). The activity suggests intelligence collection and possibly financial gain, as evidenced by a cryptocurrency miner found on some compromised machines.

Attack Chains

GreyVibe utilizes five distinct lures, each designed to deliver malware or trick victims into self-infection:

PhantomMail

  • Spear-phishing emails with links to Google Drive or 4sync hosting ZIP/RAR archives.
  • Decoy PDFs or fake error messages impersonate Ukrainian government, emergency services, telecom providers, and energy companies.
  • Triggers malware installation upon archive extraction or interaction.

PhantomClick

  • Fake CAPTCHA / “ClickFix” pages disguised as Zoom or LAPAS (a Ukrainian software) sites.
  • Bogus Cloudflare verification prompts instruct victims to run a command (self-infecting).
  • Abuses user trust in common verification mechanisms.

PrincessClub

  • Fake Ukrainian adult/dating websites distributing the FallSpy Android spyware and the PhantomRelay/LegionRelay Windows malware.
  • Operators employed fake female Telegram personas and later integrated WebRTC-based live calls to capture victim audio/video.
  • Highly invasive social engineering blending romance scams with espionage.

DroneLink

  • Fake Ukrainian military charity websites themed around FPV drones and UAVs.
  • Shared infrastructure and tooling with PrincessClub campaigns, indicating operational overlap.

Nebo

  • Fake “СПО НЕБО” Russian military communications login pages.
  • Likely designed to deceive Ukrainian military personnel into thinking they were accessing a legitimate Russian terminal, thereby capturing credentials or delivering malware.

Malware Arsenal

LegionRelay (PowerShell RAT)

  • Capabilities: file theft, screenshot capture, browser credential harvesting, Telegram and WhatsApp data exfiltration, and setting up Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) access.
  • Suspected to have been developed with AI assistance.

PhantomRelay (PowerShell RAT)

  • Capabilities: system fingerprinting, dynamic script loading, execution of PowerShell and Windows commands.
  • Also associated with cybercrime activity, though researchers distinguish its state-aligned usage.

FallSpy (Android Spyware)

  • Pure intelligence collection: contact lists, call logs, device/network info, location data, media files, SIM information.
  • Deployed via PrincessClub and Nebo campaigns.

Custom Obfuscators

  • LOOKVALPS, LOOKVALJS, DAYLIGHT, TEASOUP – likely developed with LLM assistance to obfuscate malicious code.

AI Utilization

A defining characteristic of GreyVibe is the systematic use of generative AI:

  • Lure content: ChatGPT, Ideogram AI, and Google Gemini produced detailed text and images. Researchers noted LLM markers (artifacts) in some images, indicating these were AI-generated and not retouched.
  • Tool development: The obfuscators mentioned above and LegionRelay itself likely benefited from LLM-assisted coding.
  • This reduces the cost and effort of creating convincing phishing materials, enabling rapid iteration of campaigns.

Attribution and Actor Profile

Evidence pointing to Russian origins:

  • Malware panel language is Russian.
  • Comments in code artifacts are in Russian.
  • Command-and-control (C2) server time zone is set to UTC+3 (Moscow time).

Despite alignment with Russian state interests, WithSecure refrains from definitively calling it a nation-state actor due to:

  • Lack of operational discipline typical of mature intelligence agencies.
  • Uploading development/test samples to a public scanning platform.
  • Dropping a cryptocurrency miner on victim machines—more typical of financially motivated crime.
  • PhantomRelay’s appearance in non-state cybercrime contexts, though separable from the GreyVibe operation.

Particularly revealing is a link to a group of former TrickBot members (UAC-0098): early and test samples used a unique ISO builder associated with that group, which targeted Ukraine at the start of the Russian invasion. This suggests possible absorption of criminal talent into state-aligned activity, independent contractors receiving state tasking, or a hybrid team.

Impact and Defenses

The article does not detail specific data losses or breaches resulting from these campaigns, but the intelligence-collection capabilities of the malware and the breadth of targeting indicate a significant threat to Ukrainian entities. WithSecure published a set of indicators of compromise (IOCs) on GitHub to help organizations detect and block GreyVibe activity.

Defensive recommendations, while not exhaustively listed in the article, would logically include:

  • Monitoring for the IOCs.
  • Blocking known malicious domains and IP addresses.
  • Training users to recognize fake CAPTCHA and verification prompts.
  • Implementing application control to prevent execution of untrusted PowerShell scripts.
  • Scrutinizing downloads from Google Drive and file-sharing services.

Conclusion

GreyVibe illustrates the evolving landscape of cyberespionage where AI lowers the barrier for creating sophisticated lures, and the line between state-directed operations and cybercrime continues to blur. Defenders must prepare for threat actors that combine espionage motives with criminal techniques, and that leverage commercial AI platforms to enhance their operational efficiency.
