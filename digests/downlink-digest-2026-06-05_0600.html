  • Cisco SD-WAN Cloud-Pro
  • Cisco SD-WAN Cloud (Cisco Managed)

Technical details

Cisco said the vulnerability stems from insufficient validation of user-supplied input. The company said the flaw can allow local attackers with low privileges to execute arbitrary commands as root.

According to Cisco, an attacker can exploit the vulnerability by uploading a crafted file to an affected system. A successful exploit can allow command injection attacks and privilege escalation to the root user.

Cisco also stated that exploitation requires netadmin privileges on the affected system. The company said this would require valid credentials or exploitation of CVE-2026-20182 or CVE-2026-20127. Cisco said it is not aware of successful exploitation by other methods.

Exploitation and observed impact

Cisco said CVE-2026-20245 is being actively exploited in attacks. Cisco’s Product Security Incident Response Team became aware of exploitation in June after Mandiant, a Google Cloud cybersecurity subsidiary, reported the flaw. The article says Mandiant did not share any details.

Cisco said it has observed limited cases where exploitation of the bug resulted in a configuration change being pushed to edge devices.

Indicators of compromise

Cisco shared indicators of compromise and warned administrators to review the SD-WAN /var/log/scripts.log file. The article says administrators should look for attempts to upload tenant configuration data to vSmart controllers in order to escalate privileges through legitimate commands.

The article provides the following example log entry:

Apr 15 09:44:57 vmanage vScript: Tenant list upload per vsmart serial number: /usr/bin/vconfd_script_upload_tenant_list.sh -cli path /home/admin/malicious.csv vpn 0

Cisco said customers who need help determining whether a Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Manager has been compromised may open a case with Cisco TAC. The company advised administrators to generate an admin-tech file first to assist with the review.

Patch status and response guidance

Cisco has not yet released patches for CVE-2026-20245. While patches for this vulnerability are not available, Cisco advised customers to upgrade to the software fixed for CVE-2026-20182 on May 14.

The article notes that CVE-2026-20182 is a maximum-severity Catalyst SD-WAN Controller authentication bypass flaw that Cisco previously tagged as actively exploited as a zero-day to gain administrative privileges on unpatched devices.

Bitdefender separately tracked FIFA lottery emails promising payouts of up to $2 million. The article also notes that a phishing-as-a-service market is selling ready-made scam kits and ticket-buying bots, making takedowns of individual operators less effective.

Streaming Apps and Android Banking Trojans

The article highlights malicious unofficial streaming apps as a major risk for fans searching for free match streams. ThreatFabric observed a spike in malicious unofficial streaming apps around the recent Champions League final, many pretending to be the popular RojaDirecta, and expects similar activity at the World Cup on a larger scale.

Kaspersky tied those same apps to Android banking trojans built to steal from banking and crypto apps. The named malware families are Massiv and Perseus. These apps are not on Google Play, so installing them requires users to click past warnings that would normally block them.

After installation, the malware can abuse Android accessibility tools. The article says it can:

  • Place fake bank login screens over real apps.
  • Record what the user types.
  • Intercept one-time codes from text messages and login apps.
  • Remotely control the screen.

Perseus is described as being built on leaked code from an older Trojan called Cerberus. It can also read note-taking apps for saved passwords and crypto recovery phrases. ThreatFabric’s simplest red flag is a streaming app asking for accessibility access, because such an app has no honest reason to need it.

Social Scams and Stolen Credentials

Bitdefender found more than 55 football-themed ad campaigns on Facebook and Instagram. These campaigns promoted counterfeit kits, fake Panini stickers, and phishing pages. Two merchandise operations were traced back to Chinese operators through ad-tracking tags.

Fortinet counted more than 1,700 spoofed FIFA accounts, nearly 90% of them on Facebook and Instagram. It also found a scheme using fake FIFA job ads and calendar invites to send applicants to a lookalike Google login page.

The article says stolen FIFA logins are already circulating. Fortinet found hundreds of thousands of user logins and more than 4,600 FIFA web addresses in data collected by credential-stealing malware including Vidar, LummaC2, and RedLine.

Host-City Wi-Fi Risks

The article also discusses public Wi-Fi exposure in host cities. A Kaspersky survey drove around Mexico City, Monterrey, and Guadalajara and found that 10% to 12% of networks were open and password-free. It also found WPS pairing still enabled across nearly half of networks.

According to the article, both open networks and WPS exposure create opportunities for rogue “evil twin” hotspots. These hotspots can copy a real network and quietly read its traffic.

Recommended Precautions for Fans

The article gives several direct recommendations for fans:

  • Buy tickets only through fifa.com.
  • Type the address manually instead of trusting an ad or search result.
  • Enable multi-factor login.
  • Treat any seller requesting cryptocurrency as a scam, because FIFA’s ticketing does not accept crypto.
  • Avoid sideloaded streaming apps, especially those requesting Android accessibility access.
  • Use mobile data where possible on open Wi-Fi in host cities.
  • Avoid logging into bank or email accounts on open Wi-Fi.

Recommendations for Security Teams

For security teams, the article describes a straightforward monitoring and fraud-preparation task. Teams should watch for new FIFA-themed domains and lookalike login pages. They should also flag staff or customer logins that appear in Vidar, LummaC2, or RedLine stealer logs.

The article further recommends preparing fraud teams for ticket and chargeback spikes through mid-July. This aligns with the tournament window described in the article, from June 11 to July 19, when searches for tickets, streams, and travel are expected to peak.

Responses and Remaining Risk

Meta said it is responding by showing warning pop-ups when people search Facebook for FIFA tickets. It also said it worked with Visa to take down a Facebook network linked to fake World Cup sites pushing bogus gambling. The FBI is asking scam victims to report incidents through IC3.

The article ends by emphasizing that the fraud wave may not have fully activated yet. Group-IB counted roughly 3,800 fraudulent FIFA domains that were parked and unused, ready to be switched on. With ready-made scam kits and bots already for sale, the article identifies June 11 to July 19 as the likely peak period for ticket, streaming, and travel-related scams.

EXEC Executive Overview

This coverage window is dominated by active exploitation of edge and web-facing platforms, alongside large-scale fraud activity tied to the FIFA World Cup 2026. The most technically significant items are an unauthenticated WordPress plugin RCE affecting Everest Forms Pro and an unpatched Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Manager zero-day used for root privilege escalation.

01 Active Exploitation of Web and Network Management Platforms

Threat actors are actively exploiting CVE-2026-3300, a critical remote code execution vulnerability in the Everest Forms Pro WordPress plugin affecting versions up to and including 1.9.12. Wordfence attributed the flaw to unsafe handling in the Calculation Addon’s process_filter() function, where user-submitted form values are concatenated into PHP code without proper escaping before being passed to eval().

Successful exploitation allows unauthenticated attackers to execute arbitrary PHP code on vulnerable WordPress sites. Reported post-exploitation outcomes include creation of rogue administrator accounts, deployment of web shells, and persistence on compromised servers. Everest Forms Pro 1.9.13, released on March 18, 2026, contains the fix for the vulnerability.

Cisco separately warned that CVE-2026-20245, a high-severity zero-day in Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Manager, is being actively exploited and remains unpatched. The flaw affects all deployment models, including on-premises, Cisco-managed cloud, Cloud-Pro, and FedRAMP environments, and allows local attackers with netadmin privileges to upload crafted files and execute arbitrary commands as root. Cisco linked the issue to insufficient validation of user-supplied input and published indicators involving activity in /var/log/scripts.log related to attempts to upload tenant configuration data to vSmart controllers.

02 FIFA World Cup 2026-Themed Fraud, Credential Theft, and Mobile Malware

Researchers and law enforcement are reporting an early wave of FIFA World Cup 2026-themed cybercrime, including fake FIFA domains, cloned login portals, fraudulent ticket offers, counterfeit merchandise stores, bogus streaming sites, and fake betting platforms. Group-IB tracked more than 4,300 fraudulent FIFA-related domains registered since August 2025 and linked over 300 cloned FIFA sites to a Chinese-speaking operation it tracks as GHOST STADIUM.

GHOST STADIUM phishing pages closely imitate FIFA’s legitimate login flow and prompt victims to reset passwords, enabling attackers to seize accounts and potentially monetize tickets associated with them. The campaign illustrates how major global sporting events continue to drive early credential-harvesting infrastructure long before the event itself, especially where account access, ticket resale, and fan payment activity intersect.

Mobile malware is also appearing in the FIFA-themed threat ecosystem. ThreatFabric and Kaspersky reported unofficial Android streaming apps impersonating services such as RojaDirecta and delivering banking trojans including Massiv and Perseus, both of which can abuse Android accessibility features to steal credentials and intercept verification codes. Fortinet also found hundreds of thousands of user logins and more than 4,600 FIFA web addresses in data gathered by credential-stealing malware families including Vidar, LummaC2, and RedLine.

03 Abuse of Trusted Services for Skimming and Exfiltration

Sansec reported separate skimmer campaigns that abuse trusted services including Stripe, Google Tag Manager, and Google Firestore for command-and-control and data exfiltration. This activity reflects an ongoing pattern in which attackers embed malicious commerce infrastructure within services that defenders and customers are less likely to distrust by default.

The skimmer reporting sits adjacent to the Everest Forms Pro exploitation because both affect the broader web commerce and CMS ecosystem, where attackers can convert site compromise into credential theft, payment data theft, or persistent access. Together, these reports show continued pressure on web-facing applications, plugins, and third-party service integrations that sit directly in user transaction flows.
