Overview

Google’s Threat Intelligence Group reported a China-linked espionage campaign against North American medical, academic, and military research networks. Google attributes the activity with high confidence to UNC6508, a cluster it says targeted organizations in the United States and Canada. The affected organizations were described as clinical providers, academic centers, military health institutions, advocacy groups, and health regulators.

The campaign combined a REDCap server compromise with abuse of Google Workspace administration features. The attackers used a backdoor on REDCap research servers to steal credentials and move through victim environments. The most notable exfiltration method was the use of the victims’ own Google Workspace content compliance rules to copy selected emails to an attacker-controlled inbox.

Google said it notified affected organizations and disrupted the group’s infrastructure.

Actor and targeting

The activity is attributed by Google’s Threat Intelligence Group with high confidence to UNC6508. The article describes the group as China-linked and says both the actor and its REDCap backdoor had been surfaced earlier by Google in a broader report on state-backed attacks against the defense sector.

The victims were not named. Google described them as multiple organizations across the United States and Canada, including:

  • Clinical providers
  • Academic centers
  • Military health institutions
  • Advocacy groups
  • Health regulators

The article says the group remained inside North American medical, academic, and military research networks for more than a year while stealing sensitive research and defense-related email.

Initial access through REDCap

The entry point was REDCap, or Research Electronic Data Capture, a web platform used by hospitals and universities to build and manage study databases. UNC6508 compromised externally facing REDCap servers.

Google has not identified the initial access vector. The article states that Google did not name a specific CVE or list affected versions, although it observed the group probing older, vulnerable REDCap versions.

The earliest known compromise dates to September 2023, and activity continued through November 2025.

INFINITERED backdoor

Around three months after initial access, UNC6508 deployed custom malware that Google calls INFINITERED. The malware trojanizes REDCap’s own system files and performs three main functions.

INFINITERED:

  • Hijacks the REDCap upgrade process so each new version reinjects the malicious code instead of removing it.
  • Harvests usernames and passwords from the REDCap login page and stores them, encrypted, in local database tables.
  • Acts as a backdoor that receives commands through HTTP cookies and runs on every page load.

After gaining a foothold, UNC6508 conducted internal reconnaissance and credential discovery. The group pulled database and service account credentials, then used those logins to move into the internal network and eventually obtain a domain administrator account. Google did not disclose the exact path to the domain administrator account.

Email theft through Google Workspace rules

The exfiltration method used a legitimate Google Workspace admin feature rather than malware on the mail server or a separate exfiltration tool. UNC6508 abused content compliance rules, which can scan mail for keywords and copy or forward matching messages.

With administrator access, the attackers created a rule named “Patroit.” The rule monitored nearly 150 keywords, search terms, and email addresses. When a message matched the rule, Google Workspace silently BCC’d it to an attacker-controlled Gmail address.

Google has since disabled the attacker-controlled Gmail address.

The article emphasizes that this approach generated no separate mail-server malware, no standalone exfiltration tool, and no unusual network traffic. Instead, a built-in mail feature was turned into a way to copy organizational secrets to an inbox controlled by the attackers.

Collection priorities reflected in keywords

The rule’s keywords mapped to UNC6508’s stated collection priorities. The article lists the following areas reflected in the monitored terms:

  • Geo-strategic policy
  • Military strategy and equipment
  • Advanced technology, including AI and uncrewed vehicles
  • Offensive cyber programs
  • Medical research

One specific keyword stood out: “chikungunya,” the mosquito-borne virus behind a 2025 outbreak in China’s Guangdong province.

Technique significance

The article notes that MITRE already catalogs email-forwarding-rule abuse as a known technique. What Google Threat Intelligence Group highlighted as new in this case was the use of domain content compliance rules for the same purpose.

According to the article, GTIG said it had not previously seen this method used by a China-linked actor. The significance is that once attackers have administrator access, a built-in cloud email feature can become a quiet exfiltration path.

Recommended defensive actions

The article recommends starting with REDCap defenses. Organizations should patch externally facing REDCap servers and remove old versions outright rather than leaving them alongside the current build. REDCap allows legacy versions to run side by side, which the article says can enable downgrade attacks where an attacker forces software back to a known-vulnerable release.

On the mail side, defenders should review Google Workspace or equivalent content compliance and mail-forwarding rules. The article specifically recommends looking for rules that BCC or reroute mail to external addresses.

It also recommends checking admin audit logs for when rules changed, not only reviewing the current rule configuration. Defenders are advised to pull Google’s published indicators and hunt for INFINITERED. The article also recommends phishing-resistant MFA on administrator accounts because the mail-theft step depended on admin access.

Key takeaway

The campaign shows how a compromise of REDCap research infrastructure enabled credential theft, internal movement, and eventual abuse of Google Workspace administration features. The most important technical point is that UNC6508 used a legitimate content compliance rule to silently copy sensitive emails matching selected terms to an attacker-controlled Gmail account. The article concludes that defenders need to audit built-in cloud mail features, not only search for the REDCap backdoor.

  • The exfiltration path was difficult to spot because it used a legitimate Google Workspace admin feature rather than malware, a separate exfiltration tool, or unusual mail-server network traffic.
  • REDCap legacy versions can remain runnable alongside newer builds, so simply installing the latest version may not remove exposure to downgrade attacks or older vulnerable code.
  • The INFINITERED backdoor was designed to survive REDCap upgrades by hijacking the upgrade process and reinjecting itself into new versions.
  • Defenders need to review historical admin audit logs for content compliance and forwarding-rule changes, because the current rule configuration alone may not show when or how exfiltration was enabled.
  • The misspelled Workspace rule name “Patroit” and its nearly 150 monitored terms provide concrete hunting leads for identifying similar abuse of mail-routing rules.

Overview

Cybersecurity researchers reported malicious campaigns that show similarities to the persistent North Korea-linked threat cluster known as Contagious Interview. The cluster is also referred to in the article as Famous Chollima, HexagonalRodent, and Void Dokkaebi. One campaign, tracked by Proofpoint as UNK_DeadDrop, used developer role recruitment and code review themes to target individuals at nearly 100 organizations.

Proofpoint said the campaign affected organizations in finance, cryptocurrency, education, technology, and several other sectors. The activity involved more than 250 emails sent over a six-week period. More than 75% of the targeted entities were located in the United States, followed by the United Kingdom, Australia, France, Brazil, Germany, India, Israel, Japan, and the Netherlands.

UNK_DeadDrop campaign

Proofpoint described UNK_DeadDrop as a phishing campaign that began with emails containing links to actor-controlled GitHub repositories. These repositories hosted malicious scripts and were presented as technical assignments, cryptocurrency-related projects, or later as open-source projects that targets were asked to review. Recipients were instructed to clone the repositories and open them in VS Code or Cursor.

Once opened, the repositories could execute operating system-specific malware loaders for Linux, macOS, and Windows. The article identifies the use of Microsoft Visual Studio Code projects employing the “runOn: folderOpen” technique as an important connection to Pyongyang-linked activity. This technique causes malicious code to execute when a code editor opens a folder, without additional user interaction.

Proofpoint said Contagious Interview actors have used this approach since December 2025. The article notes that Proofpoint is tracking UNK_DeadDrop separately from Contagious Interview because of differences in initial access methods and malware tooling. Specifically, Proofpoint highlighted LinkedIn versus email as an initial access difference and noted the use of the open-source Overlord framework rather than the custom malware families traditionally associated with Contagious Interview, such as BeaverTail, InvisibleFerret, and OtterCookie.

Malware behavior and infection chains

The UNK_DeadDrop loaders differed by operating system. On macOS and Linux, the loader was a shell script. On Windows, the loader was a VBScript that ran a CMD file.

The loaders installed a malicious VS Code extension in VSIX format. The extension masqueraded as a legitimate Google service and communicated with an external server to enable remote command execution, system reconnaissance, and data exfiltration. Targeted data included browser wallet extension data, credentials, and desktop wallet application data.

The Linux and macOS infection chains led to a custom version of the open-source Go framework Overlord. This version included data theft capabilities and prompted users to enter their system password through a fake security pop-up. The Windows chain used VBScript and CMD to install the extension, exfiltrated stolen results to the server, performed cleanup, and terminated.

Proofpoint reported that the end goal was to steal credentials and wallet-related data and exfiltrate results to the server at 23.137.105[.]75:5173 using an HTTP POST request. The article states that, unlike the Linux and macOS agent, the Windows pipeline did not maintain a persistent connection. It uploaded ZIP files, cleaned up, and exited.

Proofpoint also found that the actor had previously distributed a Windows Go binary of Overlord. The article says the actor later shifted to the newer Windows method, likely in an attempt to avoid detection.

Significance of the developer-tooling abuse

The article emphasizes that developer tools are being used as malware delivery and execution channels. In UNK_DeadDrop, GitHub repositories, VS Code projects, Cursor, and VS Code extensions were central to the attack flow. The execution mechanism relied on normal developer workflows, such as cloning a repository and opening it in an editor.

Proofpoint characterized the activity as evidence that North Korea-aligned operations targeting developers for financial gain are maturing and evolving. The company said the shift from active social engineering over social media platforms for fake interviews to larger recruitment-themed phishing email campaigns may indicate industrialization and scaling of operations.

Malicious VS Code marketplace extensions

The article also reports findings from Yeeth Security about three malicious VS Code extensions discovered on the official marketplace. The extensions were named “ByteBinTools.jupyter-powerdev-2026.6.8.vsix,” “ToolCraft.jupyter-powertools-3.21.0.vsix,” and “OLDev.markdown-mode-devtools-2.1.0.vsix.” They were presented as Jupyter Notebook productivity tools but were described as a sophisticated, multi-stage backdoor designed to bypass endpoint defenses.

Yeeth Security said the malware included several components:

  • A SharePoint site used as a command queue, victim registry, and exfiltration channel.
  • A JavaScript layer for command-and-control communication through Microsoft Graph API and SharePoint.
  • Components for arbitrary file read, write, and exfiltration, as well as code execution through a Windows executable and a Python script for Linux and macOS.

The command-and-control channel supported commands and scripts, as well as a third command type called “host_action.” The host_action command enabled file system operations such as pwd, ls, cd, and cat, along with file uploads and downloads.

Yeeth Security did not identify direct overlap with any publicly documented North Korean campaign. However, the article says Yeeth Security observed echoes of Contagious Interview in the split between JavaScript and Python developer tooling, and similarities between the Microsoft Graph API authentication mechanism and Lazarus Group’s Dream Job attacks detailed by S2 Grupo LAB52 in October 2025.

Related North Korea-linked activity described in the article

The article places the UNK_DeadDrop and VS Code extension findings within a broader set of recent North Korea-linked campaigns and techniques.

Axios-related supply chain follow-up

The article describes a follow-up to the Axios supply chain attack that used three malicious npm packages: redeem-onchain-sdk@1.0.7, nicegui@0.1.4, and period-newline@0.1.0. These packages delivered an information stealer and exfiltrated harvested data to different command-and-control infrastructure. They were listed as dependencies on GitHub projects disguised as cryptocurrency trading bots.

OpenSourceMalware said the first secondary payload appeared on the registry less than 18 hours after the Axios malicious packages were removed from npm. The article quotes OpenSourceMalware as saying this suggested the threat actor had prepared backup infrastructure and could quickly deploy alternative delivery mechanisms.

TaskJacker

The article describes a campaign called TaskJacker that dropped malicious VS Code task files into GitHub users’ existing repositories. OpenSourceMalware said the campaign weaponized VS Code’s tasks.json auto-execution feature so that opening a cloned repository in an IDE could compromise a system. The article says this created worm-like spreading behavior.

Git hooks in coding assessment repositories

The article says Contagious Interview used Git hooks, specifically “.githooks/pre-commit,” to execute malicious code when a target cloned a “coding assessment” repository. This was described as a shift from hiding malicious code in .vscode/tasks.json or package.json files.

Compromised Packagist package

The article reports that Contagious Interview used a compromised Packagist package, “roberts/leads,” to target PHP developers. The package delivered a JavaScript malware loader that contacted blockchain and public RPC infrastructure to fetch, decrypt, and execute a next-stage JavaScript payload. The adversary also used access to compromised developer systems to tamper with commits and inject multi-stage obfuscated JavaScript code into source files in repositories.

The final payload in that activity was described as a variant of the DEV#POPPER RAT.

Downstream developer compromise

Trend Micro said Void Dokkaebi operations do not end with one infected developer. The article quotes Trend Micro explaining that compromised machines can become launchpads, with threat actors weaponizing the victim’s own repositories and turning code contributions into infection vectors for downstream developers. Trend Micro described this as resembling worm-like behavior rather than a traditional targeted attack.

InvisibleFerret and BeaverTail changes

The article says Contagious Interview migrated InvisibleFerret from readable Python scripts to Cython-compiled binaries. The malware was distributed as .pyd files on Windows and .so files on macOS. Trend Micro said this added evasion while preserving InvisibleFerret’s capabilities, including backdoor access, browser credential theft, clipboard monitoring, keylogging, and cryptocurrency wallet targeting.

The article also states that BeaverTail expanded beyond its original downloader and stealer role into broader malware with overlapping functions, including credential harvesting and wallet trojanization.

Malicious npm packages

The article describes multiple malicious npm package campaigns. One package, “terminal-logger-utils,” targeted Telegram data, SSH keys, crypto wallets, cloud configurations, and environment variables. It was published by “jpeek895,” an account also flagged for publishing a similar package called “terminal-logger-pack” in late April 2026.

Another npm package, “js-logger-pack,” delivered an ELF binary with infostealer and remote access trojan capabilities. A separate package, “node-env-resolve,” used six runtime dependencies matching the OtterCookie toolkit.

The article also describes a cluster of obfuscated malicious npm packages published by multiple throwaway accounts that delivered OtterCookie variants using a postinstall hook.

BlueNoroff and macOS targeting

The article discusses BlueNoroff, also known as Sapphire Sleet and UNC1069, targeting macOS environments in high-value financial sectors. The targeting involved social engineering against individuals in cryptocurrency, investment, and Web3. Some efforts used fake Zoom and Microsoft Teams meeting-themed lures, ClickFix-style prompts, and instructions to install supposed missing meeting SDKs.

These attacks led to deployment of updated variants of Cabbage RAT, also known as CageyChameleon, PowerShell implants capable of credential and data theft, or a macOS data-stealing toolkit called Mach-O Man. Microsoft said Sapphire Sleet persuaded users to manually execute AppleScript or Terminal-based commands, shifting execution into a user-initiated context and allowing activity to proceed outside macOS protections such as TCC, Gatekeeper, quarantine enforcement, and notarization checks.

Contagious Trader

The article says Contagious Trader used more than 50 malicious packages embedded across over 100 GitHub repositories to target cryptocurrency developers. These delivered PromptMink, OtterCookie, and a new Windows clipboard stealer called ClipViper. Panther said the repositories were promoted through verified accounts on X and Reddit, used spoofed developer identities and bot-inflated star counts, and were distributed across more than 40 GitHub users and organizations as redundant delivery fronts.

jsonspack and sleek-pretty

A supply chain campaign called jsonspack used 27 malicious npm packages to deliver a JavaScript RAT and infostealer or to drop a loader that fetched an unspecified payload. Another npm package, “sleek-pretty,” targeted developers running Polymarket trading bots. It performed system fingerprinting, SSH backdoor installation, filesystem exfiltration, and theft of Polymarket CLOB API credentials.

108-package npm campaign

The article describes a sustained npm malware campaign spanning 108 malicious packages and 261 package versions. It targeted developers between March 20 and April 20, 2026. Its goals included stealing credentials, Telegram Desktop sessions, and wallet keys, as well as establishing persistent access with malware families such as BeaverTail and OtterCookie.

Financial motivation and impact

The article frames the activity as North Korea-aligned developer targeting for financial gain. Expel said North Korea’s existing sanctions environment means financially motivated cybercrime differs from how it would affect most nation-states. Expel stated that there is little more that can be done to deter North Korea through sanctions, while the country has much to gain because its economic activity is severely constrained.

Expel also reported that North Korea-linked campaigns stole $12 million in cryptocurrency in the first three months of 2026. According to Expel’s Marcus Hutchins, the campaigns exfiltrated 26,584 cryptocurrency wallets from 2,726 infected developer systems.

Main takeaway

The article presents a picture of North Korea-linked actors increasingly abusing developer workflows and software supply chain mechanisms. The campaigns use GitHub repositories, VS Code configuration behavior, VS Code extensions, npm packages, Git hooks, Packagist packages, and social engineering lures to deliver credential stealers, wallet stealers, remote access tooling, and backdoors. Across the reported activity, developers and developer tooling are both the target and the delivery mechanism for broader compromise.

  • Opening a cloned repository in VS Code or Cursor can be enough to trigger compromise when malicious project configuration uses auto-execution behavior such as "runOn: folderOpen" or weaponized task files.
  • The campaign shows a shift from one-to-one social engineering on platforms like LinkedIn to higher-volume email phishing that distributes links to malicious repositories, which Proofpoint described as industrializing and scaling North Korea-aligned developer targeting.
  • The Windows infection chain appears designed for reduced exposure because it uploads stolen ZIP files, cleans up, and terminates instead of maintaining a persistent connection like the Linux and macOS agent.
  • Compromised developer machines are being used as propagation points, with threat actors weaponizing victims' own repositories and code contributions to infect downstream developers.
  • Legitimate collaboration and productivity platforms, including GitHub, VS Code, Cursor, SharePoint, and Microsoft Graph API, are being abused as delivery, execution, command-and-control, and exfiltration infrastructure.
