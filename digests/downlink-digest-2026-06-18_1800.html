The most important finding was that a second Microsoft Authenticator app had been set up for the compromised user without the user’s knowledge. Mitiga argues that the attack exposed weaknesses in Microsoft 365 MFA, Microsoft Authenticator, and Microsoft 365 Identity Protection. The article states that these weaknesses can allow full compromise of accounts even when MFA is enabled.

How the MFA Weakness Worked

Mitiga explains that Microsoft 365 checks the existing token in an active session to decide whether authentication is required. If the session was already properly authorized with MFA, Microsoft does not necessarily require a fresh MFA challenge. In sign-in logs, this can appear as: “Previously satisfied – MFA requirement satisfied by claim in the token.”

The article states that customers do not have flexibility to force MFA re-challenges for specific high-risk actions, other than controlling the duration of the MFA token. Mitiga says the Microsoft authentication engine decides when MFA is needed, which prevents customers from configuring stronger controls for these cases.

Security Info Registration as the Persistence Mechanism

The attack relied on access to the Security Info section of the account profile, where users can manage authentication methods. Mitiga states that Microsoft does not require an MFA re-challenge for accessing and changing user authentication methods in this section when the existing token is considered previously satisfied. This allowed the attacker to add a new Authenticator app without a new MFA challenge.

In the case Mitiga investigated, the attacker started a security info registration at 8:32:09 and completed it within five seconds. About half a minute earlier, at 8:31:27, the attacker had logged in to the “My Access” portal, where authentication details were edited. The article says the portal required MFA through Conditional Access, but the details showed that no new MFA prompt occurred because the requirement had already been satisfied by the token.

Mitiga emphasizes that even a strict MFA expiration time of one day would not prevent this technique. Once the attacker registers their own Authenticator app during the compromised session, they can later use it to authenticate again.

Privileged Identity Management Example

The article also highlights Microsoft Privileged Identity Management as a surprising example of the same broader issue. PIM is intended to let administrative users work with non-administrative rights and elevate only when needed. Mitiga states that Microsoft does not allow customers to require an MFA re-challenge for this activity, despite the risk of privilege elevation.

According to the article, if an account is compromised, the attacker can go to the PIM portal and activate administrator privileges themselves. Mitiga notes that the user will at least receive a notification that someone activated the privilege.

Microsoft Identity Protection Limitations

Mitiga notes that Microsoft Identity Protection identified some of the sign-ins as risky. However, the article says the default behavior is to require an MFA re-challenge. Mitiga argues that this is ineffective once the attacker has already set up their own Authenticator app.

This means a risk-based MFA prompt may no longer distinguish between the legitimate user and the attacker after the attacker has successfully registered an authentication method on the account.

Impact Described in the Article

The article describes the impact as persistent compromise of a Microsoft 365 account protected by MFA. The attacker gained access to an executive’s account and mailbox and registered a second Authenticator app. The BEC fraud attempt was prevented, but the access demonstrated that the attacker had obtained sensitive information from the compromised account.

Mitiga characterizes the broader impact as a weakening of MFA as a primary defense against identity attacks. The article says attackers have developed ways to overcome MFA through techniques such as adversary-in-the-middle phishing.

Recommendations

Mitiga recommends adding another layer of defense beyond MFA. Specifically, the article recommends a third factor tied to a physical device or to the employee’s authorized laptop and phone. It says Microsoft 365 can provide this through Conditional Access by requiring authentication from an enrolled and compliant device.

The article also recommends that organizations monitor for new authentication method registrations on accounts. It advises treating sign-ins from unexpected locations as potential indicators of compromise, rather than only responding with another MFA prompt. Mitiga states that requiring access through an enrolled and compliant device would completely prevent AiTM attacks in this context.

01 Microsoft 365 Identity Abuse and SaaS Supply-Chain Exposure

Mitiga detailed an attempted business email compromise campaign against a Microsoft 365 organization in which an executive account was accessed from multiple geographic locations, including Singapore, Dubai, and San Jose, California. The initial compromise used an adversary-in-the-middle phishing technique that gave the attacker access to the executive’s account and mailbox.

The notable persistence mechanism was the registration of a second Microsoft Authenticator app for the compromised user. Mitiga reported that Microsoft 365 did not require an MFA re-challenge when authentication methods were changed in the Security Info section if the existing session token had already satisfied MFA, allowing the attacker to weaken the account’s effective protection after initial access.

Jamf separately disclosed that a cybersecurity incident at third-party vendor Klue allowed an unauthorized party to access data in Jamf’s Salesforce instance through Klue’s integration. Jamf said its products and customer service operations were not affected, that it disabled the integration after notification, and that it had no evidence of lateral movement. The company warned that contact information stored in Salesforce could be used in phishing campaigns impersonating Jamf employees or IT professionals.

03 Surveillance Technology Exports and Dual-Use Controls

The Record reported on Human Rights Watch findings that Bulgaria approved export licenses from 2018 through 2023 allowing surveillance firm Circles to sell products to law enforcement and intelligence agencies in countries described as repressive. Approved destinations reportedly included El Salvador, the United Arab Emirates, Serbia, Azerbaijan, Guatemala, Bahrain, Jordan, Malaysia, Morocco, and Panama.

The tools identified in the report included Pixcell, which can capture internet data, phone calls, and messages; Landmark, which can geolocate mobile phones in real time; and Voice Over Location Enabler software, which can remotely intercept incoming and outgoing voice calls using the SS7 protocol. Human Rights Watch said the records cast the European Union’s export control regime in a harsh light, while Bulgaria stated that exports contradicting national, European, and international commitments are not allowed. The European Commission said member states remain responsible for dual-use export licensing decisions.
