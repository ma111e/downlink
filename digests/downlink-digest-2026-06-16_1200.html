Overview

Zimperium’s zLabs has documented a new Android banking trojan called Rokarolla. The article says the malware targets 217 banking and cryptocurrency apps and contains 137 remote commands, giving an operator what the report describes as near-total control over an infected phone. Its listed capabilities include stealing lock-screen credentials, reading and sending SMS, altering clipboard contents to redirect cryptocurrency transactions, and disabling Google Play Protect.

The malware is named after its command-and-control infrastructure. Zimperium did not attribute Rokarolla to any named threat group. The article frames the build as a banker specifically assembled to defeat protections users are typically told to rely on, from Play Protect to the Android lock screen.

Distribution and Initial Access

According to the article, Rokarolla spreads through malicious websites that impersonate familiar applications such as TikTok and Chrome. Victims are first tricked into installing a dropper that masquerades as Google Play Protect.

That fake Play Protect component serves two purposes described in the article:

  • it helps deliver and install the main malware payload
  • it is used to obtain Accessibility access

Once Rokarolla is active, one of its commands disables the real Google Play Protect, removing a built-in Android safety mechanism.

Credential Theft via Overlays

The article says Rokarolla’s main theft mechanism relies on HTML overlays. The malware receives a target list from its command-and-control server and downloads a corresponding fake HTML login page for each active app of interest. These pages are then stored in a local database on the device.

When a victim launches a legitimate banking or cryptocurrency app, Rokarolla overlays it with the fraudulent login page and captures anything typed into it. The article notes that this includes credentials and card details.

Zimperium’s report reportedly includes an example of an overlay impersonating the banking app imagin. A separate overlay imitates the Android lock screen, allowing the malware to collect the victim’s PIN, pattern, or password.

Device Takeover and User Monitoring

The article describes Rokarolla as extending well beyond simple banking credential theft. It can:

  • read all SMS messages on the device
  • send SMS messages itself
  • log keystrokes
  • log screen activity
  • scrape contacts
  • read notifications
  • manipulate the clipboard

The lock-screen overlay is particularly significant because it gives the operator a path to controlling the device even when it is locked. The article explicitly says this can allow operator control of the phone while locked.

SMS and Call Manipulation

SMS access gives Rokarolla the ability to collect one-time codes used by banks to approve logins and transactions. The article says this is enough to capture SMS-based verification codes commonly used in banking workflows.

In addition, the malware can make itself the device’s default app for texts and calls. The article says that by doing so it can block incoming calls, which could prevent a fraud-warning call from a bank from reaching the victim.

Cryptocurrency Theft Behavior

Rokarolla also targets cryptocurrency users through clipboard rewriting. The article says it silently swaps copied wallet addresses with attacker-controlled addresses, so a payment copied by the user can be redirected to the wrong account.

This is presented as one of the trojan’s direct monetization paths alongside banking credential theft and SMS interception.

Surveillance Method

For screen surveillance, Rokarolla does not use the more visible MediaProjection-based screen casting mechanism. The article explains that MediaProjection would generate a visible recording prompt that could alert the user.

Instead, Rokarolla uses Accessibility to take screenshots, compresses them as PNG, and exfiltrates them one frame at a time. The article says this snapshot approach is quieter and simpler than the hidden live VNC method seen in malware families such as Klopatra.

Command-and-Control Resilience

The malware is described as having multiple fallback C2 domains and the ability to receive new domains dynamically. According to the article, this means taking down a single command-and-control server would do little to stop the operation.

The article also compares Rokarolla with another Android banking trojan, HOOK, stating that Rokarolla’s 137 commands exceed the 107 commands Zimperium counted in HOOK.

Broader Context

The article places Rokarolla within a broader wave of 2026 Android bankers described by Zimperium. It says the same playbook has been appearing across these campaigns:

  • fake-app droppers
  • Accessibility abuse
  • HTML overlay-based credential theft

This context presents Rokarolla not as an isolated anomaly, but as part of a repeating Android banking malware pattern documented by the researchers.

Impact and Risk Framing

The article portrays Rokarolla as highly capable because it combines several forms of access and deception into one workflow:

  • initial infection through fake app websites
  • installation aided by a fake Play Protect interface
  • Accessibility privilege abuse
  • banking and wallet overlays for credential capture
  • lock-screen overlays for PIN theft
  • SMS interception for one-time codes
  • call blocking to suppress warnings
  • clipboard manipulation for cryptocurrency theft
  • screenshot-based surveillance

Together, these functions give the operator broad visibility into the device and the ability to interfere with both user actions and defensive notifications.

Response and Defensive Guidance

The article says there is no patch for Rokarolla because it is malware rather than a product flaw. The defenses recommended are standard anti-banker precautions for Android:

  • install apps only from Google Play
  • keep Play Protect enabled
  • treat unexpected Accessibility requests as a major warning sign

The article emphasizes that Accessibility permission is central to the attack chain.

Detection and Attribution

Zimperium says its own security products detect Rokarolla. It also states that indicators of compromise are available in the company’s GitHub repository.

Attribution remains unresolved in the article. Zimperium did not connect Rokarolla to a named threat actor or group.

Bottom Line

The article describes Rokarolla as an Android banking trojan built for broad device compromise rather than narrow credential theft alone. Its combination of fake Play Protect installation, Accessibility abuse, banking and lock-screen overlays, SMS interception, clipboard hijacking, and quiet screenshot surveillance makes it a comprehensive mobile theft platform aimed at banking and cryptocurrency users.

Overview

These loops generate effectively endless valid file paths and can cause tools that scan directories recursively, including EDR products, to follow the loop and never complete the scan. According to the article, this leaves malicious files in the same folder unexamined.

The article frames junctions and symbolic links as file system features that are legitimate but often overlooked by security teams. It emphasizes one property in particular: any user can create them. No administrator privileges are required; only write access to the target folder is needed.

How the article explains NTFS junctions

The article says a junction is a type of NTFS reparse point that redirects one directory to another. It provides a command example using mklink /J to create a directory junction. Once created, applications see the target directory’s contents as if they were local to the junction path.

It also notes an important practical constraint: classic Windows systems impose a 260-character maximum path length. Although NTFS itself can support much longer paths, and the limit can technically be extended to 32,767 characters through a registry change, many applications and utilities still do not handle paths beyond 260 characters correctly. The article says this path length limitation determines how deep the recursive loops can go and how many unique paths GhostTree can produce.

GhostBranch: the simpler recursive loop

The article first introduces GhostBranch as the simpler version of the technique. In this setup:

  • A parent folder contains a file such as program.exe.
  • A child folder is created inside the parent.
  • That child folder is configured as a junction that points back to the parent directory.

The article’s example is:

C:\Parent\program.exe
mklink /J C:\Parent\Child C:\Parent

This creates what the article calls a logical loop. Because the child points back to the parent, the child appears to contain everything in the parent, including the child itself. As a result, many different path strings all resolve to the same executable, such as:

  • C:\Parent\Child\Program.exe
  • C:\Parent\Child\Child\Program.exe
  • C:\Parent\Child\Child\Child\Child\Program.exe

The article says this means an attacker can keep adding Child to the path and it will still remain valid.

GhostTree: adding branching to recursion

The article then describes GhostTree as an extension of GhostBranch. Instead of creating one child junction, the attacker creates multiple child folders, each of which points back to the parent. The example uses two child junctions:

mklink /J C:\Parent\Child1 C:\Parent
mklink /J C:\Parent\Child2 C:\Parent

Now each level in the path can branch through either Child1 or Child2, but both still lead back to the parent. The article gives examples such as:

  • C:\Parent\Child1\Program.exe
  • C:\Parent\Child2\Program.exe
  • C:\Parent\Child1\Child1\Program.exe
  • C:\Parent\Child1\Child2\Program.exe

According to the article, this transforms a simple recursive loop into a branching path structure resembling a binary tree.

Path calculations in the article

The article compares the path generation potential of GhostBranch and GhostTree under Windows path length constraints.

GhostBranch calculations

To maximize depth under the 260-character path limit, the article proposes:

  • A single-letter folder such as P
  • An executable named 1.exe
  • A path structure like C:\P\P\P\...\1.exe

It says this allows approximately 126 unique directory structures before path length limits are reached.

GhostTree calculations

For GhostTree, the article introduces two one-letter folder names, P and B, and paths such as:

  • C:\B\1.exe
  • C:\P\B\1.exe
  • C:\P\B\P\B\...\1.exe

2^126 ≈ 8.5 × 10^37

The article emphasizes the scale of that number by comparing it to the estimated number of grains of sand on Earth and the number of atoms in a human body.

01 Platform Abuse, Fraud, and Identity Controls

India temporarily blocked Telegram nationwide ahead of the rerun of the NEET-UG medical entrance exam and also required the service to disable message editing in the country for a limited period. Authorities said scammers had used Telegram channels, groups, and bots to falsely claim they had advance access to exam papers, prompting takedowns despite the agency stating that no paper was actually leaked. The move underscores how encrypted and high-reach messaging platforms continue to be treated as fraud infrastructure during high-stakes public events.

In the UK, proposed rules to bar under-16s from social media would likely require age verification during account creation through ID submission or facial age scans. The plan would reportedly extend beyond traditional social media to additional services and features, including livestreaming and stranger contact on gaming platforms. Critics cited in the reporting argue the model is both easy to bypass and likely to create new privacy and identity-theft risks by centralizing sensitive identity and biometric data.

The FTC’s latest fraud figures add financial context to this broader platform-abuse trend. Americans reportedly lost \(3.5 billion to imposter scams in 2025, with more than \)2.1 billion of those losses traced to social media; Facebook alone accounted for more losses than text and email combined. Bank security impersonation remained especially costly, while business and government impersonation scams each drove close to or above $1 billion in losses.

02 Malware Tradecraft and Detection Evasion

Researchers described GhostTree, a Windows evasion technique that abuses recursive NTFS junctions to generate effectively limitless valid paths to the same file. A simpler variant, GhostBranch, loops a child junction back to its parent, while GhostTree expands this into a branching structure with an enormous number of possible path combinations. The reporting says the technique can be created by any user with write permissions and does not require administrator privileges.

The significance of GhostTree is less the filesystem trick itself than what it says about defensive blind spots. Researchers tested the method against Windows Defender and reported it to Microsoft, with the issue later patched after an initial position that the behavior did not cross a security boundary. For detection engineers, the case illustrates how path parsing and file resolution edge cases remain a viable avenue for malware concealment and scanner bypass.

03 Mobile Banking and Cryptocurrency Theft

Zimperium detailed a new Android banking trojan, Rokarolla, targeting 217 banking and cryptocurrency applications and supporting 137 remote commands. The malware is delivered through malicious websites masquerading as popular apps such as TikTok and Chrome, using a dropper disguised as Google Play Protect to install the payload and obtain Accessibility permissions. That installation chain reflects the continued effectiveness of fake security prompts and sideloading lures in mobile malware operations.

Once installed, Rokarolla uses downloaded HTML overlays to impersonate legitimate banking and wallet applications and harvest credentials, payment card data, and even device unlock secrets such as PINs and patterns. The malware can read and send SMS, intercept calls by making itself the default communications app, scrape notifications and contacts, log keystrokes and screen activity, and replace copied cryptocurrency wallet addresses in the clipboard. The article also notes resilient command-and-control design, including multiple fallback domains, dynamic domain updates, and the ability to disable Google Play Protect after compromise.
