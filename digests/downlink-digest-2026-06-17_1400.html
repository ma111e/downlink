Overview

A newly discovered data leak dubbed “FortiBleed” exposed what appears to be Fortinet and FortiGate VPN credentials for 73,932 firewall URLs at organizations worldwide. The data was first discovered by security researcher Bob Diachenko, who said he found a server containing apparently valid Fortinet VPN credentials. The exposed data included usernames, email addresses, and plaintext passwords.

According to screenshots and information shared by Diachenko, the dataset included entries for major organizations such as Chevron, Samsung, Foxconn, Comcast, AT&T, Mercedes-Benz, Toyota, Sinopec, State Grid, and others. The exposed data also contained comments listing each organization’s industry, revenue, and number of employees, which the article says was likely for planning attacks.

Reported Campaign Details

Diachenko described the incident as a “Massive Fortinet/FortiGate bruteforce/active exploitation campaign” in a LinkedIn post. He said thousands of top vendor instances were listed in the files and noted that one file alone contained 21,634 domain names. He also said the data included potentially working passwords to FortiGate appliances obtained through various means.

Diachenko later shared additional information claiming that the operation was conducted by a Russian-speaking multi-operator threat group. He said the group harvested credentials for FortiGate SSL VPN devices.

According to Diachenko’s investigation, the attackers allegedly conducted:

  • Approximately 1.16 billion credential attempts against 320,777 FortiGate targets.
  • An additional 2.1 billion attempts against 163,650 Microsoft SQL Server systems.

Diachenko further claimed that the threat actors intercepted SSL VPN authentication hashes, cracked them with a 45-GPU cluster managed through Hashtopolis, and used recovered credentials to move laterally into internal Active Directory environments.

Exposed Attacker Infrastructure and Files

Diachenko told BleepingComputer that he obtained additional details by analyzing files inadvertently exposed on the same server. He said the operators accidentally left an open directory containing artifacts, connection strings, tooling, scripts, and data. He also said analytics from cron jobs, bash histories, and logs were exposed.

The researcher stated that multiple organizations in Japan, Taiwan, Vietnam, Iraq, and Turkey were fully compromised. He also said a Turkish NATO defense contractor was among the affected organizations and that classified documents were allegedly stolen from it.

Hudson Rock Analysis

Threat intelligence company Hudson Rock later published its own analysis after receiving the dataset from Diachenko. Hudson Rock described the collection as one of the largest known troves of compromised Fortinet-related credentials.

According to Hudson Rock, the dataset contains:

  • 73,932 unique firewall URLs.
  • Affected systems across 194 countries.
  • 21,632 unique impacted domains.

Hudson Rock said the attackers maintained detailed logs of successful compromises and assembled a database of verified credentials for organizations across nearly every major industry sector. The company said organizations appearing in the dataset include Foxconn, Samsung, Comcast, Siemens, Lenovo, PwC, Accenture, Oracle, government agencies, and critical infrastructure operators.

Hudson Rock also released statistics showing that the highest number of affected devices was in India, the United States, Taiwan, Mexico, Turkey, Thailand, Colombia, Malaysia, Chile, and the United Arab Emirates. It said the most common sectors among listed companies were telecommunications, IT services, financial services, government organizations, healthcare providers, educational institutions, and manufacturing.

Unusual Password Findings

The article notes that one unusual aspect of the leak is that many exposed credentials were long, complex passwords. These would ordinarily be considered difficult to crack. The article does not identify a confirmed method by which those credentials were originally obtained.

Beaumont Review and Configuration Data Findings

Cybersecurity researcher Kevin Beaumont independently reviewed portions of the exposed data and told BleepingComputer that some of the credentials are authentic. He said he was able to confirm the authenticity of some admin logins and passwords and described the material as a real dump.

After reviewing data shared by Hudson Rock, Beaumont published additional findings indicating that the dataset contains credentials for roughly 75,000 Fortinet devices. He said most of those devices remain online.

Beaumont said the data appears to have originated from exported Fortinet configurations. He based this on the presence of information, including email addresses, that is typically accessible only through configurations.

He also said the affected IP addresses differ from those in the 2025 Belsen Group Fortinet leak. According to the article, this further indicates that FortiBleed is a more recent and larger collection of compromised devices.

Beaumont said multiple organizations listed in the dataset were using valid credentials. He also observed that many affected devices were running relatively recent FortiOS versions.

Internet Exposure and Scope

Based on Shodan network data, Beaumont said the leak contains approximately half of all internet-accessible Fortinet firewalls. He also said that a majority of the affected devices expose their FortiGate management interfaces directly to the internet.

The article states that the source of the configuration data remains unknown. It is unclear whether the data was stolen through previously disclosed Fortinet vulnerabilities, a newly discovered flaw, or another method. Diachenko, Hudson Rock, and Beaumont have not identified how the configuration data was originally obtained.

Recommended Actions

The article says Hudson Rock created a free FortiBleed lookup tool for organizations to check whether they are impacted. It also says organizations in the dataset should immediately take response steps.

Recommended actions include:

  • Rotating passwords associated with Fortinet VPN and administrative interfaces.
  • Enforcing MFA.
  • Examining gateway logs for suspicious activity.
  • Monitoring for exposed employee credentials.

Delivery and lure infrastructure

The campaign uses ClickFix-style social engineering. Victims are directed to compromised or attacker-controlled websites and instructed to copy and execute a Terminal command manually. Netskope observed three landing page variations:

  • A fake macOS utility page branded as “StellarScan Solutions.”
  • A fake GitHub repository page that mimics a trusted code repository layout.
  • A localized IT support page masquerading as a macOS support and IT services provider based in Berlin.

All active variants served the same JavaScript file, /links/page-loader.js, across active lure domains. The script fetched the payload command from links/data.txt and populated it into a #cmd DOM element. When the victim clicked #copyBtn, the page used the Web Clipboard API to write the execution string to the user’s clipboard.

The JavaScript contained Russian inline comments. The article connects this with the loader’s CIS geofencing behavior and revisits it in relation to attribution.

Fileless execution chain

The infection chain is designed to remain fileless until persistence is installed. The clipboard command fetches a zsh script embedded as a gzip-compressed, base64-encoded heredoc and immediately evaluates the content. This keeps the decoded stage 1 loader in memory and avoids writing it to disk.

The stage 1 loader performs several steps before destructive behavior:

  • It checks the macOS keyboard input source list from ~/Library/Preferences/com.apple.HIToolbox.plist.
  • It searches for a Russian keyboard layout.
  • If Russian input is enabled, it sends a cis_blocked event to the C2 and exits silently.
  • It sends a telemetry beacon before fetching the next payload.
  • It fetches the AppleScript stage with a spoofed Chrome user-agent and pipes it directly into osascript.

The telemetry beacon is sent to hxxps://qwqerrqwr2145qw[.]com/api/debug/event. It includes the victim’s external IP address, locale details, hostname, OS version, CIS status, and the build hash 3447ad192726ee391881be6e86c7eeab.

Stage 2 payload: “Meow (DEBUG)”

The second-stage AppleScript payload is internally labeled “Meow (DEBUG).” The article states that it was not seen on VirusTotal at the time of discovery. It executes through osascript entirely in memory.

The payload first attempts to obtain the user’s login password through a spoofed macOS system dialog. The dialog impersonates System Preferences, uses the legitimate LockedIcon.icns system icon, and asks the user to enter a password to “update settings.” The password is validated using dscl . authonly with the username and entered password, and the payload retries up to 10 times before giving up.

If a valid password is obtained, it is written to the staging directory and used to unlock the login keychain. The payload then extracts Safe Storage keys for Chrome, Brave, and Microsoft Edge, including behavior described as relevant beginning with macOS Tahoe 26.

Data theft capabilities

The payload targets a wide range of browsers and data stores. The article lists Chrome, Brave, Edge, Opera, OperaGX, Vivaldi, Orion, Sidekick, Chrome Canary, Chromium, Chrome Dev, Arc, Coccoc, Chrome Beta, Firefox, and Safari.

For Chromium profiles, it copies items including Login Data, Cookies, Network/Cookies, Web Data, Local Extension Settings, and IndexedDB. For Firefox, it copies cookies.sqlite, logins.json, key4.db, cert9.db, places.sqlite, and formhistory.sqlite. For Safari, it copies Cookies.binarycookies, Form Values, and History.db.

The payload also targets cryptocurrency-related data. It collects data associated with more than 100 Chromium cryptocurrency wallet extension IDs, including MetaMask. It also expands the standalone desktop wallet collection to 25 wallets and copies wallet directories that can contain active wallet files, transaction histories, seed phrases, or private keys left in configuration files.

Additional targeted data includes:

  • Telegram Desktop tdata directories, including 16-character hexadecimal subdirectories and key_datas.
  • Discord Local Storage/leveldb directories across discord, discordcanary, discordptb, and discorddevelopment.
  • Steam loginusers.vdf, config.vdf, and ssfn files.
  • Files from ~/Desktop and ~/Documents to a depth of three directories, including document, credential, key, JSON, RDP, and PNG file types.
  • Apple Notes data from NoteStore.sqlite and associated media attachments up to 30MB total.
  • The full ~/Library/Keychains/ directory.

Collected data is staged under /tmp/shub_<7-digit-random>/ and compressed to /tmp/shub_log.zip using ditto. The archive is posted to https://qwqerrqwr2145qw[.]com/gate with an API key and build ID listed in the article.

Wallet application injection

After exfiltration, the malware attempts to modify desktop cryptocurrency wallet applications. It targets Exodus, Atomic Wallet, Ledger Wallet, Ledger Live, and Trezor Suite.

For each targeted wallet, the injection sequence checks whether the application is installed, downloads a zip archive from the C2 gate, extracts a replacement app.asar file, kills the running application with pkill -9, overwrites the application bundle’s app.asar, and performs ad-hoc re-signing. The endpoints named in the article include /exodus-asar, /atomic-asar, /ledger-asar, /ledgerlive-asar, and /trezor-asar.

The re-signing uses codesign -f -d -s to force an ad-hoc signature. The article states that this restores a structurally valid signature so the modified bundle can launch cleanly without triggering native Gatekeeper warnings. Netskope states that it did not have the contents of the injected app.asar files and makes no claims about their specific behavior beyond what the injection code shows.

Persistence and remote access

The malware installs persistence after exfiltration. The persistence method depends on whether a valid password was obtained.

If a valid password is obtained, the malware writes a LaunchDaemon to /Library/LaunchDaemons/com.apple.accountsd.plist and places a service binary at /Library/Application Support/.com.apple.accountsd/.service. If no valid password is obtained, it falls back to a user-level LaunchAgent at ~/Library/LaunchAgents/com.apple.accountsd.plist.

In both cases, the plist label is com.apple.accountsd, matching Apple’s legitimate account synchronization daemon name. The StartInterval is set to 60 seconds.

The persistence script posts heartbeats to the C2 endpoint /api/bot/heartbeat. Each beacon includes the host’s IOPlatformUUID as the bot identifier, along with build ID, hostname, IP, and OS version. If the C2 response contains a code field, the payload base64-decodes it, writes it to /tmp/.c.sh, executes it, and deletes it, giving the operator arbitrary command execution on the host.

The article states that the lure domains share the same registrar admin contact email, which also ties the two C2 domains qwqerrqwr2145qw[.]com and scope-quest[.]com to the same operator.

The domain naming clusters include file* domains and cyberdrift, stellar, and tensorforge naming patterns. The article also identifies meadowfileengine[.]com and pa2373[.]com as outliers, and crystalfilehaven[.]com as a fake-GitHub variant.

Notable forensic indicators identified in the article include:

  • /Library/Application Support/.com.apple.accountsd/.
  • /tmp/shub_ staging directory patterns.
  • /tmp/.c.sh temporary command execution pattern.
  • Lure delivery through /links/page-loader.js and links/data.txt.

ThreatLabz stated that it observed multiple instances of typosquatting domains hosting malicious content generated with AI-powered website creation tools. The article emphasizes that threat actors are using website builders to create convincing lures quickly and at scale. In this campaign, the lure progressed from a fake CAPTCHA to a fullscreen fake BSOD and system recovery prompt designed to pressure victims into running a PowerShell command.

ThreatLabz noted that Trend Micro had previously written about the same malware family under the name Banana RAT, but with a different attack path.

AI-generated lure and anti-inspection behavior

The fraudulent bank page contained source-code comments that ThreatLabz assessed as likely generated by an AI tool. The comments included generic section headers with a templated structure that is commonly seen in AI-generated webpages where sections are labeled for later developer integration.

The page also contained anti-inspection measures. Its script intercepted keyboard shortcuts for DevTools, Console, Inspector, and page source viewing by using capture-phase keydown handlers and calling preventDefault() and stopPropagation(). Every three seconds, the script logged a crafted Image object whose getter triggered console.clear(), repeatedly wiping the console while DevTools was open.

ClickFix infection flow

After the victim clicked the fake CAPTCHA, the page copied a malicious PowerShell command to the clipboard, placed the browser into fullscreen mode, and displayed a fake BSOD system recovery page. A lockdown routine attempted to keep the victim trapped in the tab or window, restrict keyboard input, and enforce fullscreen mode.

The lockdown routine first attempted to use navigator.keyboard.lock(). It then registered a capture-phase keydown handler that blocked most keystrokes while temporarily allowing the keystrokes needed for the ClickFix flow, including Win+R, Ctrl+V, and Enter. It also listened for window.blur and repeatedly called window.focus() to regain focus if the victim switched away.

To enforce fullscreen, the script checked fullscreen state every 50 milliseconds and called requestFullscreen, with fallbacks to webkitRequestFullscreen and mozRequestFullScreen. The PowerShell command copied to the clipboard included a random number of trailing spaces, which ThreatLabz noted was likely intended to bloat the payload and/or evade detection because a single added space changes the hash value.

Payload delivery chain

The copied PowerShell command downloaded and executed the next stage from 64[.]95[.]13[.]238:

  • It retrieved st.txt from the hardcoded IP address.
  • st.txt functioned as a stealth PowerShell dropper.
  • The dropper used Windows API calls to hide its console window.
  • It downloaded payload.php from the same IP address.
  • It saved the downloaded content as C:\Users\Public\Documents\msedge.txt.
  • It immediately executed the saved script as a script block.

The payload.php file contained another PowerShell script. This script suppressed errors, decoded hardcoded Base64 strings to obtain an AES key and IV, decrypted an AES-CBC encrypted blob, and executed it with ScriptBlock::Create(). The decrypted blob was the PowerShell RAT that ThreatLabz named SmartRAT.

SmartRAT purpose and configuration

SmartRAT is a Brazil-focused banking RAT implemented entirely in PowerShell and identified by the embedded string SMART_V25. Its stated objective is remote access and financial data theft using features such as fake bank-branded password forms, keylogging, and QR code interception.

SmartRAT decrypts two C2 configurations:

  • The primary C2 domain is c[.]windowsupdate-cdn[.]com, decrypted using XOR with the key 2.
  • The fallback C2 is 162[.]141[.]111[.]227, decrypted using XOR with the key 233.
  • The malware communicates on port 51888.

SmartRAT hides its PowerShell window using user32.dll’s ShowWindow function. It writes debug logs to C:\ProgramData or %APPDATA%\Microsoft\Diagnosis\ETW\client_debug.log, with a fallback to %TEMP%\clientdebug.log. It also creates a per-process log at C:\ProgramData\Microsoft\Diagnosis\ETW\process.log to record RAT activity.

Identity, encryption, and C2 security model

SmartRAT generates a unique identity token by hashing the machine GUID, MAC address, UTC ticks, a newly generated GUID, and the computer name with SHA-256. It stores the token in etw.dat and install.token.

It then computes an HMAC-SHA256 of the token using the hardcoded master key iuhbdaubdvauygd5562\(3@##\)r. The same hardcoded master key is used in two ways: the raw UTF-8 bytes of the plaintext key are used as the HMAC secret, while the SHA-256 hash of the string is used to derive the 32-byte AES encryption key.

C2 command traffic is encrypted and decrypted with AES-CBC. The encryption function generates a fresh IV for each message using GenerateIV(), hex-encodes the IV and ciphertext separately, and returns them as a colon-delimited string. The decryption function splits the colon-delimited value, hex-decodes the IV and ciphertext, and decrypts the command with the same AES key.

Persistence and privilege strategy

SmartRAT checks whether it is running as LocalSystem by comparing the current Windows identity SID to S-1-5-18, or whether it was launched with the -ServiceMode flag. If either condition is true, it connects to the C2 immediately. Otherwise, it performs persistence and elevation steps.

The malware copies itself to %APPDATA%\Microsoft\Diagnosis\ETW\msedgeupdate.txt. It attempts to create a logon-triggered scheduled task named MicrosoftEdgeUpdateCore. If scheduled task creation fails, it writes a MicrosoftEdgeUpdateCore value under HKCU\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run to re-execute the malware at user logon.

SmartRAT then prompts for UAC elevation. If elevation is approved, it compiles inline C# service code using csc.exe and installs a Windows service named MicrosoftEdgeUpdateCore under %ProgramData%\Microsoft\Diagnosis\ETW\. The service is configured to run with System privileges, and a watchdog checks every five seconds to ensure the SmartRAT PowerShell process continues running.

If UAC elevation is denied, SmartRAT does not create the Windows service. Instead, it launches a hidden PowerShell process that bypasses the UAC logic and beacons to the C2, while the scheduled task can prompt for UAC elevation again at the next logon.

The malware also compiles a C# component that uses DuplicateTokenEx and CreateProcessAsUser to spawn a new PowerShell process in the current user’s session, even when the RAT is running as SYSTEM.

Supported command-line parameters

SmartRAT supports command-line parameters for installation, service control, cleanup, and execution mode. The article lists the following parameters:

  • -InstallService installs and starts the MicrosoftEdgeUpdateCore Windows service.
  • -UninstallService stops and deletes the Windows service and its executable.
  • -Uninstall removes persistence, including scheduled tasks, registry keys, and files.
  • -Reinstall uninstalls and then reinstalls SmartRAT.
  • -ServiceMode runs SmartRAT as a service and verifies internet connectivity by resolving google.com before execution.
  • -ServiceStatus displays the current status of the service and scheduled tasks.
  • -ScriptPath defines the source file location for installation.
  • -Force kills other PowerShell instances except itself and deletes lock PID files.

SmartRAT outputs the string SMART_V25 with the current timestamp as confirmation that it executed successfully.

Embedded C# components and victim interaction

Before connecting to the C2, SmartRAT compiles and loads several embedded C# classes into memory:

  • NativeInput handles mouse and keyboard input, including freezing victim input.
  • WinEUpjgHelper captures the screen using BitBlt, although the article states this class is compiled but not invoked at runtime.
  • WindowMonitor retrieves the foreground window title and process name.
  • InputTracker acts as a high-priority keylogger.
  • IdleDetector tracks user inactivity with GetLastInputInfo.
  • QRDetector detects QR codes using pixel pattern analysis.
  • DisplayOverlay renders fullscreen fake overlays such as Windows Update, BSOD, and bank-branded security screens.
  • QROverlay displays fake overlays with bank branding.

SmartRAT enumerates connected monitors to map screen coordinates and resolutions. It calls SetProcessDpiAwareness from shcore.dll to bypass DPI scaling and obtain true physical pixel values. The malware stores monitor results in a global array so the operator can select a monitor index and align overlays and screen captures accurately.

Financial activity monitoring

SmartRAT monitors window titles for keywords associated with banks, payment platforms, credit unions, cryptocurrency exchanges, and generic banking terms. The monitored terms include Santander, Bradesco, Itau, Caixa, bb.com.br, bancodobrasil, Nubank, Inter, C6 Bank, Safra, BTG, Sicoob, Sicredi, Mercado Pago, PicPay, PagSeguro, PayPal, Binance, Mercado Bitcoin, bank, and banco.

When a foreground window title matches the watchlist, SmartRAT logs the title, keyword, process name, and timestamp. It sends the information to the C2 as a BrowserAlert message with type 0x80. The article states that this alerts the operator that the victim is interacting with a financial application.

After receiving this alert, the operator can issue a dataEntry command containing bank-specific branding parameters, including name, color palette, prompt text, and input length. SmartRAT can then launch a fullscreen overlay such as a bank verification prompt, and the captured information is exfiltrated to the C2.

C2 communication and message handling

SmartRAT attempts to connect to its C2 server indefinitely. If domain resolution fails, it falls back to the hardcoded IP address. Once connected, it communicates over a raw TCP socket on port 51888 using a binary-framed message format.

During connection and setup, SmartRAT sends or handles these message types:

  • ClientHello, type 0x01, sends version string 7.3 to the server.
  • GuestInfo, type 0xE6, sends victim profile JSON including OS, username, host, privilege, session ID, install token, and HMAC.
  • Session negotiation uses message types 0x06, 0xE0, and 0xE1.
  • Monitor List, type 0x14, sends monitor layout information.

After connecting, SmartRAT enters a continuous loop. It pauses screen capture after more than 20 minutes of inactivity and resumes when activity returns. It also processes up to 20 C2 packets per main loop iteration.

QR code interception workflow

SmartRAT includes QR auto-detection functionality. When enabled by the C2 with the AutoQRToggle command, the client scans all connected monitors every three seconds using a heuristic pixel-contrast and clustering algorithm to locate QR-code-shaped regions.

When it detects a QR region, it captures the full monitor as a JPEG, computes the QR bounding box, and sends the screenshot, region coordinates, monitor offset, and a deduplication hash to the C2 using the QRCodeDetected packet. The article states that in QR-swap workflows, the C2 performs the QR decoding server-side.

The C2 can then respond with a ShowQROverlay command containing an attacker-supplied QR image. The client renders the QR image as a borderless TopMost window at the exact pixel coordinates of the original QR, effectively replacing the legitimate banking QR with the threat actor’s QR. The overlay remains until removed by the HideQROverlay command, and failed-decode regions are blacklisted for 30 to 60 seconds to avoid repeated transmissions.

Censys analyzed AdaptixC2, an open-source post-exploitation command-and-control framework, and found that its default configuration makes deployed infrastructure straightforward to identify through passive internet scanning. The framework’s default HTTP error responses include branded headers and a distinctive 404 page body, both visible without authentication.

As of 16 June 2026, Censys tracked 412 web properties across 236 hosts running AdaptixC2 with default or near-default settings. The article stresses that these hosts do not all necessarily represent malicious activity, because some may be authorized red team infrastructure. The central finding is that the infrastructure exists, is detectable, and can be monitored or blocked by defenders using the described fingerprints.

AdaptixC2 Context

AdaptixC2 is a public post-exploitation framework with a Go-based teamserver and a C++/Qt GUI client. It supports two agent families:

  • Beacon, a C++ implant supporting BOF execution across Windows, Linux, and macOS.
  • Gopher, a Go implant supporting async BOF execution across the same platforms.

The framework implements listeners as loadable plugins, or “extenders,” covering HTTP/S, DNS/DoH, SMB named pipes, and raw TCP transports. Its teamserver exposes REST and WebSocket APIs for operator control, including credential management, agent tasking, screenshots, tunnel management, and multi-operator collaboration.

Core Fingerprint

The main detection signal comes from AdaptixC2’s default profile.yaml. Its error handler sets branded headers on unmatched routes:

  • Server: AdaptixC2
  • Adaptix-Version: v1.2

Because these headers are configured in the error handler rather than a specific route, they appear on arbitrary unauthenticated requests that do not match the configured endpoint. Requests to paths such as /, /robots.txt, /favicon.ico, or other arbitrary paths can return the branded 404 response.

The default 404 body provides a second independent signal. The article identifies the default body string:

You need to enter the correct connection details.

This body string remains useful when operators modify headers, and Censys notes that a “TestCoonection” header-modified variant was still detectable through the unchanged 404 body.

Teamserver and Listener Architecture

AdaptixC2’s teamserver uses a configured endpoint path, defaulting to /endpoint, and exposes multiple route groups beneath that path. The article explains that routes outside the endpoint prefix, or invalid paths inside it, hit the NoRoute handler and return the default branded 404 response.

The HTTP beacon listener behaves differently from the teamserver API. The BeaconHTTP extender starts its own separate HTTP server on the operator-configured callback port rather than using the teamserver’s public endpoint group. This distinction explains why Censys observed two populations:

  • Teamservers, often on port 4321, used for operator API access.
  • Beacon listeners, often on high ports such as 43211, used for agent callbacks.

Both surfaces can return the default 404 page, but they serve different operational purposes.

Authentication and Exposed Surface

The article states that the teamserver management API is reasonably secured when the password is set correctly. Operator authentication begins with POST /endpoint/login, which returns a 12-hour JWT access token and a 168-hour refresh token if the credential is correct. Authenticated operators can generate one-time OTP tokens for WebSocket upgrades and file transfers.

The unauthenticated surface is limited to branded error responses and login or refresh attempts. Failed authentication attempts return the same branded 404 response as invalid routes. This prevents clients from distinguishing invalid credentials from nonexistent paths, but it also exposes the framework identity on the first unauthenticated probe.

DNS Listener Signal

The BeaconDNS extender provides a tertiary detection signal. It sets the authoritative-answer bit on every response and returns TXT "OK" for any query that does not match its beacon protocol format, specifically queries with fewer than five labels. Censys captures this as a DNS service with version: "OK" on UDP/53.

The article warns that this signal requires cross-referencing because many legitimate DNS servers use custom version strings. On genuine listeners, a short random hostname returns TXT "OK", while a beacon-check-in-style query with five or more labels returns A 127.0.0.1.

The article also discusses KharonHTTP, a third-party AdaptixC2 listener extension found on two hosts in the dataset. KharonHTTP implements a malleable C2 profile system with configurable URI paths, headers, User-Agent patterns, request body wrappers, and cookie or parameter encoding.

Censys provides several detection methods. The simplest is the Censys threat label:

Header-based detections include:

  • Server: AdaptixC2
  • Adaptix-Version: v1.2

Body-based detection uses the default 404 body string:

You need to enter the correct connection details.

The DNS listener signal is:

host.services.dns.version: "OK" and host.services.protocol = "DNS"

The article states that Censys threat detection THREAT-0210 covers the default headers, body hash, and the “TestCoonection” evasion variant.

Key Takeaways

The most important conclusion is that AdaptixC2’s default branding makes passive detection trivial. Because the identifying headers are applied by the error handler, no valid credentials or endpoint knowledge are needed to fingerprint many deployments.

The second major takeaway is that single-signal detection is incomplete. Operators who changed headers may still leave the default 404 body intact, so the article recommends using both header-based and body-based detection.

The third important point is that clustered listener infrastructure can be identified through shared operational artifacts. Adjacent IP allocations, high callback ports, and reused default OpenSSL certificates helped Censys identify three beacon listener clusters likely representing coordinated, redundant callback infrastructure.

Finally, the article notes that external visibility depends on transport choice. BeaconHTTP, BeaconDNS, and BeaconTCP expose internet-reachable sockets, while BeaconSMB does not create an externally visible teamserver socket because the named pipe lives on a compromised victim host.

  • Layered detection is necessary because at least one operator deliberately changed the `Server` header to `TestCoonection` while leaving the default 404 page body intact, so body-based matching catches evasion that header-only scans miss.
  • Beacon listener clusters can be separated from teamservers by their high callback ports and shared TLS certificate patterns, which helps analysts distinguish operator API infrastructure from agent callback infrastructure in passive scan data.
  • The default OpenSSL certificate value `C=AU, ST=Some-State, O=Internet Widgits Pty Ltd` appeared across multiple AdaptixC2 listener clusters, making TLS certificate reuse an actionable clustering signal for identifying related callback servers.
  • BeaconDNS deployments may be visible even when BeaconHTTP is absent because the DNS extender returns `TXT "OK"` for short or unrecognized queries and sets the authoritative-answer bit on every response.
  • Recovered AdaptixC2 Beacon binaries can reveal C2 servers, callback URIs, and listener RC4 keys through static extraction because the encrypted configuration blob stores the RC4 key inside the binary itself.

01 ClickFix Malware Campaigns and AI-Enabled Phishing

Zscaler ThreatLabz reported a March 2026 ClickFix campaign using an AI-generated typosquatting site impersonating a Brazilian bank to deliver SmartRAT. The lure presented a fraudulent Cloudflare CAPTCHA followed by a fullscreen fake BSOD recovery prompt, pressuring victims into executing a PowerShell command that downloaded the malware. SmartRAT provides encrypted C2, remote screen and input control, keylogging, fake bank overlays, QR code interception, and persistence via scheduled tasks and a Windows service.

SmartRAT is financially focused, monitoring window titles associated with Brazilian banking, payment, and cryptocurrency activity and alerting operators when victims interact with targeted services. ThreatLabz also found a weakness in the SmartRAT web C2 panel in which client-side authentication could be bypassed by setting arbitrary localStorage values.

Netskope Threat Labs separately described a macOS ClickFix campaign observed on May 31, 2026 that delivered an upgraded AppleScript payload labeled “Meow (DEBUG).” The infection chain remained fileless until persistence, using a victim-executed curl command to fetch a compressed stager and pipe AppleScript into osascript memory. The payload checks for Russian keyboard input, steals credentials, cookies, keychain contents, crypto wallet data, messaging sessions, files, gaming data, and Apple Notes, then persists as com.apple.accountsd for recurring C2 beacons and arbitrary command execution.

04 C2 Infrastructure Exposure and Post-Compromise Access

Censys analyzed AdaptixC2, an open-source C2 framework whose default configuration exposes identifiable HTTP headers such as Server: AdaptixC2 and Adaptix-Version: v1.2 on unauthenticated error responses. As of 16 June 2026, Censys tracked 412 web properties across 236 hosts using default or near-default AdaptixC2 settings. The research identified beacon listener clusters hosted by Hivelocity, M247, and PSB Hosting, as well as one host serving AdaptixC2 payloads from an open HTTP directory.

The exposed directory included a Linux installer that deployed timesync.bin and established 12 persistence mechanisms. Censys noted that even operators who changed the default Server header could remain detectable if they left the default 404 response body string intact.
