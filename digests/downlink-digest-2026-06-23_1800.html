The article describes a large operation called FortiBleed, linked to a Russian-speaking group, that targeted more than 430,000 FortiGate firewalls and other internet-facing business systems around the world. The attackers broke into devices, quietly collected login details passing through them, and may have gathered more than 110 million credentials, especially affecting small and medium businesses in places like the United States and India. Summary
A Russian-speaking initial access broker is linked to FortiBleed, a large credential-harvesting campaign that compromised FortiGate firewalls and other internet-facing systems to collect more than 110 million credentials. The operation used mass scanning, brute forcing, and a custom FortigateSniffer tool to steal cleartext credentials and hashes for resale, lateral movement, and further intrusions.

A Russian-speaking initial access broker driven by financial gain is assessed to be behind FortiBleed, a large-scale credential-harvesting operation targeting more than 430,000 FortiGate firewalls globally. The campaign has been active since February 2026 and involves collecting credential lists, searching for exposed services, brute-forcing accessible systems, and deploying custom sniffers on compromised firewalls. SOCRadar said the sniffers capture cleartext and hashed credentials from traffic passing through compromised devices, which are then cracked, validated, and reused against Active Directory domains and other exposed services.

Central to the operation is FortigateSniffer, a Golang-based tool that uses the FortiOS built-in “diagnose sniffer packet” command to passively capture authentication traffic from infected appliances. The tool monitors traffic across 24 protocols and extracts credentials from services such as Kerberos, SMB, LDAP, RDP, WinRM, MS-SQL, MySQL, PostgreSQL, and RADIUS. The attackers are estimated to have launched at least 659 credential-harvesting pipelines on May 31 and June 15, 2026, identifying more than 110 million credentials, including RADIUS credentials, NTLM hashes, Kerberos hashes, and MySQL authentication tokens.

SOCRadar said FortiBleed appears to be part of a broader multi-vendor initial access operation that also targets Synology NAS, Sophos firewalls, RDWeb portals, Citrix SSL-VPNs, and MS-SQL servers using automated brute-forcing. The campaign focuses heavily on small and medium businesses with fewer than 200 employees, with notable emphasis on the United States and India and a key focus on the IT services sector. The operation includes target ranking by economic value, geofencing to specific IP ranges, activity limited to Moscow working hours, and use of stolen credentials for lateral movement, Active Directory enumeration, data exfiltration, and persistent authenticated access.

  • The actors ranked targets by economic value before allocating exploitation resources, indicating that the campaign was not purely indiscriminate despite relying on mass scanning and brute forcing.
  • The campaign focused heavily on small and medium businesses with fewer than 200 employees, and IT services providers were a key target because compromising them could create access paths into customer environments.
  • The sniffing activity included operational controls such as geofencing to specific IP ranges and limiting activity to between 7 a.m. and 6 p.m. Moscow Time.
  • Some username and password pairs were repeated across thousands of distinct IP addresses, which Zenox said raises the possibility that the accounts were planted by the attacker as clandestine backdoor access.
  • The FortiGate-related operation ran in five-hour cycles with 1,000 simultaneous validation threads, and early cycles reportedly had a successful validation rate near 90%.

Cisco released security updates for the flaw on June 3 and warned that successful exploitation could allow attackers to gain root privileges on affected devices. The issue affects Cisco Unified Communications Manager and Cisco Unified Communications Manager Session Management Edition.

Cisco said the vulnerability is caused by improper input validation for specific HTTP requests. An unauthenticated remote attacker could exploit it by sending a crafted HTTP request to an affected device, allowing files to be written to the underlying operating system for later privilege escalation. Threat intelligence firm Defused reported that exploitation was observed over the weekend and originated from a single IP address using properly constructed file:// payloads.

Defused said the observed proof-of-concept activity appeared to be reconnaissance, attempting to write a file named /tmp/cve-2026-20230-test.txt to vulnerable devices. SSD Secure later published a technical write-up and proof-of-concept exploit explaining how the Webdialer component’s handling of user-supplied URLs can be abused for arbitrary file writes.   • SSD Secure said exploitation requires the target system’s hostname, but its write-up showed that attackers can retrieve that information from the device before carrying out the file-write attack.
  • The exploitation observed by Defused appeared to be reconnaissance rather than full compromise, but a technical write-up and proof-of-concept exploit were published after the activity was disclosed.

In plain words

Microsoft released an optional Windows 11 update for versions 24H2 and 25H2 that starts adding a new way to quickly return a PC, including apps, settings, and personal files, to how it was at an earlier time. This matters because it can help people and organizations recover faster when a computer problem happens, without needing long troubleshooting or technical skill. The update also fixes several bugs, including one in the Recycle Bin, but Microsoft says one known problem remains where some third-party apps may not be able to open Microsoft Office apps or documents directly.

Summary
Microsoft’s optional Windows 11 KB5095093 preview update for versions 24H2 and 25H2 begins rolling out a new Point-in-Time Restore feature that can roll back the full PC state using recent restore points. The update also includes many fixes and improvements, including a Recycle Bin filename bug fix, though a known issue with third-party apps launching Office documents remains.

Microsoft released the KB5095093 preview cumulative update for Windows 11 24H2 and 25H2. The update is part of Microsoft’s optional monthly non-security preview update schedule, which tests fixes and features ahead of the next Patch Tuesday. It can be installed through Windows Update or downloaded manually from the Microsoft Update Catalog.

The update begins rolling out Point-in-Time Restore, a feature that lets users restore a Windows PC to the state it was in at an earlier point in time. Microsoft says it can restore the operating system, applications, and files in minutes using restore points stored locally with Volume Shadow Copy Service. Consumer restore points are created every 24 hours and kept for up to 72 hours or until allocated storage runs out, while enterprise licenses can configure shorter or longer snapshot and retention intervals.

KB5095093 also includes numerous fixes and improvements, including a fix for a Recycle Bin issue that could show an internal file name instead of the original file name when permanently deleting a file. Other changes cover areas such as Secure Boot certificate rollout, authentication, networking, the emoji panel, widgets, File Explorer, Bluetooth, printing, WSL, graphics, accessibility, and general reliability. Microsoft says a known issue remains where third-party applications may be unable to launch Microsoft Office applications or open documents, and affected users should open the application or document directly.

  • Point-in-Time Restore is limited to recent recovery because restore points are kept for a maximum of 72 hours, so it is designed for fast rollback from recent issues rather than long-term recovery.
  • Enterprise-licensed systems have more control than consumer systems because they can configure restore point capture and retention intervals at 4, 6, 12, 16, or 24 hours.
  • Because KB5095093 is an optional non-security preview update, users normally must choose “Download and install” unless they have enabled “Get the latest updates as soon as they're available.”
  • Increasing the storage space allocated to Point-in-Time Restore can allow more frequent snapshots without them being deleted because of low storage.
  • Affected users who encounter the remaining Office launch issue should open the Office application or document directly instead of launching it through the affected third-party software.
Key Points
  • 01Microsoft released the optional KB5095093 preview cumulative update for Windows 11 24H2 and 25H2 as part of its monthly non-security preview update schedule.
  • 02The update begins rolling out Point-in-Time Restore, which lets users restore a Windows PC, including apps, settings, and personal files, to a recent automatic restore point.
  • 03For consumers, Point-in-Time Restore creates new restore points every 24 hours and deletes them after 72 hours or when allocated storage space runs out.
  • 04KB5095093 fixes a known Recycle Bin issue where the confirmation dialog could show an internal file name instead of the original file name when permanently deleting a file.
  • 05Microsoft says a known issue remains where third-party applications may be unable to launch Microsoft Office applications or open documents, and affected users should open the application or document directly.

Summary
Silent Push has partnered with UK-based CDL Cyber Intelligence to offer preemptive threat intelligence that identifies attacker infrastructure before campaigns reach targets. The partnership focuses on giving UK organizations earlier warning against state-linked and other threats using Silent Push’s Context Graph and Traffic Origin capabilities.

Silent Push has entered a strategic partnership with CDL Cyber Intelligence, an intelligence-led cybersecurity firm serving organisations across the UK and Europe. The article says CDL’s clients include organisations across government, financial services, healthcare, and critical infrastructure.

Silent Push says capable threat actors often register infrastructure, age domains, and set up hosting weeks or months before targeting organisations. Its Context Graph tracks how infrastructure is created, managed, and connected across DNS, WHOIS, certificates, and hosting data, then surfaces clusters as Indicators of Future Attack when they match adversary operational patterns.

It also says the UK faces sustained attention because it is a NATO member, a major financial centre, and a jurisdiction with significant government and critical infrastructure targets. Silent Push highlights Traffic Origin as another relevant capability, saying it identifies the true upstream country of origin controlling a connection even when the observed IP looks clean.

  • The partnership is positioned around CDL’s ability to interpret and act on raw intelligence, not just deploy security tools, which is presented as necessary for using Silent Push’s early-warning data effectively.
