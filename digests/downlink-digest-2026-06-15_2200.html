Overview

British Prime Minister Keir Starmer said the UK intends to block children under 16 from using social media. The government described the plan as a landmark child online safety move and said it would become the strongest set of children’s online safety measures in the world once implemented.

The UK government plans to bring legislation before Christmas. It expects the ban to be enforced by spring 2027.

Scope of the proposed ban

The ban is intended to cover “user-to-user platforms” whose purpose is to enable social interaction and that allow users to post material alongside algorithms. The article names the following platforms as subject to the ban:

  • TikTok
  • Facebook
  • Instagram
  • Snapchat
  • X
  • YouTube

Messaging platforms such as WhatsApp are not included, according to the cited government press release.

Additional restrictions beyond social media access

The UK proposal goes beyond a blanket social media ban. The government also plans restrictions on functions it describes as harmful, including:

  • Livestreaming involving children under 16
  • Stranger communication with children under 16

The article says these restrictions will apply to a wider range of online services, including gaming sites.

AI-related restrictions are also included. AI romantic companion chatbots and “intimate functionalities” on other chatbots will be restricted for children under 18.

Age assurance requirements

The UK plans to require “highly effective age assurance” methods. The article says these methods are intended to go beyond those used in Australia.

The UK’s communications regulator has been tasked with designing the age assurance measures. It is due to report back to the central government with a plan by October.

The government is also expected to announce additional measures next month. These may include overnight curfews and required breaks in infinite scrolling for teens under 18.

Comparison with Australia

The UK plan is modeled after a similar social media ban that took effect in Australia last December. However, the UK government says its approach will go further by adding safeguards and broader restrictions.

The article cites a study released in March by Australia’s eSafety Commissioner. According to that study, Australia’s social media ban has not succeeded at keeping large numbers of Australian youth off regulated platforms.

The study found that surveyed parents reported that nearly half of children had an account on a regulated platform before the ban. After the ban took effect, 31.3% still had an account on at least one regulated platform.

More than two-thirds of Australian children who still maintained accounts reportedly did so because tech firms had not asked them to verify their age.

Public consultation and government rationale

The UK has conducted pilot programs to test certain restrictions and held a consultation process inviting public input. The government press release said nine in 10 of 116,000 surveyed parents backed a ban.

Starmer said he had heard directly from families calling for change and said the government would act on those concerns. He described the package as more comprehensive than a blanket ban because it responds to how children experience harm online, rather than only where that harm occurs.

Technology Secretary Liz Kendall said tech companies had been given many chances to reform their practices and had failed to do so. She said that failure made the ban necessary.

International context

The article places the UK proposal within a broader international movement toward youth social media restrictions. Several countries are described as having implemented or considered similar bans.

Spain’s Prime Minister Pedro Sanchez said in February that his government would ban social media for children under 16. He described social media as a “digital Wild West” and said it had become a “failed state” where laws are ignored and crimes are tolerated.

The Netherlands’ new minority government said in February that it would seek a ban for children aged 14 and younger. French lawmakers are working on legislation to ban children under 15 from social media. Malaysia and Turkey have bans in place for children under 16 and under 15, respectively.

Criticism and concerns

Some human rights and civil liberties organizations are critical of the UK’s approach. Kerry Moscogiuri, chief executive of Amnesty International UK, called it “the right diagnosis but the wrong prescription.”

Amnesty International UK argued that the problem is not that children are present on social media. Instead, it said social media companies have built platforms that are unsafe by design, and that the remedy should be regulating platforms rather than excluding children.

Privacy risks are also identified as a key issue in the public debate. Joe Jonas, director of research and insights at the IAPP, said privacy will be a major factor because of the role of age assurance in the ban.

Jonas also said Starmer’s political weakness could make it difficult for the government to advance the ban. He said political uncertainty around the current government may affect whether the initiative can move forward.
