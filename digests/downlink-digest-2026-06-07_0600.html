Overview

The article presents a Security Strategist podcast conversation between Richard Stiennon, Chief Research Analyst and founder of IT-Harvest, and Rob Gurzeev, CEO of CyCognito. The topic is external attack surface management and why it remains difficult for organizations to understand and manage their internet-facing exposure.

Gurzeev’s central argument is that many organizations believe they understand their security posture because they run penetration tests, invest in tools, and build teams, but breaches can still occur through assets that no one knew existed. He says this gap is what CyCognito was built to close.

Background and Perspective

Gurzeev says he did not choose attack surface management so much as it “chose” him. He describes growing up around IRCs and prank-style exploitation among peers, which he characterizes as an early form of penetration-testing experience.

He later joined an intelligence agency at age 18, where he worked on what cybersecurity now calls attack surface management. In that context, he focused on finding the path of least resistance into systems to acquire relevant intelligence. He describes the work as fascinating and like puzzle solving, but also stressful because real lives were on the line.

This background shaped his view of the difference between attacker and defender perspectives. He says much of the cybersecurity industry has built layers of defense based on deploying tools and knowing where assets are. By contrast, he says his training emphasized that defenders do not always know reality and must look for the path of least resistance.

Why Organizations Still Miss External Assets

Gurzeev argues that organizations often focus on known assets and established KPIs rather than asking where their security posture is most likely to fail. He says security and IT teams may not be rewarded for telling boards that existing processes or technologies cover only a small part of the real environment.

He gives the example of an organization spending $2 million on manual penetration testing while only testing 1 percent of its internet-facing applications. He says a security leader may not be rewarded for telling a mostly non-technical board that 99 percent of those applications were not tested.

The article frames this as an organizational and technical problem. Gurzeev says that attacker-oriented work focuses on the path of least resistance, while defenders are often focused on building systems, completing projects, and meeting KPIs.

Growth of the Modern Attack Surface

Gurzeev says the complexity of enterprise attack surfaces has grown significantly. He contrasts the environment 20 years ago, when Apple and Google each had one main website or one internet-connected thing, with modern large companies that may have between 100,000 applications, devices, and cloud assets exposed to the internet.

He says CyCognito’s largest customer has 100 million internet-connected items that external attackers can interact with. He notes that manual penetration testing may cover 100, 500, or perhaps 1,000 web applications, leaving open the question of what happens to the rest.

He attributes part of the growth to the increased ease of building applications and changing network configurations. Twenty years ago, he says, one person in a company might have had the authority to make major network changes. Now, he says, people in departments such as HR and finance can use tools like Claude Code or Lovable to assemble sensitive information, build applications, and expose them to the internet either intentionally or accidentally.

External Attack Surface Management Versus Vulnerability Management

The article distinguishes between vulnerability management and external attack surface management while noting that they are adjacent. Gurzeev says vulnerability scanning and management focus on mapping potential vulnerabilities. Attack surface management, in his description, focuses on mapping, classifying, and understanding what an organization has.

He says the internal and external views are significantly different. For internal attack surface management, he says teams can use integrations with sources such as ServiceNow, endpoint protection tools, and AWS instances, with deduplication and presentation being major challenges. In that case, he says, the data is there.

External attack surface management is described as much harder because no one knows the full reality. Gurzeev gives examples such as:

  • A third party building a marketing campaign application with the company’s logo and customers’ PII.
  • A company having 100 subsidiaries, each with its own technology stack.
  • Subsidiaries that may not cooperate with the global security team.
  • A joint venture in China or an engineer somewhere creating assets outside central visibility.

He says that between 10 and 50 percent of applications, devices, and cloud assets may not be known by any technology or database when CyCognito begins working with an organization. He emphasizes that only one unknown or undermanaged asset is needed to enable a breach.

Recall, Precision, and Blind Spots

Gurzeev uses recall and precision to explain the difficulty of external attack surface management. Recall refers to how much of the real asset base is found. Precision refers to how accurate the attribution is.

He explains that finding 99 of 100 assets gives 99 percent recall, but claiming one million assets are owned by the organization when only 100 are truly owned would create poor precision. He says balancing recall and precision is very hard.

He criticizes what he calls the “lazy approach” used by many vendors and technologies: asking teams what they already know about their attack surface and then finding adjacent assets. He acknowledges that this has value, but says the greatest risk lies in complete blind spots that no one knows about.

AI and the Changing Remediation Timeline

The article says AI is reshaping attack surface discovery, exposure management, and remediation expectations. Gurzeev says there is now consensus that AI changes the conversation on mean time to remediation, which needs to be measured in hours rather than days.

He says many companies still take months to remediate even critical issues. He also says that even a person who is not highly technical can now exploit applications and complex systems within minutes.

This makes it more important, in his view, for organizations to know what is exposed and to quickly determine where relevant assets are when a new emerging threat appears. He says he hears from customers multiple times per week about code-red emerging threats.

Gurzeev also says CyCognito is spending time expanding deterministic security testing capabilities into AI-enabled creative testing at scale. He describes deep testing of one asset at a time as almost a solved problem with AI, while continuously testing all assets within budget remains the next major problem to solve.

Main Recommendation for Security Leaders

Gurzeev’s closing advice to CISOs and CIOs is to examine mean time to remediation for whatever their organization defines as critical issues. He asks leaders to be honest about whether remediation occurs within a couple of hours, a few days, or a few weeks.

He then recommends asking whether current efforts, processes, and technologies can reduce remediation time to one or two hours. If not, he suggests organizations should consider whether they need a different approach.
