The Amazon Simple Email Service is experiencing a significant increase in malicious abuse, allowing threat actors to distribute convincing phishing emails that evade standard detection mechanisms.

While the platform has been leveraged for attacks before, the current spike correlates with a large volume of AWS Identity and Access Management access keys exposed in publicly accessible locations.

Kaspersky researchers have documented this trend, noting that attackers are using the service to deliver links that redirect victims to malicious websites.

The primary catalyst for this abuse is the automated discovery of credentials through tools like TruffleHog, which scan GitHub repositories, .ENV files, Docker images, backups, and S3 buckets.

Threat actors have streamlined the entire attack lifecycle by automating secret scanning, permission validation, and email distribution.

This automation enables them to dispatch massive volumes of phishing messages without manual intervention.

The resulting campaigns feature high-quality custom HTML templates that replicate real service interfaces and realistic login flows.

Specific attack vectors include fake document-signing notifications that direct users to AWS-hosted phishing pages, as well as advanced business email compromise schemes.

Perpetrators fabricate entire email threads and generate fake invoices to trick finance departments into processing fraudulent payments.

Since Amazon SES inherently passes authentication checks, attackers no longer need to worry about SPF, DKIM, or DMARC compliance.

Furthermore, traditional mitigation strategies like blocking offending IP addresses are ineffective because doing so would disrupt all legitimate traffic routed through the service.

To combat this, researchers advise implementing least-privilege IAM policies, enabling multi-factor authentication, rotating keys frequently, and applying IP-based access restrictions.

AWS has responded by pointing to its official security guidance on exposed credentials and encouraging users to report suspicious activity to AWS Trust & Safety.

EXEC Executive Overview

This digest synthesizes threat intelligence reported during the May 4, 2026 coverage window, highlighting a pronounced shift toward abusing legitimate cloud infrastructure and remotely managed tools to bypass traditional security controls and establish persistent access. Two distinct but thematically linked campaigns illustrate how threat actors are increasingly leveraging trusted services to evade detection, compromise credentials, and maintain long-term operational continuity.

The first trend centers on the automated exploitation of leaked AWS credentials to abuse Amazon Simple Email Service (SES) for large-scale phishing operations. Automated bots continuously scan publicly accessible repositories, including GitHub and S3 buckets, to harvest exposed access keys. Once obtained, these credentials enable attackers to route malicious emails directly through Amazon SES, effectively bypassing standard email authentication mechanisms such as SPF, DKIM, and DMARC. This infrastructure abuse supports highly sophisticated phishing campaigns that mimic legitimate platforms like DocuSign and facilitate business email compromise through fabricated invoices. The volume and sophistication of these messages indicate a mature, automated delivery pipeline designed to maximize reach while minimizing detection by email gateways.

Concurrently, the VENOMOUS#HELPER campaign has impacted over 80 organizations, predominantly across the United States, through a targeted phishing initiative impersonating the Social Security Administration. Rather than relying on traditional malware, this campaign delivers disguised executables that install SimpleHelp and ConnectWise ScreenConnect as persistent Windows services. Both remote monitoring and management (RMM) tools are configured with self-healing watchdogs to ensure continuous operation. The threat actors leverage legitimately signed RMM binaries to circumvent antivirus and signature-based detection, while simultaneously exploiting built-in privilege escalation mechanisms to achieve SYSTEM-level access. This architecture provides fully interactive desktop environments and active screen monitoring capabilities. Notably, the campaign employs a redundant dual-channel access model, deploying both SimpleHelp and ScreenConnect to maintain operational resilience should one platform be remediated.

Together, these incidents underscore a broader operational trend: the strategic pivot from disruptive payloads to the covert integration of legitimate, enterprise-grade tools into adversary infrastructure. By abusing compromised AWS credentials for email delivery and repurposing signed RMM software for persistence and lateral movement, threat actors are effectively neutralizing perimeter defenses and authentication checks. The convergence of automated credential harvesting, cloud service abuse, and dual-channel RMM deployment reflects a maturation in adversary tradecraft, prioritizing stealth, redundancy, and sustained access over immediate impact. These campaigns collectively demonstrate how threat actors are systematically exploiting trusted third-party ecosystems to maintain long-term footholds and evade conventional detection methodologies.
