Poland busts SIM-swapping gang tied to millions in crypto theft

In plain words

Police in Poland arrested four people accused of taking over victims’ phone numbers and email accounts so they could get into cryptocurrency exchange accounts and steal money. The people affected were account holders whose phone messages, emails, and crypto accounts were hijacked, with losses estimated at millions of U.S. dollars. The case matters because it shows how criminals can turn control of a phone number into access to financial accounts and then move stolen funds through banks and digital wallets across countries.

Summary
Polish authorities arrested four alleged members of a SIM-swapping cybercrime gang accused of breaching telecom partners and hijacking email accounts to steal millions from cryptocurrency exchange accounts. The operation was supported by the FBI and HSI, and the suspects face up to 25 years in prison.

Authorities in Poland arrested four members of an organized cybercrime group accused of breaching telecommunications partners and hijacking email accounts to carry out SIM-swapping attacks. The operation was conducted by the Polish Cybercrime Bureau with support from the FBI and Homeland Security Investigations in the United States. Investigators said the suspects used specialized software and social engineering to gain unauthorized access to infrastructure connected to telecommunications operators and employee email accounts.

The suspects allegedly hijacked victims’ phone numbers, intercepted SMS messages and email communications, and gained control of accounts at cryptocurrency exchanges. Authorities estimate that millions of U.S. dollars were stolen and then laundered through a distributed financial network. The Polish Cybercrime Bureau said the total value of funds laundered in this manner exceeds several tens of millions of Polish złoty, or at least $5 million based on the current exchange rate.

Polish authorities said the actors treated the activity as a regular source of income and used multiple bank accounts across various countries and digital wallets to transfer stolen funds. The four arrested individuals were placed in pre-trial detention and face offenses including participation in an organized criminal group, hacking into IT systems to commit theft, and money laundering. The maximum penalty for these offenses is 25 years in prison.

  • The group allegedly gained the data needed for phone-number takeovers by accessing entities that cooperated with telecommunications operators, showing that partner organizations were part of the attack path.
  • The suspects combined phone-number hijacking with intercepted SMS messages and email communications to gain control of cryptocurrency exchange accounts.
  • Polish authorities described the activity as a regular source of income for the suspects, with stolen funds moved through multiple bank accounts in various countries and digital wallets.
  • Although Polish authorities did not name the arrested suspects, blockchain investigator ZachXBT identified one alleged member from images released after the police raid.

Anthropic appears to be testing Claude Cowork support on mobile, which would allow users to manage long-running Claude tasks from a phone. Claude Cowork is described as a desktop-focused agentic mode for Claude that brings some of Claude Code’s task-running abilities to regular knowledge work. Unlike Claude Code, which is optimized for coding and development tasks, Cowork can work on longer tasks, use files, create documents, generate spreadsheets, write reports, and continue in the background while users monitor progress.

The article says screenshots posted on X suggest Anthropic is preparing a mobile experience for Cowork. The screenshots indicate users would be able to start and steer tasks directly from a phone, and check in from a phone, browser, or Claude desktop app. Anthropic also notes in the screenshots that work continues in the background even when the app is closed.

The article states that Claude Cowork on mobile does not appear intended to turn the phone into a full desktop-style agent platform. Instead, it appears to work more like a remote control for Cowork on a PC, with the computer still doing the heavy lifting. Anthropic has not publicly announced full mobile Cowork support, but the screenshots suggest the feature is being prepared inside Claude for mobile.

  • Claude Cowork’s mobile support appears designed to extend access to desktop-running tasks rather than move file access and task execution onto the phone.
  • The feature could let users manage ongoing work across phone, browser, and desktop app while relying on the computer that has access to the selected local files.
  • Because Cowork can continue working after the mobile app is closed, users may need to think of mobile sessions as controlling persistent work rather than a task that stops when they leave the app.
  • The article’s storage-cleanup example shows Cowork can be applied to local system maintenance tasks, not only document, spreadsheet, or report generation.
