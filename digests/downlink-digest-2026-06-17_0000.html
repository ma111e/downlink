Overview

A newly discovered data leak dubbed “FortiBleed” exposed what appears to be Fortinet and FortiGate VPN credentials for 73,932 firewall URLs at organizations worldwide. The data was first discovered by security researcher Bob Diachenko, who said he found a server containing apparently valid Fortinet VPN credentials. The exposed data included usernames, email addresses, and plaintext passwords.

According to screenshots and information shared by Diachenko, the dataset included entries for major organizations such as Chevron, Samsung, Foxconn, Comcast, AT&T, Mercedes-Benz, Toyota, Sinopec, State Grid, and others. The exposed data also contained comments listing each organization’s industry, revenue, and number of employees, which the article says was likely for planning attacks.

Reported Campaign Details

Diachenko described the incident as a “Massive Fortinet/FortiGate bruteforce/active exploitation campaign” in a LinkedIn post. He said thousands of top vendor instances were listed in the files and noted that one file alone contained 21,634 domain names. He also said the data included potentially working passwords to FortiGate appliances obtained through various means.

Diachenko later shared additional information claiming that the operation was conducted by a Russian-speaking multi-operator threat group. He said the group harvested credentials for FortiGate SSL VPN devices.

According to Diachenko’s investigation, the attackers allegedly conducted:

  • Approximately 1.16 billion credential attempts against 320,777 FortiGate targets.
  • An additional 2.1 billion attempts against 163,650 Microsoft SQL Server systems.

Diachenko further claimed that the threat actors intercepted SSL VPN authentication hashes, cracked them with a 45-GPU cluster managed through Hashtopolis, and used recovered credentials to move laterally into internal Active Directory environments.

Exposed Attacker Infrastructure and Files

Diachenko told BleepingComputer that he obtained additional details by analyzing files inadvertently exposed on the same server. He said the operators accidentally left an open directory containing artifacts, connection strings, tooling, scripts, and data. He also said analytics from cron jobs, bash histories, and logs were exposed.

The researcher stated that multiple organizations in Japan, Taiwan, Vietnam, Iraq, and Turkey were fully compromised. He also said a Turkish NATO defense contractor was among the affected organizations and that classified documents were allegedly stolen from it.

Hudson Rock Analysis

Threat intelligence company Hudson Rock later published its own analysis after receiving the dataset from Diachenko. Hudson Rock described the collection as one of the largest known troves of compromised Fortinet-related credentials.

According to Hudson Rock, the dataset contains:

  • 73,932 unique firewall URLs.
  • Affected systems across 194 countries.
  • 21,632 unique impacted domains.

Hudson Rock said the attackers maintained detailed logs of successful compromises and assembled a database of verified credentials for organizations across nearly every major industry sector. The company said organizations appearing in the dataset include Foxconn, Samsung, Comcast, Siemens, Lenovo, PwC, Accenture, Oracle, government agencies, and critical infrastructure operators.

Hudson Rock also released statistics showing that the highest number of affected devices was in India, the United States, Taiwan, Mexico, Turkey, Thailand, Colombia, Malaysia, Chile, and the United Arab Emirates. It said the most common sectors among listed companies were telecommunications, IT services, financial services, government organizations, healthcare providers, educational institutions, and manufacturing.

Unusual Password Findings

The article notes that one unusual aspect of the leak is that many exposed credentials were long, complex passwords. These would ordinarily be considered difficult to crack. The article does not identify a confirmed method by which those credentials were originally obtained.

Beaumont Review and Configuration Data Findings

Cybersecurity researcher Kevin Beaumont independently reviewed portions of the exposed data and told BleepingComputer that some of the credentials are authentic. He said he was able to confirm the authenticity of some admin logins and passwords and described the material as a real dump.

After reviewing data shared by Hudson Rock, Beaumont published additional findings indicating that the dataset contains credentials for roughly 75,000 Fortinet devices. He said most of those devices remain online.

Beaumont said the data appears to have originated from exported Fortinet configurations. He based this on the presence of information, including email addresses, that is typically accessible only through configurations.

He also said the affected IP addresses differ from those in the 2025 Belsen Group Fortinet leak. According to the article, this further indicates that FortiBleed is a more recent and larger collection of compromised devices.

Beaumont said multiple organizations listed in the dataset were using valid credentials. He also observed that many affected devices were running relatively recent FortiOS versions.

Internet Exposure and Scope

Based on Shodan network data, Beaumont said the leak contains approximately half of all internet-accessible Fortinet firewalls. He also said that a majority of the affected devices expose their FortiGate management interfaces directly to the internet.

The article states that the source of the configuration data remains unknown. It is unclear whether the data was stolen through previously disclosed Fortinet vulnerabilities, a newly discovered flaw, or another method. Diachenko, Hudson Rock, and Beaumont have not identified how the configuration data was originally obtained.

Recommended Actions

The article says Hudson Rock created a free FortiBleed lookup tool for organizations to check whether they are impacted. It also says organizations in the dataset should immediately take response steps.

Recommended actions include:

  • Rotating passwords associated with Fortinet VPN and administrative interfaces.
  • Enforcing MFA.
  • Examining gateway logs for suspicious activity.
  • Monitoring for exposed employee credentials.

AI-generated lure and anti-inspection behavior

The fraudulent bank page contained source-code comments that ThreatLabz assessed as likely generated by an AI tool. The comments included generic section headers with a templated structure that is commonly seen in AI-generated webpages where sections are labeled for later developer integration.

The page also contained anti-inspection measures. ClickFix infection flow

After the victim clicked the fake CAPTCHA, the page copied a malicious PowerShell command to the clipboard, placed the browser into fullscreen mode, and displayed a fake BSOD system recovery page. A lockdown routine attempted to keep the victim trapped in the tab or window, restrict keyboard input, and enforce fullscreen mode.

The lockdown routine first attempted to use navigator.keyboard.lock(). It then registered a capture-phase keydown handler that blocked most keystrokes while temporarily allowing the keystrokes needed for the ClickFix flow, including Win+R, Ctrl+V, and Enter. It also listened for window.blur and repeatedly called window.focus() to regain focus if the victim switched away.

To enforce fullscreen, the script checked fullscreen state every 50 milliseconds and called requestFullscreen, with fallbacks to webkitRequestFullscreen and mozRequestFullScreen. The PowerShell command copied to the clipboard included a random number of trailing spaces, which ThreatLabz noted was likely intended to bloat the payload and/or evade detection because a single added space changes the hash value.

Payload delivery chain

The copied PowerShell command downloaded and executed the next stage from 64[.]95[.]13[.]238:

SmartRAT purpose and configuration

SmartRAT is a Brazil-focused banking RAT implemented entirely in PowerShell and identified by the embedded string SMART_V25. Its stated objective is remote access and financial data theft using features such as fake bank-branded password forms, keylogging, and QR code interception.

SmartRAT decrypts two C2 configurations:

  • The primary C2 domain is c[.]windowsupdate-cdn[.]com, decrypted using XOR with the key 2.
  • The fallback C2 is 162[.]141[.]111[.]227, decrypted using XOR with the key 233.
  • The malware communicates on port 51888.

SmartRAT hides its PowerShell window using user32.dll’s ShowWindow function. It writes debug logs to C:\ProgramData or %APPDATA%\Microsoft\Diagnosis\ETW\client_debug.log, with a fallback to %TEMP%\clientdebug.log. It also creates a per-process log at C:\ProgramData\Microsoft\Diagnosis\ETW\process.log to record RAT activity.

Identity, encryption, and C2 security model

SmartRAT generates a unique identity token by hashing the machine GUID, MAC address, UTC ticks, a newly generated GUID, and the computer name with SHA-256. It stores the token in etw.dat and install.token.

It then computes an HMAC-SHA256 of the token using the hardcoded master key iuhbdaubdvauygd5562\(3@##\)r. The same hardcoded master key is used in two ways: the raw UTF-8 bytes of the plaintext key are used as the HMAC secret, while the SHA-256 hash of the string is used to derive the 32-byte AES encryption key.

C2 command traffic is encrypted and decrypted with AES-CBC. The encryption function generates a fresh IV for each message using GenerateIV(), hex-encodes the IV and ciphertext separately, and returns them as a colon-delimited string. The decryption function splits the colon-delimited value, hex-decodes the IV and ciphertext, and decrypts the command with the same AES key.

Persistence and privilege strategy

SmartRAT checks whether it is running as LocalSystem by comparing the current Windows identity SID to S-1-5-18, or whether it was launched with the -ServiceMode flag. If either condition is true, it connects to the C2 immediately. Otherwise, it performs persistence and elevation steps.

The malware copies itself to %APPDATA%\Microsoft\Diagnosis\ETW\msedgeupdate.txt. It attempts to create a logon-triggered scheduled task named MicrosoftEdgeUpdateCore. If scheduled task creation fails, it writes a MicrosoftEdgeUpdateCore value under HKCU\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run to re-execute the malware at user logon.

SmartRAT then prompts for UAC elevation. If elevation is approved, it compiles inline C# service code using csc.exe and installs a Windows service named MicrosoftEdgeUpdateCore under %ProgramData%\Microsoft\Diagnosis\ETW\. The service is configured to run with System privileges, and a watchdog checks every five seconds to ensure the SmartRAT PowerShell process continues running.

If UAC elevation is denied, SmartRAT does not create the Windows service. Instead, it launches a hidden PowerShell process that bypasses the UAC logic and beacons to the C2, while the scheduled task can prompt for UAC elevation again at the next logon.

The malware also compiles a C# component that uses DuplicateTokenEx and CreateProcessAsUser to spawn a new PowerShell process in the current user’s session, even when the RAT is running as SYSTEM.

AdaptixC2 Context

AdaptixC2 is a public post-exploitation framework with a Go-based teamserver and a C++/Qt GUI client. It supports two agent families:

  • Beacon, a C++ implant supporting BOF execution across Windows, Linux, and macOS.
  • Gopher, a Go implant supporting async BOF execution across the same platforms.

The framework implements listeners as loadable plugins, or “extenders,” covering HTTP/S, DNS/DoH, SMB named pipes, and raw TCP transports. Its teamserver exposes REST and WebSocket APIs for operator control, including credential management, agent tasking, screenshots, tunnel management, and multi-operator collaboration.

Core Fingerprint

The main detection signal comes from AdaptixC2’s default profile.yaml. Its error handler sets branded headers on unmatched routes:

  • Server: AdaptixC2
  • Adaptix-Version: v1.2

Because these headers are configured in the error handler rather than a specific route, they appear on arbitrary unauthenticated requests that do not match the configured endpoint. Requests to paths such as /, /robots.txt, /favicon.ico, or other arbitrary paths can return the branded 404 response.

The default 404 body provides a second independent signal. The article identifies the default body string:

You need to enter the correct connection details.

This body string remains useful when operators modify headers, and Censys notes that a “TestCoonection” header-modified variant was still detectable through the unchanged 404 body.

Teamserver and Listener Architecture

AdaptixC2’s teamserver uses a configured endpoint path, defaulting to /endpoint, and exposes multiple route groups beneath that path. The article explains that routes outside the endpoint prefix, or invalid paths inside it, hit the NoRoute handler and return the default branded 404 response.

The HTTP beacon listener behaves differently from the teamserver API. The BeaconHTTP extender starts its own separate HTTP server on the operator-configured callback port rather than using the teamserver’s public endpoint group. This distinction explains why Censys observed two populations:

  • Teamservers, often on port 4321, used for operator API access.
  • Beacon listeners, often on high ports such as 43211, used for agent callbacks.

Both surfaces can return the default 404 page, but they serve different operational purposes.

Authentication and Exposed Surface

The article states that the teamserver management API is reasonably secured when the password is set correctly. Operator authentication begins with POST /endpoint/login, which returns a 12-hour JWT access token and a 168-hour refresh token if the credential is correct. Authenticated operators can generate one-time OTP tokens for WebSocket upgrades and file transfers.

The unauthenticated surface is limited to branded error responses and login or refresh attempts. Failed authentication attempts return the same branded 404 response as invalid routes. This prevents clients from distinguishing invalid credentials from nonexistent paths, but it also exposes the framework identity on the first unauthenticated probe.
