FBI: Russian hackers now target Signal backup recovery keys
bleepingcomputer.com · 26 Jun 2026 22:06 UTC openai-codex / gpt-5.5
TAGScisadevice-linkingfbifederal-security-servicefsb-border-guardsiranrussiarussian-intelligence-servicessignal-backup-recovery-keyssignal-secure-backupsspearphishingukraineunc4221unc5792united-states

In plain words

The FBI and CISA say Russian intelligence-linked hackers are sending fake Signal support messages to trick people into giving up the special key that unlocks their saved Signal message history. The campaign is aimed at high-value people such as current and former government officials, military personnel, political figures, journalists, and key officials in Ukraine. This matters because if victims share that key, attackers can restore and read their old private and group messages on another device, even though Signal’s normal message protection has not been broken.

Summary
The FBI and CISA warn that Russian intelligence-linked phishing campaigns are now tricking high-value Signal users into revealing Backup Recovery Keys, which lets attackers restore and read their historical messages. Users should never share Signal recovery keys and must generate a new key if one may have been exposed.

The FBI and CISA warned that a phishing campaign targeting Signal users and tied to Russian intelligence services has evolved to steal Signal Backup Recovery Keys. The campaign targets individuals of high intelligence value, including current and former U.S. and international government officials, military personnel, political figures, journalists, and key officials in Ukraine. The agencies attribute the activity to Russian Intelligence Services, including officers embedded with Russia’s Federal Security Service Border Guards and other actors working on behalf of the Russian military.

The attackers continue to impersonate Signal support teams and send messages falsely claiming that Signal is introducing mandatory two-factor verification after alleged attacks by hackers from Iran and post-Soviet countries. Victims are instructed to enable Signal backups and view their recovery key, then later told their data is at risk of permanent loss due to a synchronization issue and asked to paste the key into the message. Anyone with the Backup Recovery Key can restore the backed-up data on their own devices and access historical private and group messages.

The FBI said creating a new Signal account with the same phone number does not invalidate a stolen Backup Recovery Key. Users must generate a new Backup Recovery Key through Signal’s backup settings to invalidate the previous key for future backup downloads. However, generating a new key will not prevent attackers from accessing backups they already downloaded using the compromised key.

  • The attackers are not breaking Signal’s end-to-end encryption; they are using phishing to obtain recovery keys that let them restore encrypted cloud backups on their own devices.
  • A stolen Signal Backup Recovery Key can remain useful even after a victim creates a new Signal account with the same phone number, so victims must specifically generate a new backup recovery key to invalidate the old one for future downloads.
  • Generating a new recovery key does not undo prior exposure, because it cannot stop attackers from accessing backups they already downloaded with the compromised key.
  • The phishing messages use detailed, app-specific instructions inside Signal’s backup settings, making the scam depend on persuading users to perform legitimate Signal actions and then share the resulting key.
Key Points
  • 01The FBI and CISA warned that a phishing campaign tied to Russian intelligence services has evolved to steal Signal Backup Recovery Keys, which can allow attackers to access victims’ historical messages.
  • 02The campaign targets individuals of high intelligence value, including current and former US and international government officials, military personnel, political figures, journalists, and key officials located in Ukraine.
  • 03The FBI attributes the activity to Russian Intelligence Services, including officers embedded with Russia’s Federal Security Service Border Guards and other actors working on behalf of the Russian military, and says the campaign is publicly tracked as UNC5792 and UNC4221.
  • 04The attackers impersonate Signal support and instruct victims to enable Signal backups, copy the recovery key, and later paste it into a message under the false claim that their data is at risk of loss due to a synchronization issue.
  • 05The FBI warned that creating a new Signal account with the same phone number does not invalidate a stolen Backup Recovery Key, and users must generate a new key in Signal’s backup settings to invalidate the previous key for future backup downloads.

The Hidden Attack Surface of Agentic AI: Securing AI Agent Integration Platforms
cyera.com · 26 Jun 2026 22:12 UTC openai-codex / gpt-5.5
TAGSafricaapacarcadecirclecicomposiocyeraeuropeexafirecrawlgithubllamaindexmodel-context-protocolnangonorth-americatavily

In plain words

Cyera researchers found many publicly exposed keys for services that let AI agents connect to tools like Gmail, GitHub, Slack, Salesforce, CircleCI, and other business systems. The affected people and organizations included startup founders, technology leaders, AI company executives, enterprise software vendors, financial services organizations, and companies across North America, Europe, Africa, and APAC. This matters because one leaked key can let someone see connected services, read data, use linked tools, or even keep using separate downstream access tokens after the original key is changed.

Summary
Cyera found widespread exposure of API keys for AI agent integration platforms like Composio, Nango, Arcade, and others, showing that leaked credentials can unlock broad access to connected tools such as email, code repositories, CI/CD systems, and enterprise apps. The key takeaway is that organizations need to inventory and govern their AI integration layer because a single exposed agent-platform key can create a large downstream breach path.

Cyera researchers examined the AI integration layer across hundreds of organizations and found thousands of exposed credentials for platforms including Composio, Arcade, Nango, Tavily, Exa, LlamaIndex, Firecrawl, and others. The exposed owners included startup founders, CTOs, AI company executives, enterprise software vendors, and financial services organizations across North America, Europe, Africa, and APAC. The article says these platforms connect AI models and agents to enterprise systems, creating additional credentials, secrets, and trust relationships that many security teams have not mapped.

The article highlights that AI integration platforms can become highly privileged points because they manage authentication, OAuth tokens, action execution, and access to downstream tools. In a Composio test, Cyera connected Gmail, GitHub, and CircleCI, then showed that a single read-only call returned the Gmail access token and refresh token, the GitHub bearer token, and the CircleCI API key. The article also describes Composio’s Proxy Execute API, which allowed fetching Gmail data through a leaked API key, and notes that rotating the Composio key would not stop downstream refresh tokens until each connected grant is revoked.

Cyera found that MCP servers were the largest category by count, but the article says simply knowing an MCP server exists does not reveal what data it can access or what actions it can perform. The article states that organizations need more than inventory: they must understand what each integration can reach, what permissions agents hold through it, and what an attacker could gain from compromise. Cyera recommends building a continuously updated inventory of AI integration infrastructure, understanding the exposure of each integration, and enforcing controls on sensitive data access, excessive permissions, exposed secrets, unsanctioned applications, and risky integrations.

  • An asset inventory that only labels something as an “MCP server” is not enough to judge risk, because one MCP server may only expose a public knowledge base while another may reach production cloud environments or customer data.
  • AI integration platforms can turn convenience features into concentration risk, because one easy onboarding flow can connect email, source code, CI/CD, and other tools behind a single platform key.
  • Some leaked AI integration credentials may expose the underlying third-party tokens themselves, meaning the incident response must include revoking each downstream grant, not just rotating the integration platform key.
  • Search and retrieval services are described as less critical than agent authentication platforms, but the article highlights that a compromised search layer can still feed manipulated information into an agent’s decisions without an audit trail.
  • Cyera found exposed credentials belonging not only to general users but also to founders, CTOs, SVPs of Engineering, AI executives, and enterprise software vendors, including people responsible for building the AI systems at risk.
Key Points
  • 01Cyera researchers found thousands of exposed credentials for AI integration platforms including Composio, Arcade, Nango, Tavily, Exa, LlamaIndex, Firecrawl, and other services across hundreds of organizations.
  • 02The article says AI integration platforms can become highly privileged points in an agent stack because a single integration may grant access to many downstream applications and services.
  • 03In Cyera’s Composio test, a read-only call returned a Gmail access token and refresh token, a GitHub bearer token, and a CircleCI API key from connected tools.
  • 04The article states that rotating a leaked Composio key may not end an incident because downstream refresh tokens can continue working until each connected service grant is revoked.
  • 05Cyera recommends that organizations build a continuously updated inventory of MCP servers, agent governance platforms, retrieval services, and integration providers, including unsanctioned and externally facing deployments.

It is broadly relevant for security professionals because it highlights a high-impact but often unmapped attack surface, though it is not a breaking active-exploitation incident or coordinated patch advisory.
