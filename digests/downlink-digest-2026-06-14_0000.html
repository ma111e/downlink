Overview

The attack targeted an employee at a sports data technology company in June 2026. A colleague at the same organization reported the email, and one mailbox was quarantined.

Why the Email Looked Legitimate

The email passed all major authentication checks listed in the article:

  • SPF passed.
  • DKIM passed.
  • DMARC passed.
  • The DMARC policy for international.informdata[.]com was set to p=REJECT sp=REJECT.

The article emphasizes that p=REJECT is the strictest DMARC enforcement policy. Despite that, the message was malicious because the attacker-controlled reply and link infrastructure did not match the legitimate sender identity.

The message was sent through SendGrid infrastructure. The article identifies the sending hostname as wfbtrqkw.outbound-mail.sendgrid.net and the sending IP as 159[.]183[.]84[.]25.

The authentication alignment appeared valid because the sending infrastructure was configured as authorized for international.informdata[.]com. The DKIM signature used selector s1 under that domain, and the Return-Path pointed to a VERP-encoded address at em8327.international.informdata[.]com, a SendGrid subdomain.

The article states that DMARC did exactly what it was designed to do: it verified that the sending infrastructure was authorized for the domain. It also states that authorization and intent are not the same thing.

The article says the attacker either compromised the InformData SendGrid account or configured a new SendGrid identity that could sign for the domain. It does not identify a named threat actor or group.

The central point is that the attacker was able to send email that aligned with the legitimate domain’s authentication setup. This allowed the phishing email to pass SPF, DKIM, and DMARC checks while still directing user interaction to attacker-controlled destinations.

The campaign illustrates that email authentication success does not necessarily indicate benign intent when legitimate or authorized infrastructure is abused.

02 Business-Themed Phishing with Reply Diversion and Victim Tracking

The employment verification lure observed by IRONSCALES used a Reply-To address at support@verifying[.]you, while its call-to-action directed recipients to verify.zippedscript[.]com. Both domains were described as attacker-controlled, creating a diversion between the authenticated sender identity and the infrastructure used for follow-up interaction. The campaign’s structure allowed the email to appear technically aligned while shifting recipient replies and clicks away from the impersonated brand domain.

The call-to-action URL included a long per-recipient tracking token that could allow the attacker to correlate clicks with specific email addresses. IRONSCALES classified the message as high risk based on behavioral indicators including a Reply-To domain mismatch, a first-time sender pattern, and mismatched link destinations. The case underscores the continued use of human resources and employment verification workflows as credible business pretexts for phishing.

Together, these reports show phishing operations operating at both industrial and targeted scales. Outsider Enterprise demonstrates the breadth of criminal phishing ecosystems built around mass URL generation, SMS delivery, and payment-card theft, while the InformData impersonation case shows how adversaries can exploit trusted email services and authentication alignment to increase the apparent legitimacy of business-themed lures.
