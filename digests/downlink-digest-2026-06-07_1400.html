Overview

Silent Ransom Group is actively targeting U.S. law firms and professional services organizations in social engineering attacks, according to a new report from Mandiant. The attacks often lead to data theft within hours of initial contact. Mandiant tracks the group as UNC3753, Luna Moth, and Chatty Spider.

The report follows an FBI FLASH advisory published the previous week that warned Silent Ransom Group was targeting U.S. law firms through social engineering and even in-person data theft attacks. Mandiant’s report adds technical details about how these intrusions are carried out. The firm said the group targeted dozens of organizations across the legal, financial, and professional services sectors between January and May 2026.

Why law firms are being targeted

Mandiant said legal firms remain especially attractive to extortion actors because they store large volumes of highly sensitive client information. The report specifically cites client transaction files, merger and acquisition plans, client trade secrets, and corporate regulatory reports as examples of sensitive data held by legal services firms.

Mandiant also said threat groups recognize that legal entities face heavy reputational and regulatory exposure. According to the report, these pressures may make legal organizations more motivated to resolve extortion situations quietly in order to protect their professional standing.

Initial contact and social engineering flow

The attacks begin with invoice-themed phishing emails sent from consumer email accounts. These emails do not contain malicious links or attachments. Instead, they serve as a precursor for follow-up phone calls from attackers impersonating corporate IT staff.

Mandiant described the activity as part of a long-running voice-based tactic used by these actors. The article notes that the group previously used BazarCall social engineering campaigns tied to Ryuk and Conti ransomware attacks. In the current campaign, the attackers impersonate IT help desks and convince employees to join remote support sessions.

Remote support sessions may be conducted through:

  • Microsoft Teams
  • Zoom
  • Quick Assist
  • Microsoft Terminal Services

During these sessions, the attackers trick targets into installing remote monitoring and management tools that provide initial access to the corporate network. Tools named in the article include:

  • AnyDesk
  • Zoho Assist
  • Bomgar
  • SuperOps

Phishing domains and use of self-destructing messages

Mandiant identified phishing domains tied to the campaign that impersonate internal IT portals. The article lists naming patterns such as:

  • <organization>-itdesk[.]com
  • <organization>-it[.]com
  • <organization>-helpdesk[.]com

The attackers also use privnote.com, a self-destructing messaging service, to share installation links and commands with targets during remote support sessions. Mandiant said this tactic helps reduce forensic artifacts left in browser histories or corporate chat logs.

Data discovery and exfiltration

Once inside a network, Silent Ransom Group searches for sensitive legal and financial documents. The article names contracts, tax records, Social Security numbers, and merger or acquisition files as targeted data types.

The attackers commonly target document management platforms and cloud storage repositories. Mandiant said the group exfiltrates data using tools such as WinSCP or Rclone.

Extortion behavior

Mandiant described the group’s extortion operation as highly aggressive. Ransom demands often arrive within 30 minutes of the attackers leaving the victim environment.

The extortion letters give organizations a three-day deadline to respond and begin ransom negotiations. If the victim does not respond, the attackers threaten to call and email target employees and external clients directly to alert them of the data breach.

According to Mandiant, the extortion letters explicitly emphasize that the leak will compromise client trust, invite substantial regulatory fines, and suggest that external clients sue the victim organization for data mishandling.

FBI warning on in-person activity

The article also references the FBI’s recent advisory about in-person data theft attacks linked to Silent Ransom Group. According to the FBI, attackers impersonate internal IT staff over phone calls and emails and then attempt to gain remote access or physically visit offices.

During these physical visits, attackers allegedly attempt to “image” computers or create backups while secretly stealing files. Mandiant said there was limited forensic evidence, but the researchers believe these in-person attacks are likely linked to UNC3753 based on similarities in targeting, timelines, and operational behavior.

Group background and evolution

Silent Ransom Group has been active since at least 2022, when it was part of the Ryuk and Conti cybercrime syndicate. The article says the actors were previously linked to BazarCall callback phishing campaigns that provided initial access in Conti and Ryuk ransomware attacks.

After the Conti syndicate shut down in 2022, the group shifted to standalone data theft and extortion operations under the Silent Ransom Group branding. Researchers said the group no longer relies on traditional ransomware encryption and instead focuses entirely on data-theft extortion, stealing sensitive data and pressuring victims to pay to prevent leaks.

Fast-flux infrastructure

A separate report released by Resecurity found that the gang is operating fast-flux infrastructure to hide and protect its data-leak platforms. The article explains DNS fast flux as a method in which attackers constantly rotate a domain’s IP addresses through a large pool of compromised devices to hide infrastructure and make takedowns or blocking more difficult.

Resecurity said the infrastructure uses residential IP addresses across multiple countries and ISPs. The company also said the group’s business-data-leaks[.]com leak site and related infrastructure rely on residential proxy networks spread across Latin America, Eastern Europe, Central Asia, the Middle East, and Asia. Resecurity also linked the infrastructure to other cybercrime-related services and domains.

Recommended defenses

Mandiant and the FBI recommend several defensive measures for organizations facing this activity. The article lists the following recommendations:

  • Implement strict verification procedures for IT support interactions.
  • Limit remote access tools.
  • Enforce multifactor authentication.
  • Restrict USB storage devices.
  • Train employees to recognize voice phishing attempts.

These recommendations directly address the group’s use of fake IT support calls, remote access tooling, and possible physical office visits.

  • Because the initial phishing emails contain no malicious links or attachments, defenses that rely mainly on scanning email payloads may miss the early stage of these attacks.
  • The use of privnote.com to share installation links and commands is specifically intended to reduce forensic artifacts in browser histories and corporate chat logs.
  • The group’s extortion strategy is built around legal firms’ reputational and regulatory exposure, with threats to contact employees and external clients directly if victims do not respond within three days.
  • The reported in-person office visits show that verification procedures for IT support interactions need to cover both remote help desk requests and physical access requests.
  • Resecurity found that the group uses DNS fast-flux infrastructure with residential IP addresses across multiple countries and ISPs, making takedowns and blocking more difficult.

For broader distribution, C0XMO downloads a Python script that installs additional packages. These include:

The Python script contains almost two dozen functions for scanning, exploiting HTTP and ADB-based vulnerabilities, detecting CPU architecture, logging in over SSH and Telnet, and checking IP addresses. The article states that its main purpose is lateral movement on the network.

Persistence and Host Control

Once C0XMO gains access to a device, it copies itself to hidden locations. The article names these paths:

  • /tmp/.sys
  • /var/tmp/.sys
  • /dev/shm/.sys

It then creates cron jobs that relaunch the malware every 15 minutes. The malware also modifies shell startup files to enable automatic execution.

Removal of Rival Malware and Tools

C0XMO scans running processes to identify competitor botnet clients on the host. It also looks for red-team tools, programming tools, and network services that may interfere with its operation. When it finds these, it terminates them.

The malware also deletes binaries and removes persistence mechanisms. The article specifically names cron jobs, init scripts, system services, and shell profile entries as persistence mechanisms that C0XMO removes.

After establishing itself, C0XMO connects to a hardcoded command-and-control address. The connection uses a custom multi-stage handshake that includes magic strings and shared secrets. The malware then waits for commands.

  • Stopping scans
  • Launching DDoS attacks

C0XMO supports 19 DDoS methods. The article lists methods including UDP, TCP, SYN, and ICMP floods, “ping of death,” NTP and Memcached amplification, Discord voice UDP floods, and Valve-specific floods.

Defensive Guidance Mentioned in the Article

The article gives general recommendations for defending against C0XMO and other botnet malware. These include keeping devices up to date, using unique administrator credentials, and disabling remote access capabilities when they are not needed.
