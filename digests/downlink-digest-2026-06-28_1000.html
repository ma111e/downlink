In plain words

Someone sent a fake legal notice from a personal Gmail account to many hidden recipients, using Hebrew and English text to make it feel urgent. The message included a shortened link that looked harmless at first but led to a malicious site meant to steal login details. This matters because ordinary email checks could see the Gmail sender and the short-link service as legitimate, so people who receive unexpected legal threats by email could be pressured into clicking before verifying through official channels.

Summary
A phishing email posing as a bilingual legal notice used a legitimate Gmail account and a short.gy link to hide a malicious credential-harvesting domain, allowing SPF, DKIM, DMARC, and basic URL reputation checks to appear clean. The key lesson is that defenders must analyze sender context and follow shortened URLs through the full redirect chain to detect these attacks.

An anonymized sender using a personal Gmail account sent a mass-BCC email with a bilingual Hebrew and English subject line that presented the message as a legal notice. The sender claimed a professional title, but the account had no prior contact history with the targeted organization. The message contained no attached lawsuit and instead included a single short.gy URL shortener link.

The shortener link uepk9u.short[.]gy/DJp0Jm resolved to hxxps://feature[.]chongdaotang[.]net/Wx6Ld3KlNmuU4tT@tDVpJltq5/, which IRONSCALES link analysis rated Malicious. The article says short.gy is a legitimate URL shortening product with clean reputation, meaning checks that evaluated only the shortener could miss the attacker-controlled destination. The domain chongdaotang[.]net was registered in November 2023 and was privacy-protected at the time of analysis.

SPF, DKIM, and DMARC all passed because the email genuinely originated from Google infrastructure through a real Gmail account. IRONSCALES Adaptive AI flagged the message at 90% confidence as a “Legal Threat Lure” based on the combination of a first-contact sender, high-pressure legal framing, BCC delivery, a personal free-mail account, and a shortened link resolving to a malicious destination. The article emphasizes that defenses need to follow shortened URLs through the full redirect chain rather than scoring only the hostname visible in the email body.

  • A clean SPF, DKIM, and DMARC result did not reduce the risk in this case because the attacker used real Google sending infrastructure rather than spoofing a domain.
  • URL reputation checks that stop at the shortener can miss the threat entirely because they evaluate short.gy, a legitimate service, instead of the malicious final destination.
  • The article identifies mass-BCC delivery as an important warning sign because legitimate legal notices should be addressed to a specific recipient rather than hidden among undisclosed recipients.
  • The strongest detection signal came from the mismatch between the sender’s claimed legal authority and the actual use of a personal free-mail account with no prior relationship to the organization.
  • The article recommends following shortened links through the full redirect chain before scoring them, since the attacker’s malicious infrastructure only appears after the redirect completes.
