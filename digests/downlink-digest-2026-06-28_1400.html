In plain words

Japanese telecommunications company KDDI says someone broke into one of its email systems by taking advantage of a weakness in third-party software it used. The incident may have exposed email addresses and passwords for up to 14.2 million current, former, and inactive customers of KDDI and five other Japanese internet providers: STNet, JCOM, Chubu Telecommunications, NIFTY, and BIGLOBE. This matters because exposed email logins can be used to try to get into people’s accounts, so customers are being told to change their email passwords and turn on extra sign-in protection if it is available.

Summary
KDDI says attackers exploited a third-party software flaw in one of its email systems, potentially exposing email addresses and passwords for up to 14.2 million current, former, and inactive customers across six Japanese ISPs. Affected users are being urged to reset passwords and enable 2FA where available.

Japanese telecommunications operator KDDI Corporation disclosed a data breach involving one of its email systems that was also used by five other internet service providers in Japan. KDDI said it discovered the compromise on June 17 and responded by blocking the attacker and implementing defensive measures. The company said the investigation found that hackers exploited a vulnerability in unnamed third-party software used on the system.

KDDI warned that customers’ email addresses and passwords may have been obtained by unauthorized third parties as a result of the incident. The affected ISP operators and services include STNet, JCOM, Chubu Telecommunications, NIFTY, and BIGLOBE. KDDI said the incident may have exposed data for up to 14.2 million current and former customers, as well as inactive accounts that may no longer be in use.

The company said some passwords were stored in hashed and/or encrypted form, but it did not specify the type of encryption or what percentage of accounts had plaintext passwords. KDDI has notified Japan’s Personal Information Protection Commission and the Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications, and is working with affected ISPs on additional security measures. Customers who may have been exposed are advised to reset their email account passwords and enable two-factor authentication if it is available.

  • The breach shows that a single KDDI-operated email system created exposure across multiple separate ISP brands, expanding the impact beyond KDDI’s own customers.
  • KDDI said some passwords were hashed or encrypted, but it did not disclose the method used or how many passwords were stored in plaintext, leaving customers unable to judge their individual risk from the article’s information.
  • Former customers and inactive accounts may be included in the exposure, so people who no longer actively use these ISP email accounts may still need to reset passwords.
  • The exact number of affected accounts has not been determined because KDDI’s investigation is still underway, so the reported 14.2 million figure is a maximum potential exposure rather than a confirmed victim count.
Key Points
  • 01KDDI Corporation disclosed a data breach in which threat actors accessed one of its email systems used by five other Japanese internet service providers.
  • 02KDDI discovered the compromise on June 17 and said it blocked the attacker and implemented defensive measures.
  • 03The company said attackers exploited a vulnerability in unnamed third-party software used on its system.
  • 04The incident may have exposed email addresses and passwords for up to 14.2 million current, former, and inactive customers, including inactive accounts.
  • 05KDDI advised potentially affected customers to reset their email account passwords and enable two-factor authentication if available.
