Summary
CISA says a critical Splunk Enterprise flaw, CVE-2026-20253, is being actively exploited and has ordered U.S. federal agencies to patch by Sunday. The bug lets unauthenticated remote attackers create or truncate arbitrary files and may enable remote code execution, with disabling the PostgreSQL sidecar service offered as a temporary mitigation.

CISA has urged federal agencies to secure their systems by Sunday against CVE-2026-20253, a critical Splunk Enterprise vulnerability that is being exploited in attacks. The flaw affects Splunk Enterprise versions 10.2.0 to 10.2.3 and 10.0.0 to 10.0.6. CISA added the vulnerability to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog and ordered Federal Civilian Executive Branch agencies to patch under Binding Operational Directive 26-04.

Splunk said the issue exists because a PostgreSQL sidecar service endpoint lacks authentication controls. This allows any network-reachable user to invoke file operations without credentials, enabling unauthenticated remote attackers to create or truncate arbitrary files on vulnerable devices. WatchTowr published a technical write-up and proof-of-concept exploit code on June 12 and warned that the flaw can be abused for remote code execution attacks.

Splunk updated its advisory on June 18 after its Product Security Incident Response Team became aware of limited exploitation of the vulnerability. Shadowserver tracks more than 1,400 Internet-exposed Splunk instances, mostly in North America and Europe, though the article says it is unknown how many are vulnerable. Splunk advised administrators who cannot immediately patch to disable the PostgreSQL sidecar service, while warning that doing so would break Edge Processor, OpAmp, or SPL2 data pipelines on affected instances.

  • The emergency deadline reflects CISA’s new BOD 26-04 risk-based patching approach, which can require federal agencies to remediate exploited flaws within days rather than on a standard patch cycle.
  • Public proof-of-concept exploit code was released before Splunk updated its advisory to confirm limited in-the-wild exploitation, making rapid patching more urgent for exposed systems.
  • The temporary mitigation is not operationally neutral: disabling the PostgreSQL sidecar service removes the attack surface but breaks Edge Processor, OpAmp, or SPL2 data pipelines on affected instances.
