Overview

IRONSCALES reported a phishing attack in which attackers impersonated InformData, a legitimate background check company, using an employment verification pretext. The email was sent to an employee at a sports data technology company and claimed to be an “Employment Verification Request.” A colleague at the same organization reported the message, and one mailbox was quarantined.

The message appeared to come from verifications@international.informdata[.]com, a domain belonging to the real company. The article states that SPF, DKIM, and DMARC all passed, and that DMARC was configured with the strictest enforcement policy: p=REJECT sp=REJECT. Despite these authentication results, the message was malicious.

Why the Attack Was Significant

The article highlights that email authentication succeeded because the attackers sent the message through infrastructure authorized for international.informdata[.]com. The sending infrastructure was SendGrid, specifically wfbtrqkw.outbound-mail.sendgrid.net with IP address 159[.]183[.]84[.]25. The DKIM signature used selector s1 under international.informdata[.]com, and the Return-Path pointed to a VERP-encoded address at em8327.international.informdata[.]com.

The article says DMARC did exactly what it was designed to do: verify that the sending infrastructure was authorized for the domain. The failure was not an authentication failure but a mismatch between authorization and intent. According to the article, the attacker either compromised the InformData SendGrid account or configured a new SendGrid identity that could sign for the domain.

Phishing Mechanics

The malicious content was visible in the reply and link infrastructure. The Reply-To header pointed to support@verifying[.]you, which the article identifies as attacker-controlled. The “Review Employment” call-to-action linked to verify.zippedscript[.]com/verify/employment/51d4ba99721cdbdbe83cc09a15f3d76b, also described as attacker-controlled.

The article notes that the long hash in the URL path functioned as a per-recipient tracking token, allowing the attacker to correlate clicks with specific email addresses. WHOIS information for zippedscript[.]com showed Cloudflare nameservers and privacy-protected registration. The unsubscribe link also pointed to verify.zippedscript[.]com/unsubscribe, which the article says confirmed attacker control of the entire link infrastructure.

Social Engineering Pretext

The lure relied on an employment verification request, a routine business process associated with hiring and background checks. The article explains that recipients may expect such messages during hiring cycles because companies like InformData regularly send verification requests. It also states that the pretext creates professional pressure because ignoring or delaying a verification can slow a colleague’s onboarding.

The message therefore combined a legitimate brand identity, authenticated sending infrastructure, and a business-process pretext. The article frames this as a high-severity credential harvesting and social engineering attack. It maps the activity to MITRE ATT&CK techniques for spearphishing links, masquerading, and phishing for information.

Detection and Response

IRONSCALES detected the message as a first-time sender flagged as high risk. Themis, the IRONSCALES Adaptive AI, labeled the recipient as a VIP and flagged the email based on behavioral indicators. These indicators included the Reply-To domain mismatch, the first-time sender pattern, and the disconnect between the legitimate brand identity and the link destinations.

The detection did not rely on failed authentication checks because there were none. The article specifically states that defenders reviewing employment verification emails should verify whether the Reply-To domain matches the From domain and whether call-to-action links resolve to the sender’s actual infrastructure. It concludes that when a background check company’s email routes replies and clicks to entirely different domains, the verification request is the attack.

MITRE ATT&CK Mapping

The article maps the activity to three MITRE ATT&CK techniques:

  • T1566.002, Phishing: Spearphishing Link, because the message used an employment verification email with a credential harvesting link.
  • T1036.005, Masquerading: Match Legitimate Name or Location, because the attackers impersonated InformData, a real background check company.
  • T1598.003, Phishing for Information: Spearphishing Link, because the URL included per-recipient tracking tokens for target validation.

Main Takeaway

The core lesson in the article is that passing SPF, DKIM, and DMARC, even with a DMARC REJECT policy, does not prove that a message is benign. In this case, authentication showed that the sending infrastructure was authorized for the domain, but the Reply-To address and call-to-action links led to attacker-controlled infrastructure. The article presents Reply-To mismatches, first-time sender behavior, and link destination mismatches as important detection signals when authentication checks pass.

  • A DMARC REJECT policy can still allow a phishing message when the attacker sends through infrastructure that is legitimately authorized for the impersonated domain.
  • Reply-To and CTA domain mismatches can reveal malicious intent even when SPF, DKIM, and DMARC all pass.
  • The employment verification theme is especially effective because recipients may feel pressure to respond quickly to avoid delaying a colleague's onboarding.
  • The long hash in the phishing URL path served as a per-recipient tracking token, allowing the attacker to correlate clicks with specific email addresses.
  • An attacker-controlled unsubscribe link can indicate that the entire link infrastructure, not just the main call-to-action, is part of the phishing operation.

01 Phishing Abuse of Authorized SendGrid Infrastructure

IRONSCALES reported an employment verification-themed phishing email impersonating InformData that was sent through SendGrid infrastructure and passed SPF, DKIM, and DMARC checks, including a DMARC policy set to REJECT. The message was associated with SendGrid host wfbtrqkw.outbound-mail.sendgrid.net and IP address 159[.]183[.]84[.]25, which the article states were configured as authorized sending infrastructure for international.informdata[.]com.

The phishing lure targeted a sports data technology company and was flagged in June 2026 after a colleague at the organization reported the email. According to IRONSCALES, one mailbox was quarantined. The message used an employment verification pretext while redirecting replies and user interaction away from the apparent sender’s legitimate domain.

02 Reply-To Diversion and Attacker-Controlled Domains

The email’s Reply-To header directed responses to support@verifying[.]you, while the call-to-action linked to verify.zippedscript[.]com; both were described by IRONSCALES as attacker-controlled domains. This combination created a mismatch between the authenticated sending domain and the destinations used for follow-up interaction, allowing the message to appear technically authenticated while steering the recipient into attacker-controlled communication paths.

The case illustrates the distinction between domain authentication and message intent. As described in the report, DMARC validated that the message was sent via infrastructure authorized for the domain, but it did not determine whether the email content, Reply-To destination, or embedded link represented a legitimate business process. For defenders and threat hunters, the incident is notable because the malicious indicators were not authentication failures, but rather the use of authorized infrastructure alongside suspicious reply routing and external verification domains.
