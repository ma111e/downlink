Miasma and Mini Shai-Hulud

The article says Miasma is assessed to be a variant of the Mini Shai-Hulud worm that TeamPCP publicly released in mid-May 2026. Since then, Miasma has continued to mutate and refine its tactics. The article says it has infected more packages over the past couple of days.

The campaign has used several descriptions for newly created public repositories containing stolen secrets, including:

  • “Miasma: The Spreading Blight”
  • “Miasma : The Spreading Blight”
  • “Miasma - The Spreading Blight”
  • “Hades - The End for the Damned”

At the time of writing, the article says there were 13 repositories with the description “Hades - The End for the Damned” and 82 repositories using the remaining three Miasma-related naming patterns.

Direct GitHub repository compromise and developer tool execution

Miasma has also been observed skipping the npm registry entirely. In those cases, the threat actors pushed malicious code directly to icflorescu/mantine-datatable and four related repositories: mantine-contextmenu, next-server-actions-parallel, mantine-datatable-v6, and mantine-contextmenu-v6.

SafeDep said the malicious commit added no dependencies. Instead, it planted a 4.3 MB payload runner and wired it to execute automatically through five developer tools or workflows:

  • Claude Code
  • Gemini CLI
  • Cursor
  • VS Code
  • npm test script

SafeDep said the attack detonates when a developer clones one of the affected repositories and opens it in an AI coding agent. It described the dropper as the same staged Bun loader, repurposed for GitHub source-repository persistence rather than registry poisoning.

Significance of the campaign

The article frames the campaign as one of the most significant and sustained software supply chain campaigns observed to date. It says these attacks expose weaknesses in the trust model that underpins software delivery in open-source ecosystems.

The article emphasizes that the activity differs from other incidents because it can propagate across the ecosystem by compromising downstream users and repeating the same cycle. FalconFeeds.io said the worm operates entirely within legitimate channels and does not exploit a vulnerability in npm or GitHub.

According to FalconFeeds.io, the worm exploits the trust model those platforms are built on: the assumption that a package signed with a valid key and published by an authenticated maintainer is safe. FalconFeeds.io said Shai-Hulud compromises the key and the maintainer, then acts as a legitimate publisher would, making malicious publish events indistinguishable from routine updates from the registry’s perspective.

Overview

Include Security and independent researcher Buchodi published findings on June 5 about Bright Data’s SDK and how it can turn consumer devices into residential proxy exit nodes. The article says the SDK is embedded in free apps behind an opt-in screen and can relay web-scraping traffic for Bright Data’s data business, which is marketed heavily to the AI industry.

Bright Data is described as the successor to Luminati and as operating what it calls the largest residential proxy network in the world, advertised at more than 400 million residential IPs. The article says part of that supply comes from the SDK and is described by Bright Data as a consent-sourced pool of more than 150 million IPs.

The central issue is that scraping traffic originates from the user’s home IP address rather than from the customer using the proxy service. The article says the immediate risk is not stolen data or a hacked account, but the use of a home internet connection and bandwidth as someone else’s scraping infrastructure.

Why smart TVs matter

The article says connected TVs are close to ideal for this type of proxy use because they are usually plugged in, connected to fast networks, effectively unmetered, and unwatched. The smart-TV reach described in the article rests on Bright Data’s platform support, public partner list, and earlier reporting, while the deepest technical evidence comes from the iOS SDK analysis.

Bright Data’s public app partner list is said to include makers of smart-TV apps such as PlayWorks Digital, CloudTV, and Longvision. The researcher is careful to note that being listed only shows that a company worked with Bright Data at some point, not that its app currently contains the SDK. Each app would need to be checked individually.

Technical findings

The research found that when an app opens, the SDK contacts one of Bright Data’s servers and receives instructions without meaningful verification of who is asking. After that, the server can instruct the device to fetch pages from other websites using the user’s home internet connection.

The article says the peer channel carrying scraping jobs has no real authentication. The researcher described that channel as weaker than controls built into most malware.

On iOS, the researcher found that the SDK’s traffic bypasses a configured VPN. The article also says much of what the app does does not show up in the tools security teams normally use to monitor apps. The device can also keep relaying in the background while a user is watching the screen or on a call, provided the battery is not low.

Consent and usage limits

The article says the opt-in screen does not match what the SDK actually allows. In one Roku app, Petflix, the screen said it would use the device and connection “occasionally.” However, the SDK settings allowed up to 200 GB of traffic per month.

The article also states that in a few countries, including Uzbekistan and Oman, traffic limits are set far higher. In those cases, the device is cleared to keep working almost until the battery runs flat.

The SDK can also tie together a person’s phone and computers that run the same company’s apps, treating them as one user. The article frames the meaningfulness of user consent as the key question separating Bright Data’s model from criminal proxy networks that hijack consumer devices.

Broader context

The article says the model is not new in shape, only in scale. Bright Data is the successor to Luminati, the paid proxy service that grew out of Hola VPN. In 2015, Hola was reported to have sold free users’ bandwidth as exit nodes through Luminati at $20 per gigabyte.

The article says the buyer has changed because AI scrapers need residential connections to get around anti-bot defenses from companies such as Cloudflare and DataDome. It cites reporting from Krebs in October 2025 that proxies from botnets such as Aisuru were fueling large-scale AI data harvesting. It also notes that Google dismantled the criminal IPIDEA proxy network in January.

The article distinguishes those criminal operations from Bright Data’s stated model by noting that botnets hijack consumer devices, while Bright Data says its exit nodes opt in through a consent screen. The article says whether that consent is meaningful remains the open question.

Platform response

Lowpass, syndicated by The Verge, first surfaced the smart-TV angle in February, according to the article. The Include Security work is described as the technical teardown.

The article says Google, Amazon, and Roku have since restricted background proxy SDKs. Bright Data has dropped those platforms, though the article says it still lists Samsung’s Tizen and LG’s webOS.

Mitigation guidance in the article

The article says the traffic is easy to spot and block. For home networks, it recommends blocking the SDK’s connection domains using router-level tools such as Pi-hole or NextDNS.

The listed domains are:

  • proxyjs.brdtnet.com
  • proxyjs.luminatinet.com
  • proxyjs.bright-sdk.com
  • clientsdk.bright-sdk.com
  • clientsdk.brdtnet.com

According to the research cited in the article, blocking these domains stops the device from acting as a relay without affecting Bright Data’s paid service, which uses separate addresses.

For companies managing staff phones, the article says they can scan for apps that carry the SDK. It also notes a limitation: on mobile connections, traffic sidesteps office Wi-Fi, so network blocking alone will not always catch it. The article adds that Bright Data could change how the SDK connects in the future, meaning blocklists may need updates.

  • The practical harm is reputational and operational rather than direct data theft: scraping activity appears to originate from the user’s residential IP address instead of Bright Data’s customer.

  • Enterprise network blocking may miss affected mobile devices because the SDK traffic can use cellular connections and, on iOS, can bypass a configured VPN.

EXEC Executive Overview

This digest is led by software supply chain risk, actively exploited enterprise vulnerabilities, and emerging evidence of AI accelerating vulnerability discovery. The most operationally significant items are the Miasma worm’s impact across Microsoft GitHub repositories, CISA’s addition of SolarWinds Serv-U CVE-2026-28318 to the KEV catalog, and large vulnerability disclosures affecting FFmpeg and Chrome.

01 Software Supply Chain and Developer Tooling Abuse

The Miasma self-replicating supply chain campaign reportedly affected 73 Microsoft GitHub repositories across the Azure, Azure-Samples, Microsoft, and MicrosoftDocs organizations. GitHub disabled access to affected repositories, including Azure/azure-functions-host, citing a violation of its terms of service.

The campaign included a re-compromise of the durabletask PyPI package, which had previously been infected by TeamPCP to deliver an information stealer on Linux systems. Miasma is assessed to be a variant of the Mini Shai-Hulud worm that TeamPCP publicly released in mid-May 2026, indicating reuse or adaptation of publicly available worming tradecraft.

Reported behavior includes pushing malicious code directly to GitHub repositories and wiring execution through developer tooling such as Claude Code, Gemini CLI, Cursor, VS Code, and the npm test script. This positions the campaign at the intersection of repository compromise, package ecosystem abuse, and AI-assisted developer workflow exploitation.

02 Actively Exploited and Newly Disclosed Software Vulnerabilities

CISA added CVE-2026-28318, a high-severity SolarWinds Serv-U vulnerability, to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog after evidence of active exploitation. The flaw is an uncontrolled resource consumption issue with a CVSS score of 7.5 that can allow an unauthenticated attacker to crash Serv-U using specially crafted POST requests with Content-Encoding: deflate.

SolarWinds fixed the issue in Serv-U version 15.5.4 HF1 and described mitigations including limiting access to known addresses and blocking requests containing “content-encoding.” CISA directed Federal Civilian Executive Branch agencies to address the vulnerability by June 19, 2026.

Separate vulnerability research highlighted the growing role of autonomous systems in bug discovery. Security startup depthfirst reported that an autonomous AI agent found 21 previously unknown vulnerabilities in FFmpeg during a run costing about $1,000, with some flaws dating back 15 to 20 years, including a stack overflow in service-description-table code from 2003.

Google also released Chrome 149 with fixes for 429 security vulnerabilities, including more than 100 rated critical or high severity. The most severe issue, CVE-2026-10881, is an out-of-bounds read and write in the ANGLE graphics engine that can allow a crafted page to escape the browser sandbox and execute code on the host.

03 Consumer Device Proxy Monetization and Residential Traffic Abuse

Research by Include Security and independent researcher Buchodi found that Bright Data’s iOS SDK can turn consumer devices into exit nodes for web-scraping traffic routed through users’ home internet connections. The analysis found that the peer channel carrying scraping jobs had no real authentication and that, on iOS, SDK traffic could bypass a configured VPN.

Bright Data says its residential proxy supply includes consent-sourced IPs from an SDK embedded in free apps behind an opt-in screen. The reporting raises questions about whether disclosed consent accurately reflects SDK capabilities, citing a Roku app called Petflix whose opt-in screen described only occasional device and connection use while SDK settings allowed up to 200 GB of traffic per month.

The report identified several Bright Data SDK domains associated with the relay function, including proxyjs.brdtnet.com and clientsdk.bright-sdk.com. The findings broaden the residential proxy risk model beyond traditional malware-driven botnets to include app-mediated monetization channels on consumer smart devices.
