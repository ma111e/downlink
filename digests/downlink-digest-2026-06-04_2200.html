Main Executive Order requirements

The article says the EO’s core cybersecurity requirements are in Section 2, “Upgrading American systems for advanced AI,” and Section 3, “Secure frontier model deployment.” Section 2 focuses on federal cyber defense, vulnerability management, an AI cybersecurity clearinghouse, grant funding, and workforce measures.

Requirements within 30 days

The article identifies several actions due within 30 days:

  • Federal agencies must begin hardening their information systems with AI-enabled cyber defenses.
  • The Committee on National Security Systems must prioritize cyber defense of National Security Systems.
  • The Secretary of War must prioritize cyber defense of Department of War information systems.
  • CISA must issue Binding Operational Directives or other guidance for civilian federal agencies.
  • Treasury, with the National Cyber Director, NSA, and CISA, must form an AI cybersecurity clearinghouse.
  • OMB, with the National Cyber Director and CISA, must determine whether existing federal grant programs can support applicants developing advanced AI vulnerability detection.

For civilian federal systems, the article says CISA’s directives or guidance must expedite and prioritize cyber defense, establish or expand programs that enhance AI-enabled defensive tools, and facilitate access to cybersecurity tools and services. The access provision may include agencies, state and local authorities, and critical infrastructure operators such as rural hospitals, community banks, and local utilities.

AI cybersecurity clearinghouse

The article highlights the AI cybersecurity clearinghouse as one of the EO’s most operationally consequential provisions. It says the clearinghouse must be formed by Treasury with the National Cyber Director, NSA, and CISA, in voluntary collaboration with the AI industry and critical infrastructure operators.

The clearinghouse has three functions described in the article:

  • Coordinate and deconflict scanning for software vulnerabilities.
  • Discover and validate those vulnerabilities.
  • Coordinate and prioritize remediation and patch distribution.

The article frames the clearinghouse as important because it describes a capability rather than only a policy. It also states that the clearinghouse will require strong engagement from private sector partners, including Tenable, to generate actionable insights on AI-associated vulnerabilities and mitigation prioritization.

Requirements within 60 days

Within 60 days, the article says the Office of Personnel Management must expand hiring and placement pathways for cybersecurity specialists through the United States Tech Force.

The article also says Treasury, NSA, and CISA, in consultation with NIST and other entities, must develop a classified benchmarking process to assess the advanced cyber capabilities of AI models. They must also establish the threshold for designating a “covered frontier model” and design a voluntary framework allowing developers to give the government up to 30 days of pre-release access to covered models.

The article notes that the EO explicitly does not create a mandatory licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirement for AI models.

Requirement with no fixed deadline

The EO directs the Attorney General to prioritize enforcement against those who use AI to illegally access or damage computer systems. The article identifies this as the EO’s criminal enforcement component.
