Incident Overview

The US House Committee on Homeland Security has initiated a formal investigation into two cyberattacks targeting Instructure Holdings, Inc. and its Canvas learning management platform. Committee Chairman Andrew R. Garbarino issued a letter to CEO Steve Daly demanding executive testimony regarding the breaches conducted by the ShinyHunters extortion group. The committee is actively examining the incident’s impact on tens of millions of students, educators, and administrators nationwide.

Attack Timeline and Technical Details

Instructure detected the initial system intrusion on April 29 and publicly disclosed the security incident on May 3. The threat actors successfully compromised internal systems and extracted sensitive user data. The exposed information consisted of names, email addresses, student identification numbers, and direct messages exchanged between students and teachers. The company verified that passwords, financial information, and government identifiers were not compromised. ShinyHunters later claimed responsibility for exfiltrating 280 million data records from 8,809 colleges, school districts, and online education platforms. The group published a detailed list of impacted organizations, with stolen record counts ranging from tens of thousands to several million per institution.

Within a single week, the attackers executed a second intrusion campaign. This follow-up attack utilized multiple cross-site scripting (XSS) vulnerabilities to obtain authenticated administrative sessions. Attackers leveraged these elevated privileges to modify Canvas login portal pages and display extortion messages. The defacement campaign disrupted academic operations across multiple states, including California, Florida, Georgia, Oklahoma, Oregon, Nevada, North Carolina, Tennessee, Utah, Virginia, and Wisconsin. Several institutions were forced to cancel final exams and suspend end-of-semester activities due to the platform disruption.

Negotiations and Resolution

The attackers publicly stated they launched the second intrusion because Instructure initially refused to negotiate. Following the defacement campaign, the threat actors mysteriously removed the company from their data leak site. Instructure subsequently disclosed that it had reached an agreement with ShinyHunters to halt the public data leak and ensure the stolen records were permanently deleted. While the company did not explicitly confirm a ransom payment, the circumstances strongly indicate a negotiated settlement to contain the breach.

Regulatory Response and Next Steps

The Homeland Security Committee has raised serious concerns regarding Instructure’s incident response capabilities and its obligations to properly protect sensitive educational data. Lawmakers are scrutinizing the company’s notification efforts and coordination with federal agencies. The committee has mandated that Instructure or a senior representative participate in a formal briefing no later than May 21. Key discussion points for the hearing include:

  • A detailed review of both intrusions and the initial compromise vectors.
  • An assessment of the scope and classification of stolen student and staff data.
  • An evaluation of the company’s containment strategies and breach notification procedures.
  • A summary of ongoing coordination efforts with federal cybersecurity agencies.

EXEC Executive Overview

The United States House Committee on Homeland Security has issued a summons to executives of Instructure, the provider of the Canvas learning management system, demanding testimony regarding a significant cyberattack campaign attributed to the threat actor ShinyHunters. This legislative action follows the disclosure of two distinct cyberattacks that compromised millions of student records and caused substantial disruption to academic operations. The committee’s inquiry aims to scrutinize Instructure’s incident response capabilities and its adherence to data protection obligations in the wake of the breach.

The attacks leveraged cross-site scripting (XSS) vulnerabilities within the Canvas platform. According to disclosures, the malicious actors utilized these vulnerabilities to deface the Canvas login portals. Beyond visual defacement, the intrusion facilitated the exfiltration of sensitive personal data, including names, email addresses, and student identification numbers belonging to millions of users. The scale of the compromise indicates a widespread impact across the educational sector, affecting a vast number of institutions and individuals utilizing the platform.

Instructure publicly disclosed the security incident on May 3, 2026. Following the disclosure, the company engaged with ShinyHunters and reached an agreement to halt the public dissemination of the stolen data. Despite this agreement, the House Committee has moved forward with formal oversight proceedings. The committee has requested a briefing from Instructure by May 21, 2026, to evaluate the company’s response to the incident. This request underscores the government’s focus on assessing how private sector entities manage critical data assets and respond to high-profile cyber incidents involving educational infrastructure.

The summons highlights the increasing scrutiny placed on technology providers hosting sensitive personal data by federal legislative bodies. The focus on XSS vulnerabilities points to persistent risks associated with web application security in widely deployed platforms. The involvement of ShinyHunters, a known group associated with data theft and extortion, reinforces the threat landscape targeting educational institutions. The ongoing proceedings indicate that Instructure must prepare to address questions regarding the technical details of the breach, the effectiveness of its security controls, and the measures taken to mitigate the impact of the data exposure on affected students and institutions.
