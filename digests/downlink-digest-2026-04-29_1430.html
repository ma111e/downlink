Categorizing the OAuth Sprawl

  • Shadow apps refer to unapproved business applications signed up with either corporate or personal accounts.

  • Shadow extensions encompass browser add-ons that provide visibility into browser activity, ranging from untrustworthy to outright malicious.
  • Shadow integrations involve unapproved OAuth connections between applications, even when the base app itself has received prior approval. All four categories significantly expand the organization’s attack surface and require distinct mitigation strategies.

Introduction and Core Problem

Security teams frequently close hundreds of vulnerabilities each quarter, resulting in dashboards filled with green metrics. However, leadership often questions whether these remediation efforts actually translate to improved safety. Traditional patch counts and CVSS scores cannot provide the necessary context to answer this question. Exposure management was created specifically to bridge the gap between remediation activities and measurable risk reduction. Despite this clear need, the market has responded with a flood of platforms that claim to deliver true exposure management.

Four Architectural Approaches

Most exposure management platforms fall into one of four categories, each defined by how the vendor processes and correlates data. Stitched portfolio platforms are the result of acquisitions, bundling point solutions like cloud security and identity analytics under a single brand. While these vendors unify findings in a shared console, each module retains its own data model and operates independently. Data aggregation platforms ingest findings from existing scanners and third-party tools to normalize data in a unified interface. These systems are strictly limited to the data they receive, meaning they cannot correlate disconnected findings or model how one exposure enables another. Single-domain specialist platforms focus deeply on one area, such as cloud misconfigurations or network vulnerabilities. While they deliver strong results within their specific domain, they cannot model how exposures chain across different environments. Integrated platforms are built from scratch to discover and correlate multiple exposure types within a single engine. These systems construct a digital twin of the environment and map how attackers move laterally across on-premises, cloud, and hybrid boundaries.

Five Evaluation Criteria

The architecture behind each approach dictates what security teams can actually see, validate, and act upon. Evaluating these platforms requires asking five specific questions about their capabilities:

  • Exposure Coverage and Depth: Teams must verify how many exposure types the platform discovers and whether it analyzes them natively. CVEs account for roughly twenty-five percent of exploited exposures, while misconfigurations, cached credentials, and identity weaknesses make up the remainder. Native discovery is essential to control every layer of information rather than relying on third-party metadata.
  • Cross-Environment Attack Path Mapping: The platform must map attack paths across environment boundaries instead of relying on simple network topology. Stitched products often derive paths from connectivity alone, while aggregators produce no paths at all. The real test is whether the system can trace lateral movement across hybrid environments where attackers bypass cloud-native defenses.
  • Exploitability Validation: The platform must validate exploitability by testing multiple conditions like open ports and running processes. True validation requires checking if vulnerable libraries are loaded by active processes or if ports are reachable. The system should deliver binary answers grounded in the actual environment rather than general assumptions.
  • Security Control Integration: The platform must factor in existing security controls such as firewalls, MFA, and EDR. A high-severity vulnerability blocked by a firewall cannot be used for lateral movement, while a lower-severity identity exposure with a direct path to a domain controller represents an emergency. Ignoring controls leads to prioritizing findings that carry no real risk.
  • Asset-Focused Prioritization: Prioritization must work backward from critical business assets to identify choke points where a single fix eliminates multiple attack paths. Score-based or asset-tag rankings ignore the blast radius of an exposure or the actual business needs. This approach narrows the priority list to approximately two percent of all exposures.

Operational Impact and Conclusion

The choice of platform architecture directly determines how secure an environment becomes and how teams spend their time. Stitched and aggregated platforms force teams to reconcile findings across multiple tools and chase exposures that lead to dead ends. Single-domain platforms provide depth in one area but leave blind spots across the rest of the attack surface. An integrated approach eliminates this overhead by correlating exposures into validated attack paths and factoring in deployed controls. When a remediation closes a choke point, continuous exposure management platforms update the risk graph in real time. This ensures that exposures previously deemed urgent now lead nowhere, keeping the priority queue aligned with current risk. When a platform validates exploitability, models security controls, and maps every viable path to critical assets, security leaders can confidently answer whether their environment is actually safer.
