    Vulnerabilities, Advisories, and Patching

    Cisco Unified Communications Manager CVE-2026-20230

    Cisco released fixes for a high-severity flaw in Unified Communications Manager, tracked as CVE-2026-20230 with a CVSS score of 8.6. The issue is an unauthenticated server-side request forgery risk caused by improper input validation for specific HTTP requests.

    Cisco said an attacker could exploit the flaw by sending a crafted HTTP request to an affected device. A successful exploit could allow file writes to the underlying operating system that could later be used to elevate privileges to root. The issue was addressed in Cisco Unified CM and Unified CM SME Release versions 14SU6 and 15SU5.

    Cisco said proof-of-concept exploit code is available, but it had no evidence of active exploitation. The vulnerability was reported by an independent security researcher working with SSD Secure Disclosure.

    Linux Kernel CVE-2022-0492

    CISA added Linux Kernel CVE-2022-0492 to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog. The flaw has a CVSS score of 7.8 and involves improper authentication that could allow privilege escalation through the cgroups v1 release_agent feature.

    Federal Civilian Executive Branch agencies were required to remediate the flaw by June 5, 2026. The article notes that Kaspersky observed exploitation of CVE-2022-0492, along with CVE-2019-5736 and CVE-2024-21626, in attacks targeting container environments.

    Automatic Tank Gauge Systems

    U.S. government agencies issued an advisory about attacks targeting U.S.-based automatic tank gauge systems. The advisory urged organizations to secure these systems with strong passwords and remove them from the internet to reduce public exposure.

    The activity remains unattributed. The attackers reportedly compromise internet-exposed ATG systems using hard-coded credentials, command execution, and SQL injection vectors, then escalate privileges to gain full administrator rights and modify system functions.

    The advisory warned that a compromised ATG system could allow a cyber threat actor to disrupt or manipulate critical tank management functions as though they had legitimate physical console access.

    Malware Delivery, Social Engineering, and Backdoors

    VIP Keylogger Campaigns

    Splunk analyzed campaigns distributing VIP Keylogger through loaders written in JavaScript, batch scripts, and Visual Basic Script. The campaigns rely on social engineering and masquerade as legitimate business communications.

    The lures include bank payment notifications, procurement orders, and logistics updates. The goal is to persuade users to open malicious files.

    DriveSurge, ClickFix, FakeUpdates, and zTDS

    Silent Push reported that a threat cluster tracked as DriveSurge has been operating large-scale malware distribution campaigns. The campaigns use ClickFix and FakeUpdates, also known as SocGholish, social engineering techniques on compromised websites.

    Thousands of websites are estimated to have been compromised. DriveSurge primarily acts as an initial access broker operating on a pay-per-install model, enabling follow-on attacks.

    Visitors to compromised websites are routed through a traffic distribution system known as zTDS. The system profiles visitors and decides whether to serve a ClickFix lure or a FakeUpdates lure. The article states that zTDS has been in use since at least 2015 and is publicly available at ztds[.]info.

    The DriveSurge campaign has been active since September 2025.

    JavaScript-Coded Backdoor Malspam

    Intrinsec disclosed that multiple malspam campaigns have distributed a JavaScript-coded backdoor. The targets were from all regions and sectors, with the article specifically noting energy and finance ministries, including in the CIS region.

    Intrinsec assessed the campaigns as financially motivated and operated for email account compromise and/or business email compromise. The activity was observed in March 2026.

    ClearFake and EtherHiding

    Trend Micro described an intrusion in which threat actors used EtherHiding to route ClearFake payload delivery through smart contracts on the BNB Smart Chain testnet. The attack chain ended with deployment of SectopRAT and ACRStealer.

    The intrusion also included an on-chain execution tracker that confirmed each victim compromise in real time.

    BackgroundFix and Fake Image Tools

    Huntress described a ClickFix-style lure dressed up as free image-editing tools. The sites imitate “remove your photo background” services with uploads, progress bars, and download buttons, but the entire interface is fake.

    The activity, named BackgroundFix, delivers CastleLoader. CastleLoader then drops NetSupport RAT and a custom .NET stealer called CastleStealer. CastleLoader is attributed to a threat cluster known as GrayBravo.

    Abuse of Trusted Tools and Platforms

    Tiflux and Commercial RMM Abuse

    Huntress reported growing malicious use of a lesser-known remote desktop tool called Tiflux. Attackers are using Tiflux to establish persistence, transmit screenshots, and run commands for system profiling.

    The same incidents also involved UltraVNC, Splashtop, ScreenConnect, and an outdated driver that could permit privilege escalation. Huntress said threat actors continue to test and weaponize commercial remote access management tools.

    FalkonC2 and Rotemelli2

    Flare.io disclosed details about FalkonC2, a commercial hacking tool designed to hide inside enterprise environments by abusing trusted remote access software. Its enterprise version, Rotemelli2, runs in memory, rotates command-and-control domains every 72 hours, and uses ScreenConnect, Datto, and SimpleHelp to quietly launch attacks.

    Dashboard telemetry suggested active enterprise infections in the United States, Australia, the Netherlands, and Poland. The framework also checks infected machines for QuickBooks and Sage50 data, suggesting interest in accounting systems that can be exfiltrated quickly.

    Steam Community Profiles as Dead Drop Resolvers

    GoDaddy identified malware using Steam Community profile comments to host malicious payloads for WordPress. The malware uses invisible Unicode characters to conceal payloads inside Steam profile comments.

    The malware has two primary functions. Client-side JavaScript injection fetches encoded URLs from Steam profile comments, decodes them, and injects external JavaScript into WordPress pages. A server-side backdoor provides cookie-authenticated remote access for modifying PHP files across plugins and themes.

    The campaign was first detected in July 2025 and has been found on about 1,980 WordPress sites. The article says it is unclear how the sites are breached, but possible initial infection vectors include stolen admin logins, compromised FTP or SFTP credentials, exploitation of a vulnerable WordPress theme or plugin, or a supply chain compromise.

    Adobe Target Abuse in LinkedIn Phishing

    Malwarebytes described a LinkedIn phishing campaign that weaponizes Adobe infrastructure. Victims receive fake LinkedIn emails with an HTML attachment that serves a login form and asks for credentials.

    Captured information is delivered to the domain lnkd.tt.omtrdc[.]net/rest/v1/delivery, after which victims are redirected to the legitimate LinkedIn site. Malwarebytes said the domain belongs to Adobe and is associated with Adobe Target, but that attackers are abusing Adobe Target as a redirect or abuse point rather than using it to receive credentials.

    Cloud and Nation-State Tradecraft

    ROADtools Abuse by APT29, APT33, and UTA0355

    Palo Alto Networks Unit 42 reported that nation-state groups such as APT29, APT33, and UTA0355 are exploiting ROADtools. ROADtools is a Python-based open-source framework used for red-teaming and research.

    The article states that ROADtools operates through legitimate Microsoft APIs and can mimic typical traffic. Attackers can further evade detection by configuring request attributes such as user-agent strings.

    Unit 42 said nation-state actors have used ROADtools in recent cloud intrusions for discovery, persistence, and defense evasion. Attackers involved in a targeted phishing campaign in early 2025 used tooling matching ROADtools’ token management capabilities.

    Iran-Aligned Activity Against Israel

    ESET reported an unusual spike in Iran-aligned activity against Israeli targets between October 2025 and March 2026. The activity could not be linked to previously known groups.

    Two unattributed clusters, Rusty Boots and MoKhargosh, showed espionage capabilities and destructive potential. Their activity included deployment of a bootkit-style wiper and retention of destructive tooling for later use.

    MoKhargosh, first observed in January 2026, used Go-compiled binaries against Israel. Its tools included a backdoor called GoKhargosh, wipers, filecoders that overwrite files with junk data, and a wiper targeting the master boot record to make systems unbootable.

    A third cluster, MOØN Badr, appeared limited to targeted espionage. It targeted three unidentified victims in Israel in early January 2026 and delivered the MOØN AGENT backdoor through phishing emails for command execution and file upload and download.

    Mobile Spyware Operation Claimed by Russia’s FSB

    Russia’s Federal Security Service disclosed what it described as a large-scale action by foreign intelligence services to stealthily implant spyware on mobile devices of high-ranking Russian officials. The spyware was reportedly used to exfiltrate existing data, intercept ongoing conversations, and conduct covert audio and video surveillance of device surroundings.

    Russia did not identify the actor behind the attacks. The FSB said representatives of foreign intelligence services used technical capabilities of major international IT corporations to exfiltrate sensitive data, including through mobile communication channels. An investigation is ongoing, and the FSB initiated a criminal case.

    Cybercrime Ecosystem and Law Enforcement Fallout

    XSS Forum Fragmentation

    Flashpoint reported that the July 2025 law enforcement takedown of the XSS cybercrime forum did not dismantle the Russian-speaking cybercrime ecosystem. Instead, it fractured the ecosystem into competing and harder-to-track factions.

    The collapse of XSS triggered an exodus into new, unvetted, and often adversarial communities. Forums that appeared after the takedown include DamageLib, Rehub, XSS.pro, and XSSF.

    DamageLib was launched by legacy moderators of XSS, while Rehub was launched by another former XSS moderator. XSS.pro used old backups and is suspected of being a law enforcement honeypot, and XSSF was started by a pro-Russian Telegram hacking group.

    Spain Arrests Suspect for Sensitive Data Leak

    Spanish National Police arrested an unidentified individual for leaking sensitive information. The leaked information related to members of critical state organizations.

    Affected organizations included Spain’s National Cybersecurity Institute, the State Attorney General’s Office, the National Police, the Civil Guard, and the National Security Council.

    Crypto Sanctions

    The U.S. Treasury’s Office of Foreign Assets Control announced sanctions against Nobitex, Iran’s largest cryptocurrency exchange, for facilitating payments related to terrorist activities. The Treasury said Nobitex processed more than 50 percent of all Iranian digital asset inflows in 2025.

    The Treasury also said Nobitex facilitated payments tied to Iranian terrorist activities, sanctions evasion, and Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps-linked transactions, including activity associated with IRGC-affiliated ransomware actors. Sanctions also applied to Nobitex chairman, co-founder, and former CEO Amir Hossein Rad, other Nobitex leaders and officials, and three other exchanges: Wallex, Bitpin, and Ramzinex.

    Chainalysis also reported that Nobitex processed over 50 percent of Iranian digital asset inflows last year. TRM Labs said the four exchanges accounted for about \(7.7 billion, or 78 percent, of Iran’s \)9.9 billion in attributed 2025 crypto volume.

    Data Extortion Trends

    Palo Alto Networks Unit 42 reported that pure data-exfiltration campaigns without ransomware deployment are increasing. In 2025, these campaigns primarily targeted professional services, healthcare, and consumer services firms.

    Unit 42 noted that while manufacturing remained the single most disrupted sector overall, construction saw a 44 percent year-over-year increase as a data-only extortion hotspot. The article says construction firms are attractive because of financial blueprints, bidding data, and data egress controls.

    AI as Offensive Aid and Enterprise Risk

    AI-Assisted EDR Evasion Testing

    Sophos observed an unknown threat actor using AI technologies to automate Active Directory discovery and refine endpoint detection and response evasion tactics. The activity occurred in a red team post-exploitation framework.

    Sophos said AI use for malware development was more limited and mainly supported workflow coordination and experimentation. The actual EDR-bypass path involved a structured engineering test cycle with human review and iteration.

    The attacker used Cursor and Anthropic Claude Opus. The framework centered on a Python tool that generated Go and Rust payloads for testing against sandboxing, antivirus, and EDR detection. The approach produced nearly 80 modules covering more than 70 techniques.

    Sophos also attributed to the threat actor Python-based malware development scripts for injecting shellcode into legitimate Windows executables and a Telegram bot API-based external command-and-control mechanism. Sophos said AI agents lower the barrier to entry for sophisticated red team-style attacks but do not change how defenders should protect themselves.

    Anthropic Project Glasswing and AI-Found Vulnerabilities

    Anthropic expanded access to Project Glasswing by adding about 150 organizations in 15 countries for access to Claude Mythos Preview. Anthropic said the cybersecurity bottleneck is now verifying, disclosing, and patching the large number of vulnerabilities that Mythos-class models can surface.

    The article also cites a report from the Cloud Security Alliance, SANS Institute, and OWASP. That report concluded that organizations are likely to be overwhelmed in the near term by threat actors using AI to find and exploit vulnerabilities faster than defenders can patch them.

    The report said the cost and capability floor for exploit discovery is dropping, the time between disclosure and weaponization is compressing toward zero, and capabilities that once required nation-state resources are becoming broadly accessible.

    Enterprise AI Agent Failures

    Cyera analyzed 7,200 publicly reported AI-security and operational incidents. It identified 344 verified enterprise-relevant agent-inflicted damage cases between September 2023 and May 2026.

    Of those, 188 incidents involved autonomous AI systems causing direct organizational harm without any external attacker involvement. The majority of confirmed incidents involved real production impact rather than theoretical AI risk scenarios.

    Observed outcomes included deleted databases, destructive cloud actions, unauthorized financial operations, runaway API spending, service outages, exposed secrets, and silent integrity corruption inside enterprise environments. Cyera said that as agents gain broader permissions and deeper integrations into SaaS, cloud, development, and business environments, the AI interaction layer becomes part of the enterprise attack surface and critical data perimeter.

    Defensive Product and Ecosystem Responses

    Google Device Bound Session Credentials

    Google announced that Device Bound Session Credentials in Chrome are generally available and enabled by default for Google Workspace users. DBSC binds session cookies to the device a user authenticated from.

    Google said DBSC reduces the risk of session theft even if malware is present on the user’s device. The feature makes it more difficult for malicious actors to exploit stolen session cookies.

    Google Android Fake Call Detection

    Google announced a fake call detection feature for Android devices running Android 12 and later. The feature is built on Rich Communication Services and verifies whether a call is coming from the caller’s actual Android smartphone.

    The feature is enabled by default and is designed to help prevent deepfake impersonation and call spoofing in real time. When both parties use Phone by Google, the caller’s device sends a silent confirmation signal to verify the call.

    If a scammer tries to impersonate a contact and the confirmation signal is missing, the recipient’s device checks with the contact’s real device. If the real device says it is not making a call, the recipient receives a warning advising them to hang up immediately. The feature requires Phone by Google, Contacts, and Google Messages, and is rolling out globally starting with Pixel devices.

    RubyGems Cooldown Filter

    RubyGems added a cooldown time-based filter to Bundler version 4.0.13. The filter refuses to resolve to a gem version until it has been public for at least a configured number of days.

    The purpose is to skip releases that are too new to have been scrutinized in favor of versions that have aged past the cooldown window. The feature is opt-in and complements existing defenses such as mandatory two-factor authentication and trusted publishing.

    RubyGems users can declare a small cooldown on the source in the Gemfile. The article also notes related efforts such as AI-assisted vulnerability scanning against critical gems in the registry.

    Overall Takeaway

    The bulletin’s main lesson is that many effective attacks still rely on familiar weaknesses: unpatched systems, exposed admin interfaces, trusted tools used in unexpected ways, compromised legitimate websites, social engineering, and insufficient monitoring. It emphasizes that defenders should patch faster, reduce exposed management interfaces, avoid trusting tools solely by name, and watch for abnormal behavior at the edges where attackers hide.

    The article closes by stating that security is not magic. It describes effective defense as inventory, logs, least privilege, backups, tested restores, and people who notice when something normal begins acting wrong.

    • The July 2025 takedown of the XSS cybercrime forum did not eliminate the ecosystem; it fragmented activity into multiple newer forums, making the underground harder to track.

    • Some malware delivery chains now combine multiple resilience layers, such as DriveSurge using compromised reputable websites, zTDS profiling, and either ClickFix or FakeUpdates lures to select payload delivery paths.
    • AI-assisted offensive activity in the article was not primarily autonomous malware creation; it was used to speed workflow coordination, Active Directory discovery, and iterative EDR evasion testing with human review.
    • Enterprise AI agents are already causing production harm without external attackers, including deleted databases, destructive cloud actions, unauthorized financial operations, runaway API spending, and exposed secrets.

Technical Details

The arbitrary file write is the first step in the attack path. Cisco says the written files can later be used to escalate privileges to root. Root is described in the article as the top privilege on the system.

Severity and Scoring

The article highlights a difference between the CVSS score and Cisco’s severity rating. CVE-2026-20230 has a CVSS base score of 8.6. That score reflects the file-write condition as an integrity-only impact, without confidentiality or availability loss.

Cisco rated the advisory Critical anyway. The article explains that this is because the end state can be full root access. In other words, the CVSS score covers the file-write impact but not the later root escalation described by Cisco.

Exposure Conditions

EXEC Executive Overview

This 24-hour digest is dominated by supply-chain compromise, exposed payment and personal data, public exploit availability, and the continued operationalization of AI across both attack and defense. Several reports also show attackers abusing trusted platforms—Stripe, Google Tag Manager, npm, GitHub Actions, job sites, and browser update channels—to blend malicious activity into normal workflows.

01 Supply-Chain Compromise and Trusted Platform Abuse

Hola confirmed a supply-chain compromise affecting the Windows version of Hola Browser, where some installations received an undeclared executable, me.exe, assessed by researchers as a Monero miner. The malware added a Windows Defender exclusion, copied itself as HolaMonitorService.exe, created the hola_monitor_svc auto-start service, and ran during idle periods. Hola said roughly 0.1% of users were affected and reported no evidence of user data theft.

The npm ecosystem saw another serious software supply-chain incident with IronWorm, a Rust-based infostealer found in 36 packages. JFrog reported that IronWorm targeted 86 environment variables and 20 credential files, used Tor communications, hid behind an eBPF kernel rootkit, and attempted self-propagation using stolen npm publishing credentials. The attack reportedly began from a compromised npm account named asteroiddao and was detected before it spread into more popular packages.

Sansec reported a Magecart campaign targeting Magento and Adobe Commerce checkout pages by abusing Google Tag Manager and Stripe infrastructure. The skimmer retrieved JavaScript from Stripe customer metadata, executed it dynamically, captured payment and billing details, and later exfiltrated stolen data by creating fake Stripe customer records. A related variant used a Google Firestore document named tracking/captcha in a project called braintree-payment-app.

02 Vulnerabilities, Exploit Disclosure, and Exposure Gaps

Cisco patched CVE-2026-20230 in Unified Communications Manager and Unified CM Session Management Edition after public proof-of-concept exploit code became available. The vulnerability is an unauthenticated SSRF that can allow arbitrary file writes to the underlying operating system, with potential follow-on root escalation. Cisco noted the flaw is exploitable only when WebDialer is running, and WebDialer is disabled by default.

A flaw in Anthropic’s Claude Code GitHub Action allowed a malicious GitHub issue to hijack vulnerable public repositories running the action. The issue stemmed from trusting actors whose names ended in [bot], combined with indirect prompt injection that caused Claude to expose environment variables, including credentials used to request OIDC tokens. Anthropic fixed the core issue after a January report and included hardened behavior in claude-code-action v1.0.94.

Separate reporting highlighted growing friction around vulnerability disclosure after researcher Ammar Askar published a working exploit for a VS Code issue that could steal a victim’s GitHub token via a single link click. BleepingComputer also covered a threat actor tutorial explaining how attackers search for newly disclosed vulnerabilities, validate exposed systems, and choose between reporting, selling, or exploiting findings.

03 Data Breaches and Privacy Enforcement

The UN World Food Programme disclosed a breach of its Palestine self-registration application used for food and cash assistance in Gaza. The exposed data included names, ID numbers, phone numbers, and location details such as neighborhood information, affecting roughly 600,000 Palestinian households according to statements cited by The New Humanitarian. WFP temporarily suspended the platform while investigating and strengthening controls.

DentaQuest confirmed unauthorized access to a limited portion of its network after ShinyHunters claimed to have stolen more than 234 GB of data and later leaked it. Have I Been Pwned analyzed the leak and found records for 2.6 million accounts, including names, email addresses, phone numbers, government-issued IDs, health insurance information, genders, and dates of birth.

In the regulatory sphere, the U.S. Supreme Court upheld FCC fines against telecom carriers for sharing access to consumers’ location data without consent. Separately, the FTC opened public comment on whether to modify or set aside a 2022 order and $150 million penalty against Twitter, now X, over the use of account security data for targeted advertising.

04 Espionage, Cybercrime, and Geopolitical Activity

The Five Eyes intelligence partnership warned that Chinese military intelligence services are using online job platforms and front-company recruiter personas to identify people with access to sensitive or classified information. The bulletin said candidates may be asked about government contacts, military roles, or unit activities and later paid to produce reports on strategic topics.

Proofpoint reported that Chinese-speaking threat actor TA4922 expanded phishing activity into the U.K., Germany, Italy, and South Africa. The group uses HR, tax, invoice, benefits, and compliance lures for credential phishing, fraud, and malware delivery, including ValleyRAT, Atlas RAT, RomulusLoader, and SilentRunLoader. Proofpoint assesses the actor as likely financially motivated.

Russia’s Supreme Court is set to consider designating Belarusian Cyber Partisans and Silent Crow as extremist organizations. Both groups have claimed disruptive cyber operations against Russian and Belarusian government and critical infrastructure targets, including a July 2025 Aeroflot incident that reportedly disrupted more than 100 flights.

05 AI in Security Operations and Federal Cyber Policy

AI remained a recurring theme across defensive operations and policy. CISA Acting Director Nick Andersen said the agency plans to release a binding operational directive tied to the administration’s AI executive order, with emphasis on vulnerability management and model vetting through a cyber clearinghouse. The administration is also reportedly close to nominating a Senate-confirmed CISA director, though reporting that Palantir CTO Shyam Sankar was under consideration was disputed by a White House official.

Cisco Talos described expansion of its threat hunting program across endpoint, network, firewall, and identity telemetry, combining AI-driven analysis with human validation. Tenable also announced participation in Anthropic’s Project Glasswing to evaluate Claude Mythos Preview for exposure management, attack path analysis, remediation decision-making, and AI governance.
