In plain words

The article describes a serious flaw in Apache Tomcat that can let someone get into certain protected web applications without the right password. It affects Tomcat systems that use an outside company directory, such as LDAP or Active Directory, for sign-in in a specific setup. This matters because older internal business applications are often forgotten but may still be reachable from outside, and a successful bypass could expose whatever private data or functions those applications protect.

Summary
CVE-2026-55957 is a critical Apache Tomcat authentication bypass affecting JNDIRealm configurations that use GSSAPI LDAP binds, allowing pre-authentication access without the correct password. Organizations should upgrade to Tomcat 11.0.5, 10.1.37, or 9.0.101 and urgently identify any exposed directory-integrated Tomcat instances.

CVE-2026-55957 is a critical Apache Tomcat authentication bypass affecting JNDIRealm when it is configured to authenticate LDAP binds using GSSAPI. The vulnerability has a CVSS v3.1 base score of 9.8, while Apache classifies it as “Important” because exploitation depends on a specific deployment configuration. Exploitation is pre-authentication and can allow an attacker to authenticate without the correct password.

The affected condition applies to Tomcat instances that delegate authentication to an LDAP or Active Directory server through JNDIRealm with GSSAPI selected as the bind mechanism. A successful bypass grants access according to the directory-backed identity used inside the application, exposing resources protected by that authentication boundary. Affected versions include Tomcat 11.0.0-M1 through 11.0.4, 10.1.0-M1 through 10.1.36, 9.0.0.M1 through 9.0.100, 8.5.0 through 8.5.100, and 7.0.0 through 7.0.109.

Apache has released fixed versions for supported branches: Tomcat 11.0.5, 10.1.37, and 9.0.101. The article says Apache lists no workaround other than upgrading, and organizations on end-of-life 8.5.x or 7.0.x branches should treat migration to a supported branch as part of remediation. Recommended actions include inventorying affected JNDIRealm and GSSAPI configurations, restricting network access, monitoring directory server logs, auditing recent access, disabling GSSAPI-authenticated binds where immediate upgrade is not feasible, and confirming fixes in third-party platforms that bundle Tomcat.

  • Apache rates the issue as “Important” despite its 9.8 critical CVSS score because exploitation depends on a specific JNDIRealm configuration using GSSAPI-authenticated binds.
  • The article highlights Consumer Discretionary organizations as the largest observed exposure group at 57.1%, linking this to distributed franchise, subsidiary, and e-commerce environments with historically separate directory integrations.
  • The risk is amplified because directory-integrated Tomcat instances were often built for internal use but may later become reachable through VPN gateways, reverse proxies, or forgotten firewall rules.
  • Apache lists no workaround other than upgrading, so organizations running end-of-life Tomcat 8.5.x or 7.0.x are expected to migrate to a supported branch as part of remediation.

  • 03Affected releases include Apache Tomcat 11.0.0-M1 through 11.0.4, 10.1.0-M1 through 10.1.36, 9.0.0.M1 through 9.0.100, 8.5.0 through 8.5.100, and 7.0.0 through 7.0.109.

In plain words

Adobe released fixes for seven very serious security problems in its ColdFusion web app platform and Campaign Classic marketing platform. Adobe says it has not seen these specific issues being used in real attacks, but it rated the updates as urgent because similar Adobe flaws have often been targeted, including some used by ransomware gangs.

Summary
Adobe released urgent patches for seven maximum-severity flaws in ColdFusion and Campaign Classic that could allow remote or arbitrary code execution, urging administrators to update quickly despite no known active exploitation. The affected products include ColdFusion 2025.9, 2023.20 and earlier, and on-premises Campaign Classic 7.4.3 build 9396 and earlier.

Adobe released security patches for seven maximum-severity vulnerabilities in ColdFusion and Campaign Classic. The flaws can be exploited in low-complexity attacks that do not require user interaction and were assigned Adobe priority 1, indicating a high risk of being targeted. Adobe recommends administrators install the updates as soon as possible, such as within 72 hours, while also saying it is not aware of any exploits in the wild for the issues addressed.

Six critical ColdFusion flaws affect versions 2025.9, 2023.20, and earlier, and can allow attackers without privileges to gain remote code execution on unpatched systems. The Campaign Classic vulnerability, tracked as CVE-2026-48286, affects versions 7.4.3 build 9396 and earlier and could lead to arbitrary code execution in the current user’s context. Adobe said CVE-2026-48286 affects on-premises Adobe Campaign instances, including fully on-premises deployments and on-premises components in hybrid deployments, while Adobe-hosted instances have already been patched.

Adobe Chief Security Officer Aanchal Gupta also said the company will move from monthly to twice-monthly security bulletins starting July 14, 2026, with advisories published on the second and fourth Tuesday of each month. Adobe said its out-of-band response process will remain in effect for actively exploited vulnerabilities or externally discovered zero-day vulnerabilities. The article also notes that CISA has added 79 Adobe product flaws to its catalog of actively exploited vulnerabilities over the last five years, including 10 that were also abused by ransomware gangs.

In plain words

The article says AI tools sometimes invent web addresses, and criminals are starting to buy those made-up addresses and turn them into fake sites that steal information or push harmful apps. This affects people who trust links given by AI assistants, including users looking for banks, postal services, shopping sites, or betting sites. It matters because the fake sites can look real and may not be blocked right away, so people can be sent into danger by a tool they expected to help them.

Summary
Attackers are registering domains that AI models hallucinate and using them for phishing and malware, exploiting users’ trust in AI-generated links before reputation systems can flag the sites. Defenders can reduce risk by monitoring likely hallucinated domains and treating all AI-provided links as unverified until confirmed.

Palo Alto Networks’ Unit 42 says attackers are using a tactic it calls phantom squatting, where they register web domains that large language models invent and then host phishing pages or malware on them. The issue matters because people and AI assistants may treat model-provided links as trustworthy, even when the domain did not exist before. Whoever registers an invented domain first can benefit from that misplaced trust without needing a phishing email or malicious ad.

Unit 42 tested two AI models with 685,339 questions about 913 well-known brands and received 2.1 million links. Threat intelligence had already flagged 13,229 of those links as malicious, and roughly 250,000 invented domains had no owner. The article says newly registered phantom domains are hard for reputation systems to catch at first because they have no prior history of bad behavior.

The article describes two observed cases involving hallucinated postal-service domains that Unit 42 flagged before attackers registered them. In one case, an attacker registered a predicted domain 23 days later and used the Montana Empire phishing kit to steal card numbers, bank-transfer details, and national ID data; in another, an attacker registered a domain 51 days after it was flagged and used it to push a malicious Android app. The article advises people not to trust links just because AI provided them, to verify official domains, and to prevent AI agents from automatically opening or downloading from model-generated links without checks.

  • The article highlights that different AI models can invent the same fake domain for the same question, making future abuse predictable enough for defenders to monitor likely hallucinated domains before attackers register them.
  • Newly registered hallucinated domains are especially dangerous because they start with no reputation history, leaving blocklists and threat feeds with little to flag until after victims may already have been sent there.
  • The observed cases show that both attackers and defenders can reach the same fake domains by asking AI systems, turning model behavior itself into a way to identify likely phishing infrastructure in advance.
  • The risk increases when AI agents automatically open or download from model-generated links, because an agent may act on an invented address without the hesitation or verification a person might apply.
Key Points
  • 01Palo Alto Networks' Unit 42 calls the practice of registering AI-hallucinated web domains for phishing and malware "phantom squatting" and says it is already occurring in the wild.
  • 02Unit 42 asked two AI models 685,339 questions about 913 well-known brands, receiving 2.1 million links, including 13,229 flagged as malicious and roughly 250,000 invented domains with no owner.
  • 03In one observed case, an attacker registered an AI-predicted fake domain resembling a national postal service's online marketplace 23 days after Unit 42 predicted it and used the Montana Empire phishing kit to steal card numbers, bank-transfer details, and national ID data.
  • 04In another observed case, Unit 42 flagged a hallucinated postal-service domain 51 days before an attacker registered it, cloned a brand site, added fake credibility claims, and used it to push a malicious Android app.
  • 05The article advises users not to trust links just because an AI provided them, to verify official domains before entering passwords, and to prevent AI agents from automatically opening or downloading from model-generated links without checks.

  • Click & Collect can become a security weakness because its frictionless design lets criminals buy goods online and collect them locally before the real account owner notices an order confirmation email.

  • The combination of account takeover and Buy Now Pay Later fraud can create exposure not only for consumers but also for retailers and credit providers, including potential regulatory and reputational consequences under UK Financial Conduct Authority rules.

In plain words

Amazon will pay a $2.25 million penalty after the U.S. Federal Trade Commission said it failed to give many identity theft victims records of fraudulent transactions made in their names. Some victims and authorized law enforcement agencies were denied the records or received them too late, even though the law requires companies to provide them within 30 days. This matters because people whose identities are stolen need those records to understand what happened and prove the fraud, and the order requires Amazon to handle these requests properly going forward.

Summary
Amazon will pay a $2.25 million FTC civil penalty for allegedly violating the Fair Credit Reporting Act by denying or delaying identity theft victims’ access to records of fraudulent transactions made in their names. The proposed order requires Amazon to provide lawful records requests to victims and authorized law enforcement within 30 days.

The U.S. Federal Trade Commission said Amazon will pay a $2.25 million civil penalty to settle charges that it blocked identity theft victims’ access to transaction records. The complaint, filed with the Justice Department, alleged that Amazon failed to provide many fraud victims with records of fraudulent transactions made in their names. The FTC said this was required under Section 609(e) of the Fair Credit Reporting Act.

According to the FTC, Amazon customer service agents denied many consumers’ requests for records for “privacy” or “security” reasons. The agency also said Amazon sometimes provided requested records after the 30-day timeframe required by the FCRA. The complaint further alleged that Amazon refused to provide application and business transaction records to authorized law enforcement agencies acting on behalf of identity theft victims.

Under the proposed order, Amazon must provide access to lawfully requested records to identity theft victims and law enforcement agents within 30 days. Amazon was also ordered to notify consumers who had requested records since April 2024 but had not received them that they may request additional records. The article notes that Kohl’s paid a $220,000 fine six years ago over similar allegations, and that Amazon previously agreed to other settlements involving Alexa children’s privacy and Prime membership practices.

  • The FTC’s allegations make clear that citing “privacy” or “security” was not an acceptable reason for Amazon to withhold fraudulent transaction records from identity theft victims covered by the FCRA.
  • The proposed order applies not only to direct consumer requests but also to authorized law enforcement requests made on behalf of identity theft victims.
  • Consumers who requested records from Amazon since April 2024 but did not receive them may be notified that they can request additional records under the settlement terms.
  • The article links Amazon’s case to a prior Kohl’s settlement, showing the FTC has previously penalized retailers for failing to provide identity theft victims with transaction records.
