Overview

NetSPI described an authorized social engineering assessment focused on a client’s executive leadership team. The client wanted to understand how vulnerable its senior leaders would be to targeted social engineering, and the scope was broad because high-level executives are difficult targets. NetSPI framed the purpose of the work as identifying systemic weaknesses in policy and training, not creating a “gotcha” moment.

The central pretext was journalist impersonation. The testers posed as a reporter seeking comment on an anonymous tip alleging that the company was improperly disposing of hazardous waste at an active construction site. The scenario was tied to the company’s own public announcement about a planned new facility.

Reconnaissance and Pretext Development

NetSPI began with open-source reconnaissance. The testers reviewed the client’s website, contact pages, leadership biographies, corporate blogs, social media, and news articles. This helped them build profiles of the executives they would contact and identify a topic already relevant to the organization.

A press release about the client’s plans to construct a new facility became the anchor for the scenario. NetSPI noted that a major office expansion would likely be known to, or directly involve, the leadership team. The team decided that a media inquiry about alleged environmental incidents at the construction site would create urgency while remaining connected to real company activity.

The article states that this pretext required careful handling. NetSPI said its policy is to avoid pretexts that could be illegal, unethical, or emotionally harmful. To reduce the risk of causing an impulsive reaction against a specific person, the testers avoided naming an individual or a specific incident and instead framed the allegation as a series of events over time.

Infrastructure and Technical Setup

After choosing the pretext, the testers built supporting infrastructure. They researched journalists and local news outlets using Google, Google Maps, and LinkedIn to identify a reporter to impersonate. They created a ProtonMail account in that journalist’s name, explaining that privacy-focused email providers are commonly associated with journalists and whistleblower communications.

They also registered a lookalike domain and deployed an Evilginx server. The article describes Evilginx as an adversary-in-the-middle framework that replicates a legitimate login page, captures usernames, passwords, and MFA tokens in real time, and harvests authenticated session cookies. In this engagement, the Evilginx server was pointed at the client’s real Microsoft login flow.

Initial Outreach

NetSPI manually composed emails to members of the C-Suite requesting comment on the alleged environmental incidents. The emails emphasized that the story would be written with or without the company’s response, but that a comment would allow the company to set the record straight. NetSPI said this was intended to create urgency while offering the recipient a reason to engage.

The initial messages did not include a link. The article states that sending a link immediately would have triggered alarms for a security-conscious executive. Instead, the testers asked recipients to reply if they were willing to comment, which implied that the sender was not a bot and encouraged the target to take an active step.

Executive Response and Escalation

Two C-Suite members had out-of-office replies active. NetSPI noted that this was not directly impactful, but it suggested that the leadership team might have shared duties or divided attention. Two other executives responded by redirecting the testers to a fifth individual referred to as Bob.

Bob was the executive overseeing the new construction project. He initially responded in an expected way by providing a Teams invite controlled by his organization. NetSPI’s goal, however, was to get Bob to visit the tester-controlled landing page rather than remain inside the client-controlled Teams meeting process.

The testers replied with a claimed error message and asked Bob to join a provided Teams meeting link, which was the phishing link. NetSPI said this approach avoided waiting until the scheduled meeting time, which could have given Bob more time to question the situation.

Unintended Vendor Exposure

Bob was concerned about the environmental allegations and motivated to protect the company’s reputation. In his rush to address the situation, he forwarded the phishing link to two separate contracting firms involved with the construction project. NetSPI described this as Bob unintentionally acting as a social engineer himself.

This action expanded the engagement beyond the intended C-Suite target set and reached trusted vendors. The contracting firms were outside the authorized scope of the assessment. NetSPI therefore escalated immediately to its point of contact and killed the phishing session to avoid capturing credentials from out-of-scope parties.

Impact and Response

The article does not describe a real-world breach or an unauthorized attack. It describes an authorized assessment in which the tester-controlled infrastructure was shut down when out-of-scope third parties became involved. The disclosed impact was the demonstration that an executive could be induced to interact with and forward a phishing link during a perceived reputational crisis.

NetSPI emphasized that Bob was not negligent. The article says he was doing his job and trying to manage a potential public relations crisis for the company. The issue identified was the absence of a clear policy for how executives should handle unsolicited media inquiries tied to sensitive operational topics.

Lessons for Users

NetSPI listed several practical user-level guardrails. Users should be suspicious of urgency because pressure to act quickly is a reliable social engineering tool. They should also scrutinize URLs for misspelled company names, excessive hyphens, and uncommon domain extensions.

The article specifically warns against external links that direct users to internal platforms such as a company Microsoft login page or internal portal. NetSPI states that legitimate external parties should not have that URL. It also recommends avoiding links from untrusted sources to continue conversations and instead keeping conversations on company infrastructure.

NetSPI further recommends verifying claimed journalists or public officials through independent channels. Recipients should look up the source independently and not use contact information provided in the original message.

Lessons for Organizations

NetSPI recommended organizational controls beyond individual vigilance. These include deploying and tuning email security tooling, including domain impersonation detection and clear external sender warnings. The article also recommends phishing simulations that go beyond classic password-reset scenarios and include reporter impersonation, vendor outreach, and executive spoofing.

The article highlights the value of a simple process for media inquiries. A policy such as forwarding unsolicited media inquiries to Communications could have materially changed the outcome of the assessment. NetSPI also recommended considering organizational login restrictions and conditional access policies that limit where credentials can be authenticated, making session cookie replay attacks harder to operationalize.

Core Takeaway

The assessment showed that a realistic, urgent, and organization-specific pretext can bypass the instincts of security-conscious executives, especially when it resembles a legitimate business responsibility. The most important weakness was not an individual mistake but a missing process for handling sensitive unsolicited inquiries. Clear internal protocols, especially for executives and media-related requests, can reduce the effectiveness of this type of social engineering.

  • A phishing approach that starts with a reply-based conversation rather than an immediate link can reduce suspicion among security-conscious executives and make automated detection harder.
  • A clear policy requiring employees to forward unsolicited media inquiries to Communications could have materially changed the outcome of the assessment.
  • An executive trying to protect the company during a perceived PR crisis can unintentionally expand a targeted social engineering attempt to trusted third parties by forwarding malicious links.
  • External links that direct users to internal platforms such as a company Microsoft login page are especially important red flags because legitimate outside parties should not normally possess or send those URLs.
  • Conditional access and organizational login restrictions can reduce the practical impact of adversary-in-the-middle phishing by making session cookie replay harder to operationalize.
